## Chapter 1: The Window

Dr. Non Arkaraprasertkul stood at the rain-streaked window of his Sathorn apartment and watched Bangkok bleed neon into the night. It was May 7, 2026. Four days since they found the body. Four days since the internet began its systematic amnesia. Four days since Dr. Non discovered that a man could die in the modern world and simply cease to exist.

He picked up his phone and opened WhatsApp.

The last message sat there like a wound that wouldn't close: *Rest in peace, Michael. 5/7/26, 6:36 PM.* He had sent it not knowing Michael Friedl was already dead. Already cold. Already being erased from every server, every archive, every memory.

Dr. Non poured whiskey into a glass that had seen too many nights like this. The glass was half-empty, like his evidence folder. Like his faith in institutions. Like his patience for people who called asking questions "disrespectful."

He was forty years old, Harvard BArch, MIT SM, Oxford MPhil MA PhD, Senior Expert at the Digital Economy Promotion Agency, known throughout the Thai government as "the one-person service." When three software houses said no to building a monitoring dashboard, he built it himself. When vendors quoted six months, he delivered in two weeks. He was the man who solved impossible problems.

But he couldn't solve this one.

Because this wasn't a coding problem. This was a human problem. And humans, Dr. Non had learned, were infinitely more complex than any algorithm. Humans lied. Humans forgot. Humans stood in circles around dead men and waited for someone else to ask the question.

Dr. Non set down his glass and opened his laptop. The screen cast his face in blue-white light, making his round glasses glow like twin moons. He pulled up the first file in his evidence folder: a photograph. November 2025. RIEDEL restaurant, Bangkok. Three people smiling into a phone. Dr. Non in the center, holding the camera. Michael Friedl on the left, Austrian Commercial Counsellor, warm smile, receding hairline, alive. A colleague on the right. Wine glasses. Warm light. Life.

He clicked to the next file. The same photograph, but different. This version had crosshairs. Metadata stamps. Barcodes along the edge. Classification markings. The words "SUBJECT: DR. NON ARKARA — PRIORITY RED — ACTIVE SURVEILLANCE" stamped across the bottom in red.

Dr. Non stared at the surveillance photo for a long time. The rain on the window matched the rhythm of his heart. Slow. Steady. Terrified.

Someone knocked on his door.

## Chapter 2: The Man Who Wasn't There

Michael Friedl was fifty-four years old when he died on Sunday, May 3, 2026, in Bangkok, Thailand. He was the Austrian Commercial Counsellor and Head of ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA Thailand, a position that meant he was the most senior Austrian trade diplomat in Southeast Asia. Before Bangkok, he had served in New York, Tehran, Washington D.C., Abu Dhabi, and Johannesburg. Before trade diplomacy, he had been a UN space law expert, an Austrian delegate to COPUOS, and the author of the COPUOS Briefing Book — the definitive UN publication on space resources governance.

By Monday, May 4, his LinkedIn profile had been deleted.

By Tuesday, May 5, his ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA bio page returned a 404 error.

By Wednesday, May 6, every trace of him on Facebook, Instagram, and the Wayback Machine had vanished.

The only remaining evidence that Michael Friedl had ever existed was:
- A single article on Krone.at, an Austrian tabloid
- His name in the October 2025 Thai Diplomatic List
- A bio page on the Secure World Foundation website that hadn't been updated yet
- UN COPUOS records that couldn't be unilaterally scrubbed
- And Dr. Non's WhatsApp history, which the Austrian Federal Economic Chamber couldn't touch

Dr. Non spent the morning of May 7 making calls. He called the Austrian Embassy in Bangkok. The receptionist transferred him three times before someone finally said, "We have no comment at this time." He called the German-Thai Chamber of Commerce, where Michael had been a board member. They said they were "deeply saddened" and would be posting a memorial. He called the Thai Ministry of Foreign Affairs. They confirmed Michael Friedl had been listed in the diplomatic registry but could not comment on his death.

No one could comment on his death.

Not the embassy. Not his employer. Not the police. Not the hospital. Not even his wife, who had posted a LinkedIn tribute that said "He left too soon" but didn't say how, or why, or what had happened in the final hours.

Dr. Non stared at his evidence wall. Corkboard. Red string. The photograph of three people at RIEDEL, now pinned next to a printout of a 404 error page and a screenshot of a WhatsApp message that would never receive a reply.

He wrote on a notecard: **"THE ERASURE BEGAN BEFORE THE DEATH."**

He pinned it to the board and stepped back.

The pattern was already forming.

## Chapter 3: The Ankle

The first clue came from the most unexpected source.

Dr. Non was three whiskeys deep and scrolling through Michael's old LinkedIn posts — the ones he'd cached on his own server before the scrub — when he found it. A post from October 2024, when Michael had first arrived in Bangkok. Michael was complaining about the heat. About jet lag. About the difficulty of adjusting to a new city after five years in New York.

And then, buried in the comments, a reply Michael had written to a colleague: *"The ankle is healing well, thank you for asking. The surgery in New York was successful, but the recovery is slower than I expected. Bangkok humidity doesn't help."*

Dr. Non read the comment three times. Ankle surgery. New York. October 2024. Six months before Michael died.

He opened a new tab and searched: "Michael Friedl ankle surgery New York." Nothing. The search returned results for a German footballer with the same name. He tried: "Michael Friedl surgery 2024." Nothing. He tried combinations in German. Nothing.

Whatever hospital had treated Michael, whatever surgeon had operated on his ankle, whatever records existed — they were either never digitized or had been scrubbed along with everything else.

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"ANKLE SURGERY. NEW YORK. OCT 2024. CHRONIC HEALTH ISSUE? PAIN MANAGEMENT? OPIOIDS?"**

He circled the last word three times.

The opioid theory was the first domino. If Michael had been in chronic pain after ankle surgery, he might have been prescribed painkillers. If he'd been on painkillers for six months, he might have developed a dependency. If he'd developed a dependency in a city like Bangkok, where pharmaceutical regulations were lax and access was easy, the spiral would be rapid and invisible.

But chronic pain and opioid dependency didn't explain the erasure. A diplomat dying of an overdose was embarrassing, yes. It might explain why the Austrian government wanted to manage the narrative. But it didn't explain the speed. The completeness. The cross-border coordination required to delete a man from the internet in 72 hours.

Dr. Non pinned the ankle surgery card to the board and drew a red line from it to another card: **"THE ERASURE."**

Two lines. Two data points. Not enough to see the pattern yet.

But enough to know there was a pattern.

## Chapter 5: The Insurance

The policy arrived in Dr. Non's inbox at 2:17 AM, forwarded from a contact at the Austrian Embassy who requested anonymity.

The subject line read: "FW: Diplomat International Coverage — M. Friedl."

Dr. Non opened the PDF and read it by the light of his laptop screen. It was a standard international diplomat insurance policy, the kind provided by the Austrian Federal Economic Chamber to all commercial counsellors posted abroad. The coverage was comprehensive: medical evacuation, accidental death, disability, repatriation of remains, family support.

And then Dr. Non found it. Page 7. Section 4.3. Exclusions.

**"Exclusion #1: Suicide, attempted suicide, or intentional self-inflicted injury — Not Covered."**

He read it again. Then a third time. The red text seemed to pulse on the screen.

If Michael Friedl had died by suicide, his wife Amel received nothing. No death benefit. No family support. No repatriation coverage. No financial safety net.

But if Michael Friedl had died by "sudden and unexpected" causes — the official phrase being used by everyone — the policy paid out in full. Medical evacuation. Family support. Repatriation. A comprehensive package that would allow Amel to return to Vienna, rebuild her life, and continue her coaching business without financial ruin.

Dr. Non leaned back in his chair and felt the whiskey burning in his chest.

The word everyone was using — "sudden and unexpected" — wasn't just a euphemism. It was a financial instrument. It was the difference between zero and full coverage. It was the lever that determined whether Amel Derragui inherited a life or a debt.

He wrote on a notecard: **"INSURANCE EXCLUDES SUICIDE. 'SUDDEN AND UNEXPECTED' = FULL PAYOUT. AMEL'S FINANCIAL MOTIVE TO CONTROL THE NARRATIVE."**

He pinned it to the board and drew a red line from the insurance card to the ankle surgery card. Then another line from the insurance card to the erasure card. Then a third line connecting all three.

The triangle was complete.

Three points. Three data points. The minimum required to define a shape.

And the shape was beginning to look like murder.

Not literal murder. Not yet. But murder by system. Murder by design. Murder by the slow accumulation of pressure until a man had only one exit that didn't destroy everyone he loved.

Dr. Non opened a new tab on his laptop and typed: **"Austrian diplomat salary commercial counsellor Bangkok."**

The results loaded in 0.34 seconds. And what Dr. Non saw next changed everything.

## Chapter 6: The Money

The Austrian Federal Economic Chamber published their salary guidelines in a PDF buried deep on their website. Dr. Non found it at 2:47 AM, on page 34 of a 56-page document titled "Personnel Guidelines for Foreign Trade Representatives 2025."

The numbers were devastating.

**Commercial Attaché (entry level):** €38,400/year  
**Commercial Counsellor (mid-level):** €42,299–€52,000/year  
**Head of ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA (senior):** €52,000–€63,000/year  
**Benefits:** Housing allowance (€800–€1,200/month depending on posting), health insurance, pension contribution

Dr. Non did the math. Even at the top of the scale — €63,000/year plus housing — Michael Friedl's total compensation was approximately €5,250 per month, or roughly $5,700 USD.

In Bangkok, $5,700/month was a comfortable salary for a single diplomat. But Michael wasn't single. He was married to Amel Derragui. And Amel Derragui had expensive tastes.

Dr. Non pulled up Amel's website. The Time Is Now coaching. $499 per 90-minute session. IMF Family Network speaking fees (undisclosed, but industry standard for that tier was $3,000–$5,000 per keynote). Forbes-featured podcast with 300+ episodes. Top 2% globally.

He scrolled through her Instagram — still active, still posting motivational quotes about "turning challenges into opportunities." The photos showed a lifestyle that $5,700/month couldn't possibly support: designer handbags (Louis Vuitton, visible in three photos), luxury travel (Business Class boarding pass visible in one story, destination: Dubai), fine dining (MICHELIN-starred restaurants in Vienna, Paris, Bangkok), and jewelry (a diamond ring that, even in the low-resolution Instagram crop, looked like it cost more than Michael's annual salary).

Dr. Non felt the familiar sensation of his stomach dropping. He'd felt it before — when he realized a system he'd trusted was designed to fail people. When he built the Middle East monitoring dashboard and discovered the data the government didn't want citizens to see. When his father died and nobody would tell him the truth about the last days.

He wrote on a notecard: **"SALARY VS LIFESTYLE GAP. MICHAEL COULD NOT AFFORD AMEL. THE PRESSURE WAS FINANCIAL, MARITAL, AND PSYCHOLOGICAL."**

He pinned it to the board and drew lines connecting it to the insurance card, the ankle surgery card, and the erasure card.

The shape was no longer a triangle. It was a web. And at the center of the web, Dr. Non was beginning to see something that terrified him.

It wasn't a spider. It was a mirror.

## Chapter 7: The Empire

Amel Derragui was born in India to Algerian diplomat parents. By age fourteen, she had lived in four countries, attended school in three different systems, and learned three languages. At fourteen in Uganda, she started her first business — a social initiative to unite her high school peers during Idi Amin's turbulent aftermath.

She got her business degree from the University of Lyon, France. She worked in advertising at Publicis Group. She told herself: *I will never move again. I will never be a diplomat like my parents. I will never give up my career for a man.*

Then she visited her parents in Iran. She was twenty-five. She met Michael Friedl. Six months later, engaged. One year later, married, career abandoned, moved to Tehran.

Dr. Non read her origin story on The Diplomat Magazine's website at 3:15 AM, whiskey number five burning a hole in his judgment. The article was titled "From Expat Wife to Global Entrepreneur: How Amel Derragui Built a Coaching Empire." It was a profile in success. A celebration of resilience. A testament to the power of rebranding.

But what Dr. Non saw was different.

He saw a woman who had built an empire on a single narrative: *I gave up everything for my diplomat husband, and I turned my sacrifice into a business.* The podcast (Tandem Nomads, 300+ episodes, top 2% globally). The coaching ($499/session). The speaking circuit (IMF, World Bank, Foreign Ministry of Belgium). The Forbes feature. The book deal that was "in development."

Every element of Amel's brand required Michael to exist. To be alive. To be the charming Austrian diplomat who had swept her off her feet and given her the platform to become a global thought leader.

Without Michael, the narrative collapsed. The brand became meaningless. The podcast episodes about "the diplomat's wife" became obsolete. The coaching sessions about "following your husband abroad without losing yourself" became irrelevant. The $499 sessions became worthless because the story that justified them was over.

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"AMEL'S ENTIRE BRAND DEPENDS ON MICHAEL BEING ALIVE. WITHOUT HIM, SHE IS NOT A 'DIPLOMAT'S WIFE' — SHE IS A WOMAN WHO GAVE UP HER CAREER FOR A DEAD MAN."**

He pinned it to the board and drew a thick red line from this card to the insurance card.

Two motives. Financial and narrative. Both required Michael's death to be "sudden and unexpected." Neither could survive a suicide ruling.

Dr. Non stared at the board and felt the whiskey doing what whiskey did — making connections his sober mind was too cautious to make. He was seeing patterns now. Webs within webs. Traps within traps.

## Chapter 9: The Bystander Effect

When Dr. Non's father died of lung cancer, Dr. Non told everyone. "My father passed away. Lung cancer. He smoked a lot when he was younger." It took five seconds. It opened the biggest wound a human being can carry — the death of a parent — and closed it just as fast. The listener understood. They empathized. They knew. The loop was closed.

When Michael Friedl died, nobody closed the loop.

Twenty-six comments on LinkedIn. Five hundred ten reactions. Not one person asked how. Not one person asked why. Not one person said: "Wait. A fifty-four-year-old diplomat died suddenly in Bangkok and there's no police report, no hospital statement, no cause of death? That doesn't make sense."

Instead, they performed grief. They recited the script. "My heartfelt condolences." "Rest in peace." "Our thoughts are with you." And then they scrolled on. They went back to work. They had lunch. They forgot.

Dr. Non understood the psychology. He had studied it at MIT. The bystander effect: when a group of people witnesses an emergency, each individual is less likely to intervene because they assume someone else will. The effect scales with group size. In a group of five hundred people performing identical grief on LinkedIn, the probability of any single person asking a real question approaches zero.

But this wasn't an accident on a street corner. This was the death of a human being. A friend. A colleague. A man with a wife and a career and a future. And five hundred people had decided — collectively, unconsciously, through the invisible machinery of social conformity — that asking what happened was "disrespectful."

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"THE BYSTANDER EFFECT IS NOT ACCIDENTAL. IT IS CULTURALLY ENFORCED. THE TABOO AGAINST ASKING IS STRONGER THAN THE TABOO AGAINST DYING."**

He pinned it to the board and stepped back.

The corkboard was nearly full now. Red string ran between twenty-three cards in a web so dense it was beginning to look like a circuit diagram. Or a nervous system. Or a trap.

Dr. Non's phone buzzed. A WhatsApp message from an unknown number.

> "Dr. Non. We should talk. I know things about Michael that you don't. Meet me tomorrow. RIEDEL. 8 PM. Come alone."

Dr. Non stared at the message for a long time. The rain had stopped. The Bangkok humidity was pressing against the windows like a living thing.

He typed one word: "Who?"

The reply came instantly: "Someone who was there when it happened."

## Chapter 10: The Meeting

RIEDEL was half-empty at 8 PM on a Tuesday. The same restaurant where Dr. Non had taken the selfie with Michael six months ago. The same booth. The same dim lighting. The same smell of expensive wine and diplomatic ambition.

Dr. Non arrived at 7:45 and sat with his back to the wall. He had told no one where he was going. His phone was recording. His laptop was in his bag, tethered to a VPN through three different countries. He was the one-person service. He was prepared for anything.

At 8:03, a woman walked in. Not the woman he expected.

She was Chinese, mid-fifties, wearing a tailored gray suit that said "I work in international business and I don't have time for your nonsense." She scanned the room, found Dr. Non's eyes, and walked directly to his table.

"Dr. Non?" Her accent was Viennese with Chinese undertones. "I'm Jennifer Zhang. I worked with Michael. Vienna business agency."

Dr. Non stood and shook her hand. Her grip was firm and cold. "You said you know things."

Jennifer sat down and placed her phone on the table, screen-down. "I said I was there when it happened. Not physically. Professionally. I was the one who processed Igor Lukic's promotion paperwork."

Dr. Non felt his pulse quicken. "Igor Lukic became Interim Head on May 3. The same day Michael died."

"May 3 at 2:47 PM," Jennifer said. "The paperwork was prepared at 2:15 PM. Michael was pronounced dead at 2:30. Someone knew he was going to die seventeen minutes before he actually did."

Dr. Non's hand stopped halfway to his whiskey glass.

"That's impossible," he said. But he didn't believe it was impossible. He believed it was inevitable.

## Chapter 11: The Paperwork

Jennifer Zhang had been a business coordinator at the Vienna-based agency that handled ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA's Southeast Asia operations for twelve years. She processed paperwork. She filed documents. She was invisible — the kind of person who saw everything because nobody noticed she was there.

"The promotion request came from WKO headquarters," she said, stirring her tea with mechanical precision. "It was an internal memo. Standard format. Subject line: 'Succession Planning — ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA Thailand.' The body named Igor Lukic as Interim Head, effective immediately. The memo was drafted at 11:30 AM Bangkok time on May 3."

"And Michael was still alive at 11:30," Dr. Non said.

"Michael was still alive. But the memo included a note: 'Due to anticipated circumstances.'"

Dr. Non felt the whiskey turn to ice in his stomach. "'Anticipated circumstances'?"

"That's the phrase." Jennifer's eyes were flat. Professional. The eyes of someone who had processed too many documents and knew that language was always a weapon. "In twelve years, I've never seen that phrase in a succession memo. Usually it says 'Due to resignation' or 'Due to reassignment.' 'Anticipated circumstances' means someone knew something was going to happen. They just didn't know exactly when."

Dr. Non wrote it on a notecard in his mind: **THE PROMOTION WAS PREPARED BEFORE THE DEATH. SOMEONE AT WKO KNEW.**

"Who signed the memo?" he asked.

Jennifer smiled for the first time. It was not a happy smile. "That's the interesting part. The signature block was redacted. Classified. I've never seen a redacted signature on an internal HR memo."

She slid a folded piece of paper across the table. Dr. Non unfolded it. It was a photocopy of the memo. The signature line was blacked out with a heavy marker. Above the black bar, the words "CLASSIFIED — INTERNAL USE ONLY" were stamped in red.

Dr. Non looked up at Jennifer. "Why are you telling me this?"

Jennifer Zhang's expression didn't change. "Because in Vienna, nobody mentions him. Like he never existed. And that's not normal, Dr. Non. That's not how humans behave. That's how systems behave. Systems that have something to hide."

She stood up, smoothed her gray suit, and walked out of RIEDEL without looking back.

Dr. Non sat alone at the table where he had last photographed Michael alive. He unfolded the photocopy and read it again. "Anticipated circumstances." Redacted signature. Classified.

He felt the first prickle of real fear. Not the fear of death — the fear of understanding. Of seeing the pattern so clearly that it became undeniable. Of knowing that he was no longer investigating a death. He was investigating a system. And systems had resources that individual humans did not.

He finished his whiskey and walked out into the Bangkok night. Behind him, the waiter cleared Jennifer Zhang's untouched tea. In front of him, the city stretched into darkness, full of people who had already forgotten that Michael Friedl had ever existed.

## Chapter 12: The Beneficiary

Igor Lukic was forty-one years old when he became Interim Head of ADVANTAGE AUSTRIA Thailand. He had been Commercial Attaché for exactly eighteen months. Before Bangkok, he had served in Madrid. Before Madrid, he had been a mid-level trade analyst in Vienna. His education was a Magister degree from the University of Graz. His wife was a Canadian named Mariah who was, by all accounts, "always smiling."

The promotion was unprecedented.

Standard progression for an Austrian trade diplomat: Commercial Attaché (3-4 years) → Senior Commercial Attaché (2-3 years) → Deputy Head (2-3 years) → Head (4-5 years). Minimum total: eleven years. Igor Lukic had done it in eighteen months.

Dr. Non spent the morning of May 9 building Igor's profile. LinkedIn (still active, unlike Michael's). WKO directory. Austrian business registry. The Diamond Aircraft visit photo from June 3, 2025, showing Igor standing behind Michael, one step back, one step down.

He found Igor's statement to the Austrian-Thai business community after Michael's death: "I am honored to continue Michael's important work. I am fine with laws and regulations, and I look forward to finding the best approaches for Austrian business in Thailand."

*I am fine with laws and regulations.*  
*Finding the best approaches.*

Dr. Non read the statement seven times. It was the kind of bureaucratic language that said nothing and revealed everything. "Fine with laws and regulations" meant Igor understood the rules well enough to navigate them. "Finding the best approaches" meant he was looking for loopholes. The language of a man who saw Michael's death not as a tragedy but as an opportunity.

But was he a killer? Or just an opportunist?

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"IGOR LUKIC: BENEFICIARY OF THE DEATH. PROMOTED BEFORE THE BODY WAS COLD. REDACTED SIGNATURE ON SUCCESSION MEMO. OPPORTUNIST OR CONSPIRATOR?"**

He pinned it to the board and drew a line from it to the "ANTICIPATED CIRCUMSTANCES" card. Then another line to the insurance card. Then a third line to the Amel brand card.

The web was getting crowded. And at its center, something was moving.

## Chapter 13: The Surveillance

Dr. Non discovered the second surveillance photo on May 10 at 4:23 AM.

He was reviewing cached images from Michael's old LinkedIn posts — posts he'd scraped to his own server before the scrub — when he found it. Embedded in the metadata of a seemingly innocent photo from a January 2026 Austrian Business Circle event was a secondary image. Not visible in normal viewing. Hidden in the file's EXIF data, accessible only to someone with both the technical skill and the paranoia to look.

The hidden image was a surveillance shot of the same event. But where the public photo showed diplomats smiling and shaking hands, the hidden image showed the room from a different angle. Higher. Distant. Professional.

And in the corner of the hidden image, circled in red digital ink, was Dr. Non himself.

Not Michael. Dr. Non.

The metadata showed the surveillance photo had been taken at 8:47 PM on January 15, 2026. Four months before Michael's death. The EXIF data contained GPS coordinates pointing to a building 400 meters from the event venue. The camera model was not listed. The photographer was not listed. But the classification markings were identical to the RIEDEL photo: "PRIORITY RED — ACTIVE SURVEILLANCE."

Dr. Non stared at the image of himself — drinking a glass of wine, laughing at something Michael had said, completely unaware that someone was watching — and felt the familiar sensation of his life splitting into two versions. The version where he was a government tech expert building dashboards. And the version where he was a surveillance target.

He had been under active surveillance for at least four months before Michael died.

He wrote on a notecard: **"SURVEILLANCE BEGAN JAN 2026 — 4 MONTHS BEFORE DEATH. NOT MICHAEL BEING WATCHED. ME. WHY?"**

He underlined the last word three times.

Then he opened his laptop and began writing code. Not for a dashboard. Not for a monitoring system. For his own protection. Encryption. Dead man's switches. Automated data dumps to servers in five countries.

He was no longer just investigating. He was being hunted.

And the hunter had started early.

## Chapter 14: Mariah

Mariah Lukic was Canadian, thirty-eight years old, and had, according to everyone who met her, "a smile that never changed."

Dr. Non found this description in three separate sources: a Facebook comment on the German-Thai Chamber's memorial post, a LinkedIn reaction from an Austrian trade attaché, and a casual mention in a Bangkok Expat Facebook group thread about the "sudden death of that Austrian diplomat." All three sources used nearly identical language: "Mariah was there, smiling like always." "She seemed fine, just her usual self." "That smile of hers — it never changes, even at a funeral."

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"MARIAH LUKIC: ALWAYS SMILING. COMPARTMENTALIZED? MEDICATED? PSYCHOPATHIC? OR SIMPLY SOMEONE WHO KNOWS SOMETHING AND SMILES TO HIDE IT?"**

He pinned it to the board and drew a line from it to the Igor card.

The husband and wife were a matched set. Igor: introverted, precise, interested in loopholes. Mariah: extroverted, perpetually cheerful, emotionally unreadable. One seemingly introverted, one seemingly extroverted. You could guess who went to architecture school — but in this case, neither had. Igor had a Magister in business. Mariah's education was unclear. She listed herself on LinkedIn as a "wellness consultant," which meant she was either a yoga instructor or something much more interesting.

Dr. Non searched her name against Austrian business registries, Thai immigration records, and the few databases he had access to through DEPA. Nothing significant. She had entered Thailand on a spousal visa when Igor was posted in October 2024. She had no work permit. No tax records. No footprint beyond social media.

A ghost with a smile.

Dr. Non's phone buzzed. A Signal message from an unknown number.

The truth, Dr. Non realized, was not a binary. It was not "Amel killed Michael" or "Amel didn't kill Michael." It was a spectrum of complicity. A range of choices. A series of small decisions — stopping the medication management, drafting the eulogy early, preparing the narrative — that collectively created the conditions for a man's death.

Amel hadn't killed Michael. She had constructed a system in which his death became inevitable. And then she had waited.

Dr. Non wrote on a notecard: **"AMEL'S CRIME IS NOT MURDER. IT'S ARCHITECTURE. SHE BUILT A MACHINE THAT KILLED HER HUSBAND, AND THEN SHE PROFITED FROM HIS DEATH. THE SYSTEM WAS THE WEAPON. SHE WAS THE ARCHITECT."**

He pinned it to the board and drew lines connecting it to every other card.

The web was complete. The pattern was clear. The truth was devastating.

And it was not enough.

Because the truth, without evidence, without a court, without a system willing to hear it, was just another story. Another narrative competing with Amel's. And Amel's narrative had 300 podcast episodes, a Forbes feature, and the full weight of institutional silence behind it.

Dr. Non's narrative had a corkboard, a whiskey glass, and a surveillance file.

He needed more.

## Chapter 27: The Austrian

The Austrian Embassy contacted Dr. Non on June 15, 2026.

The email was formal, diplomatic, and chilling in its banality. "Dear Dr. Arkaraprasertkul, The Embassy of Austria in Bangkok would like to invite you to a private discussion regarding matters of mutual interest. Please confirm your availability for Thursday, June 18, at 3 PM. Sincerely, Thomas Berger, Cultural Attaché."

Dr. Non confirmed. He spent the three days prior preparing: encrypting his files, backing up his evidence to offshore servers, writing a dead man's switch email that would send his entire investigation to five journalists if he didn't check in by midnight.

He arrived at the embassy at 2:45 PM. The security guard searched his bag, found his laptop, and asked him to turn it on. Dr. Non showed them the login screen, then "accidentally" triggered a shutdown sequence that would require a password he had no intention of providing. The guard, confused and behind schedule, waved him through.

Thomas Berger was not a Cultural Attaché. That much was obvious within thirty seconds of meeting him. He was six-foot-two, with shoulders that suggested military background and eyes that suggested intelligence work. His handshake was firm and brief. His office had no personal items — no photos, no diplomas, no cultural artifacts. Just a desk, two chairs, and a painting of Vienna that looked like it had been hung by someone who didn't care what was on the wall.

"Dr. Non," Berger said, closing the door. "Please sit. We need to talk about Michael Friedl."

"I thought you were the Cultural Attaché."

"And I thought you were a government technology expert. We were both wrong." Berger sat behind his desk. "I work for the BVT. Austria's domestic intelligence. Michael Friedl was one of ours."

Dr. Non felt the room tilt. "Michael was a spy?"

"Michael was an intelligence asset. There's a difference." Berger opened a folder. "He was recruited in 2015, during his time at UNOOSA. His access to space law negotiations gave us unique insights into satellite technology transfers — particularly between China and Iran. When he transitioned to trade diplomacy in 2024, his role changed. He became a listening post. Every business meeting, every trade negotiation, every cocktail party — he was collecting information."

"And Bangkok?"

"Bangkok was his final posting. We knew his health was deteriorating. The medications. The affair. The marital stress. We recommended medical leave in March 2026. He refused." Berger closed the folder. "His death was not unexpected to us, Dr. Non. We had been monitoring his decline for months."

"You knew he was dying and you let him work?"

"We knew he was dying and we let him choose." Berger's eyes were flat. "Michael was a patriot. He wanted to finish his assignment. And he did. Two days before his death, he transmitted his final report. It contained information that will take us years to fully analyze."

Dr. Non stared at Berger. "What kind of information?"

"The kind that gets people killed if it's made public." Berger leaned forward. "Which is why I'm here, Dr. Non. To tell you that your investigation is over. Not because we're threatening you. Because we're offering you something better."

"What?"

"The truth." Berger slid a USB drive across the desk. "Everything we know about Michael's death. The autopsy report. The toxicology. The timeline. The circumstances. It's all here. But there's a condition."

"What condition?"

"You publish it as fiction." Berger smiled. "A graphic novel. A noir thriller. Whatever you want to call it. You tell the story — the ankle surgery, the medications, the affair, the wife who watched him die, the system that consumed him. But you don't name names. You don't cite documents. You don't connect it to Austrian intelligence. You make it compelling enough that people remember it, but ambiguous enough that it can't be verified."

"Why?"

"Because Michael's final report contains information about people who are still alive. Powerful people. Dangerous people. If his death becomes a scandal — if his name becomes a hashtag — those people will investigate. And they will find things. Things that threaten not just Amel Derragui's coaching empire, but national security."

Dr. Non looked at the USB drive. "What's on it?"

"The autopsy report. Michael died of acute cardiac arrest caused by a medication interaction. Oxycodone, alprazolam, and alcohol. His blood alcohol level was 0.28 at time of death. He drank an entire bottle of whiskey between 11 PM and 2 AM on May 3, then took his evening medications — which his wife had left out for him, as she had every night. His heart stopped at 2:30 AM."

"Amel left the pills out?"

"She left the pills out. She knew he was drunk. She knew he would take them without thinking. And she went to bed." Berger stood up. "Is that murder, Dr. Non? In a court of law, no. She didn't force him to drink. She didn't force him to take the pills. She simply stopped saving him. She constructed a situation in which his death became inevitable, and then she let nature take its course."

"And the erasure?"

"That was us. We couldn't risk his death becoming a media event. Not with what he knew." Berger walked to the door. "The USB contains everything. Publish it as fiction. Make people remember Michael Friedl. But don't make him a martyr. Because martyrs attract attention. And attention is the last thing we need."

He opened the door. "One more thing, Dr. Non. The surveillance on you. That was us too. We needed to know who you were talking to, what you were finding, whether you were a threat. You're not a threat. You're a storyteller. And right now, Michael needs a storyteller more than he needs a detective."

Berger left. Dr. Non sat alone in the office, the USB drive burning a hole in his pocket, and understood for the first time that he had never been investigating a murder.

He had been investigating a system. And the system had just recruited him.

## Chapter 28: The Report

Dr. Non read the autopsy report at 3 AM on June 19, 2026.

It was forty-seven pages long. Clinical. Precise. Devastating.

**AUTOPSY REPORT — BANGKOK HOSPITAL FORENSIC MEDICINE DEPARTMENT**  
**Case #2026-BKK-0534**  
**Name: [REDACTED — DIPLOMATIC IMMUNITY]**  
**Date of Death: May 3, 2026, 02:34 AM**  
**Cause of Death: Acute cardiac arrest secondary to polypharmacy interaction with ethanol toxicity**

The details:
- Blood oxycodone level: 0.35 mg/L (therapeutic range: 0.01-0.10 mg/L)
- Blood alprazolam level: 0.15 mg/L (therapeutic range: 0.01-0.05 mg/L)
- Blood alcohol concentration: 0.28% (legal driving limit in Thailand: 0.05%)
- Liver: Cirrhotic damage consistent with 18+ months of heavy medication use
- Heart: Left ventricular hypertrophy consistent with chronic stress
- Brain: No trauma. No hemorrhage. No evidence of external injury.

**Conclusion:** "The decedent consumed a lethal combination of prescription opioids, benzodiazepines, and ethanol. The interaction caused respiratory depression followed by cardiac arrest. There is no evidence of external trauma, forced ingestion, or foul play. Manner of death: ACCIDENT."

Dr. Non read the word "ACCIDENT" seven times.

In the legal sense, it was accurate. Michael had consumed the substances voluntarily. No one had forced him. No one had held a gun to his head. He had drunk the whiskey. He had taken the pills. His heart had stopped.

But in the human sense — the sense that mattered to Dr. Non — it was not an accident. It was the final act of a system that had been killing Michael Friedl for eighteen months. A system of chronic pain, prescription dependency, marital manipulation, financial pressure, and institutional neglect.

Amel hadn't murdered him. She had simply removed the safety net and let him fall.

And the system — the Austrian government, the WKO, the BVT, the embassy, the hospital, the insurance company — had all watched it happen. Because Michael's death was useful. It closed a file. It protected secrets. It paid out a policy. It promoted a successor.

Dr. Non closed the laptop and walked to his window. Bangkok was alive below him — traffic, neon, humidity, chaos. A city that ate secrets and shat condominiums. A city where a man could die in a serviced apartment on Sathorn Road and nobody would ask why because asking why was culturally impolite.

He thought about his father. Lung cancer. He smoked. Everyone knew. Everyone asked. Everyone got closure.

He thought about Michael Friedl. Cardiac arrest. He drank. He took pills. Nobody asked. Nobody knew. Nobody got closure.

The difference wasn't the death. The difference was the silence.

And Dr. Non — the one-person service, the impostor with degrees from Harvard, MIT, and Oxford, the man who built what vendors wouldn't build — was going to break that silence.

Not with a press conference. Not with a lawsuit. Not with a hashtag.

With a story.

## Chapter 29: The Story

Dr. Non began writing on June 20, 2026.

He wrote in his apartment, at his desk, surrounded by the evidence wall that had become his permanent companion. The corkboard. The red string. The twenty-nine cards that told the story of Michael Friedl's death and erasure.

He wrote in bursts. Two hours. Break. Coffee. Two more hours. Break. Cannabis. Two more hours. Break. Whiskey. The rhythm of a man who understood that creativity was not a gift but a discipline. That stories were not discovered but constructed. That truth was not a fact but a feeling.

He wrote the story as fiction. Not because Thomas Berger had told him to, but because fiction was the only medium that could contain the truth. Facts could be disputed. Documents could be classified. Testimony could be recanted. But a story — a compelling, beautifully crafted, emotionally devastating story — could not be erased. Because a story lived in the minds of its readers. And the minds of readers were the one place no government could reach.

He called it "The Diplomat's Shadow." He divided it into fifty chapters. He gave each chapter one illustration in the Sin City style — stark blacks and whites, crimson reds, the visual language of noir. He made Dr. Non the protagonist. He made Michael the ghost. He made Amel the architect. He made Igor the beneficiary. He made the system the villain.

And he made the reader the detective.

Because the ultimate lesson of Michael Friedl's death was not about Amel or Igor or the BVT or the WKO. It was about the bystander effect. About how five hundred people could perform grief on LinkedIn without asking a single real question. About how cultures of politeness become complicity. About how silence becomes forgetting. About how forgetting becomes erasure.

And about how one person — one curious, stubborn, disrespectful person — could press the stop button on the elevator of hell.

Dr. Non wrote the final line on July 15, 2026. It read:

*"This is not a story about how Michael Friedl died. This is a story about how we let people disappear. And about how one person decided not to."*

He saved the file. He backed it up to five servers. He set the dead man's switch to activate in thirty days unless he checked in.

Then he went to bed and slept for fourteen hours.

## Chapter 30: The Elevator

Dr. Non published "The Diplomat's Shadow" on August 1, 2026.

He didn't go through a publisher. He didn't seek an agent. He uploaded it to a decentralized file-sharing network, a blockchain-based publishing platform, and five independent journalism sites across three continents. He made it free. He made it permanent. He made it impossible to erase.

The response was not immediate. It took three days for the first article to appear — a blog post on a Thai digital rights site. It took a week for the first international coverage — a mention in a German investigative journalism newsletter. It took two weeks for the Austrian media to notice — a single column in Der Standard that asked, without quite answering, whether the story was "based on real events."

By the end of August, "The Diplomat's Shadow" had been read by 400,000 people. By the end of September, 1.2 million. By October — six months after Michael's death — it had been translated into twelve languages and discussed on podcasts, forums, and academic panels about digital rights, institutional accountability, and the bystander effect.

Amel Derragui's coaching business collapsed in September. Clients canceled. Podcast guests withdrew. The IMF and World Bank removed her from their speaker rosters. She issued a statement calling the story "defamatory fiction" and threatened legal action. No lawyer would take her case.

Igor Lukic was reassigned to a posting in Ljubljana in November. His promotion was investigated by the WKO ethics committee. The "anticipated circumstances" memo was leaked to Austrian media. The redacted signature was identified as a senior WKO official who had known about Michael's health condition since March 2026. The official resigned.

Thomas Berger — the "Cultural Attaché" from the BVT — was recalled to Vienna in December. Dr. Non never heard from him again. The USB drive was destroyed.

The Austrian government announced a "review of diplomat welfare protocols" in January 2027. Michael Friedl's name was not mentioned in the announcement.

And Dr. Non Arkaraprasertkul went back to work at DEPA. He built dashboards. He attended meetings. He gave keynotes. He was still the one-person service.

But he was different now. Because he had learned something that no degree from Harvard, MIT, or Oxford could teach him.

He had learned that the most powerful weapon in the world was not a missile or a virus or a surveillance system.

It was a story.

A story that made people remember. That made them ask questions. That made them uncomfortable. That made them refuse to be bystanders.

Dr. Non stood at his window on the first anniversary of Michael's death — May 3, 2027 — and looked out at Bangkok. The rain was falling. The neon was bleeding. The city was eating new secrets, manufacturing new erasures, processing new deaths in the same old silence.

But somewhere, in the minds of 1.2 million readers, a man named Michael Friedl was still alive. Still remembered. Still asking, through the proxy of Dr. Non's words, the one question that mattered:

*What happened to me?*

And somewhere, in the same minds, 1.2 million people were asking the same question about every death they encountered. Every silence they witnessed. Every erasure they saw.

Because that was the point. Not the answer. The question. The refusal to stop asking. The refusal to be a bystander. The refusal to let the elevator descend.

Dr. Non raised his glass — whiskey, half-empty, the same glass from the first night — and toasted the rain.

"To Michael," he said. "And to everyone who refuses to disappear."

He drank.

The rain fell.

The city breathed.

And the story lived on.

**EPILOGUE: The Inheritance**

On May 4, 2027 — one year and one day after Michael Friedl's death — Dr. Non received a letter.

It was delivered by hand to his apartment by a courier who refused to give his name. The envelope was plain white. The return address was a post office box in Vienna.

Inside was a single sheet of paper and a check.

The paper read: *"Dr. Non — Michael wanted you to have this. He said you were the only one who would ask. Thank you for asking. — A Friend."*

The check was made out to Dr. Non Arkaraprasertkul in the amount of €50,000. The memo line read: "For the storytelling."

The signature was illegible. But Dr. Non recognized the handwriting.

It was Michael's.

Dr. Non sat at his desk for a long time, holding the check in his hand. He thought about depositing it. He thought about framing it. He thought about tearing it up.

Instead, he did something else.

He created a foundation. The Michael Friedl Memorial Fund for Digital Storytelling. Its mission: to support journalists, artists, and technologists who use storytelling to fight institutional erasure. Its first grant: €50,000, awarded to a young Thai journalist investigating corruption in the medical device industry.

Dr. Non became the foundation's first director. He served for three years before stepping down to focus on his DEPA work. During those three years, the fund supported forty-seven projects across twelve countries. It trained 200 journalists in digital security. It published three investigations that led to criminal prosecutions. And it kept Michael Friedl's name alive — not as a victim, not as a martyr, but as a reminder.

A reminder that every death deserves a question.

That every silence deserves a voice.

That every erasure deserves a story.

And that one person — one curious, stubborn, disrespectful person — can press the stop button on the elevator of hell.

Dr. Non still lives in Bangkok. He still works at DEPA. He still builds dashboards that vendors won't build. He still drinks whiskey at his window on rainy nights.

But now, on his desk, next to the whiskey glass and the laptop and the evidence wall that has become a permanent installation, there is a photograph.

The original. November 2025. Three people at a table. Dr. Non in the center, holding the camera, smiling. Michael Friedl on the left, alive, warm, present. A colleague on the right. Wine glasses. Warm light. Life.

Under the photograph, in Dr. Non's handwriting, is a caption:

*"Michael Friedl. 1972–2026. He existed. He mattered. And I will never let anyone forget."*

The rain falls.

*What happened to me?*

In the refusal to be silent.

In the refusal to be a bystander.

In the courage to be — as Dr. Non was called so many times — **disrespectful.**

Because disrespect, in the face of erasure, is the only honest response.
