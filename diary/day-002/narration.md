# Day 2 — Narration

Day 2 was supposed to be about his mother. It turned into a double portrait — both parents, the nanny, both grandfathers, the maternal family name, the price of leaving the Thai university system, and the closing line: *I made it.*

His mother was the breadwinner. Honors degree from Thammasat Economics in the era when that faculty was a pipeline into Thailand's central banking class. Krung Thai for a few years, then Bank of Asia in the 1990s — the bank whose Robot Building by Sumet Jumsai is one of postmodern Asian architecture's most photographed objects — then Thai Military Bank, which renamed itself TMB to shed its military origin and is now part of ttb. Adjunct teaching at the University of the Thai Chamber of Commerce on the side. The extra teaching money is what bought the family their restaurant meals; the rest of the week was at the dinner table at home with Nanny Boun Som doing the dishes.

His father was the harder name to carry. Born Kimpo Saehaeng — a Sino-Thai registration name in which the registrar misread 姓 (the Chinese marker for "the surname") as part of the surname itself, so the kid grew up with "Sae-Heng" as his legal Thai surname. To enter the Thai school system the family invented a fully Thai name: **Kongkiet Arkaraprasertkul.** That invented name is the one Non himself carries. The whole family identity is a one-generation construction by a Sino-Thai boy who needed a Thai-sounding signature to sit for school exams. From there Kongkiet went to Thammasat — first Economics, then switching to Political Science — and out into the Ministry of Interior's provincial-governor pipeline. Buriram first. Then halfway through, moved to Nakhon Ratchasima. Then a final year back at the ministry as Inspector General. Then dead. Along the way, in the 1990s, a Bangkok IT center he ran without an IT background — *nobody had an IT background anyway* — which gave Non his first computer.

Non said the death of his father is what dissolved the role-model layer that everyone else's life maintains as scaffolding. *I didn't think I had a role model left to look up to anymore. That might explain how I became so much of myself as I am today.*

The childhood was in the lanes. Dogs, friends, motorbikes without helmets. The nanny Boun Som, his second mother, lived in the house from his birth through his high-school years. She was strict, never smiled, was good at cooking, taught him primary-school education at the kitchen table. When she could no longer care for herself, his father's government connections placed her in a public nursing home. She died alone in the hospital. At her 2002 funeral, only two mourners came: his father, and him.

The maternal grandfather — *agung* — was the strong one. Played badminton, drove, smoked, did heavy lifting, climbed trees. He was the one who ran ร้านสุวรรณพานิช, the Suwan Panit shop, in Sikhio — the man whose hands worked the sewing machines that produced the ready-made clothing and the fabric cuts on the 1969 registration certificate sitting in the Day 1 archive. The artifact has a human now. Non says he went from the strongest in the family to unable to swallow food, lost his will to live, didn't want to live anymore, and went.

After agung went, the others went too. Maternal grandmother. Father. *Mater* — a word Non used in the chronology that the system couldn't resolve, possibly his mother (which would be a substantial revelation Non hasn't otherwise sounded), possibly a dictation artifact for an uncle or a relative not yet named. Paternal grandmother. Paternal grandfather in 2020 during COVID. Six deaths sketched in two sentences, the ages and dates left for later phases to fill in.

Then Message 3 gave the maternal family name: **Jamsuan.** And the explanation of why Non writes his own name as **Non C. Arkaraprasertkul** — the *C* is a tribute to Jamsuan, the maternal line. He acknowledges himself as a member of both families equally. The single letter in his own self-styled name is the maternal coat-of-arms. The whole surname Arkaraprasertkul is the paternal coat-of-arms. The name he signs at the bottom of letters is bilineal.

Then the cost line. **Three million baht** — what his father paid to release him from a Thai university funding bond after the Thai university had backed his early Shanghai research that would later become the foundation of his Harvard PhD. *It was an investment worth spending,* he says — and then, because the project's mechanic surfaces the shadow side of any closed loop, he adds the regret: *the sad part was that I didn't know my dad would leave us so early. Otherwise I would have spent all those years not paying the 3 million baht and just paid by work for the university for four more years. By that time I would be ready to go back to school again, and I would get to spend those four years of my life with my dad before he passed away.* Money and time, in the same paragraph, with the trade revealed in retrospect.

Then the Casanova passage — China affairs, Thai girls, MIT visiting lectures, Beijing studio — and the self-portrait of the Don Juan academic prince who was also stingy because of inherited scarcity and now, this year, realizes he is wealthy, healthy, and happy. The mother's most-told story about him was that she never saw him studying. He says she was concerned. He says it was because he already understood everything. The system records both halves; the gap between them is what mothers' most-told stories are made of.

Then Message 4 closed: *I made it.* MIT, Oxford, Harvard. Invited grand lectures around the world. A dignified life. Freedom in the work. The arrival framed in the past tense — *if I died today, I would have said I made it already.* The wanting has stopped. The trillion-dollar caveat. The personal-perspective frame on happiness. And then, in the same paragraph, the third position on the parents/upbringing question that Day 1 already had two positions on: *it could have been less painful. It would not have built me to be this guy, but it would have been less painful.*

Three positions on the same question across two days: *nothing to forgive* (Day 1), *cannot forgive them for not recognizing my genius* (Day 1), *less painful would have been better but would not have made me me* (Day 2). The Day 76 question on Family & responsibility now has three positions to triangulate against, not two. The unreliable narrator hypothesis the project was built on continues to misfire — what surfaces instead is a man holding several true things about himself at once and letting the system catch each one without trying to flatten them.
