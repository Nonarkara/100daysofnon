## Message 1 — mother's career, father's name, AFS to US

Okay, so I remember that I never have a problem with not having enough love for my parents. I can always find my mom and my dad. I look up to them, of course. I look up to them too much that when my dad passed away, I didn't think I had a role model left to look up to anymore. That might explain how I became so much of myself as I am today.

For my mom, she was a strong person in the family because she was the breadwinner. She worked in the bank and in many banks, actually. She started off because she got an honor degree from the Faculty of Economics, which at the time was a really prestigious school that educated many famous bankers and economists and so on. She worked for the Central Bank of Thailand, sort of — the Krung Thai Bank, which is pretty much the bank that the government set up in order to do all the government businesses. I get paid through that bank.

For the past seven years I worked for the Thai government, and she worked there for a few years until the money ran out — like two kids, and she couldn't wait until she got promoted. In a state-owned bank, the salary usually doesn't rise really fast. Then the roaring 90s came in, and she had to move to a bank called the Bank of Asia or Asia Bank or something like that. The famous robot building was the iconic headquarters, which is today still there and has been changed in terms of functionality too. Many banks got sold, and I think the Bank of Asia was sold to ABN Amro and UOB or something like that, and it's no longer a Thai bank.

Before that, my mom moved to the Thai Military Bank, which later on was renamed TMB because it wanted to erase the image of being a bank for the military, which it was created for that purpose at the beginning. She had a really stable career and desired to early retire when my dad became a governor of Buriram province first, and then he got moved halfway to Nakhon Ratchasima, and then he was moved back to the ministry for his final year to work as Inspector General. That was a good career for a young boy, my dad, who did not have a good family name. I mean, he came up with a family name himself, my current family name.

What he told me, and his name on the birth certificate was actually a romanization of his Chinese name. It's the Chinese name Kimpo Saehaeng, and as we know, Sae means last name — it's the word *xing* in Chinese, but it got put in there because the person in charge of writing down the names thought the word Sae means something always part of the last name, and it's actually not.

Kimpo Saehaeng became Kongkiet Arkaraprasertkul. He told me that he had to have a Thai name in order to go to Thai school. I believe that the Teochew dialect of Chinese must have been his first language as well, because I believe that my grandmother's grandfather, when I remember them, still couldn't speak good Thai. When I was old enough to remember them, they still spoke Thai with a really thick Chinese accent. That's how my family grew up, and they happened to actually grow up in the northeastern part of Thailand. That's maybe how my dad and my mom connected very easily when they were fellow students at the Faculty of Economics at Thammasat University, even though my dad switched and got his degree instead in Political Science. That's how he later on showed us the path to become a governor, and he got to become one before he retired and passed away.

That's not it either, because I remember my mom was working quite hard. She had to leave the house really early on, and that's why I always got on with her in the car so that she can drop me off at a bus stop where I could take a bus to my school. After primary school, I had to go to school a bit further away back then around my house. Everything was rice fields, which is very different from today, whereby you get these sky train lines, like a snake cutting through with a big interchange in the middle that connects pretty much three provinces: Pathum Thani, Nonthaburi, and Bangkok in the middle. In that area alone, it was really fascinating how things move so fast from rice fields to this urban metropolis. Even people who live in the suburbs, like myself, could not feel like it's only 25 minutes to downtown with the sky train. I could definitely afford it because I'm the upper middle class now, but things were not like that.

I remember that it was very repetitive, or maybe, as psychologists like to say, kids see everything in hyper vision — as in they have these high-fidelity brains that want to capture everything, because the palette itself still needs refinement and refinement comes from the environment. That's why kids are able to actually remember boredom more than adults, and that's why kids like to play more, because when they see things in high fidelities, they want to actually experience this, and if they don't get experience, they get bored. For adults, you tend to take things for granted because large parts of our brains get occupied by something else that is less experiential, and you can even say, hypothetically — and I think behaviorally — less intuitive to human nature as well.

But then again, in a sense, I grew up in the 80s. I still remember we didn't have to wear any helmets until later on. We used to have small little motorbikes once we moved out, and there was a big, big market, a wet market, which I hated at the beginning but now I actually kind of enjoy it because it's so lively. I can feel and see the lifestyle that I missed very much when I spent a decade of my life overseas.

That's really strange because I never thought of myself as having a chance to go abroad at all, but it's just a series of coincidences. For instance, I had a friend, not a very good student. He's a very good soccer player, actually, and he can play any kind of sport. He's a big, massive guy, dark-skinned, and he looked very Thai. Among us, many of us look Chinese, like me, for instance, but many of us look Thai, like this guy. His name is Pop, by the way, that's his nickname, and funny enough, he always dragged me to all kinds of exams. He also dragged me to take these exams that, if you passed, you could skip a year of high school. Funny enough, I passed with flying colors, but Pop did not pass, along with all the colleagues that Pop brought with him to take his exam. That's how I got to college at 16 years old because of Pop, who actually had to spend a couple more years in high school before he could graduate, or even just one more year, I think.

Anyway, the point is it's just coincidence. The American Field Service Specials program that brought me to the US for the first time inspired me to come back to Harvard and MIT as well. A friend of mine wanted to apply for it. I think his nickname was Ta, and then, of course, he didn't get it because they only picked one from each province, and my school happened to be in another province, not in Bangkok. I was number one in my province of Nonthaburi, and my name and the name of the province sound similar in a sense. That was a coincidence that eventually got me to the US for the first time in 1998, which is the same year I got into college to be a first-year architecture student.

## Message 2 — childhood in the lanes, Nanny Boun Som, family dinners, the chronological order of family deaths

But the childhood itself was in the lanes. I play in the lanes. I had fun in the lanes. I played with dogs in the lanes. My parents were really busy. I grew up with my nanny Boun Som. She was really strict, and I always remember her never smiling at me or whatever, but I think she loved me a lot.

We had to take her to the nursing home, and my dad at the time worked for the government, so he had some connections to put her in a public nursing home and supported her once in a while until she could no longer speak or be able to live by herself. We had to transport her to the hospital, and then she died alone.

Ever since I was born, I think from day one, all the way to when maybe I was in high school, I lived with this lady who actually taught me a lot of things. Primary school education. She taught me to be strict, kind of, and she's really good at cooking, even though I didn't get to learn anything from her and I was a naughty kid. She's very conservative and traditional. When she died, my dad and her, I think the year was 2002, only two of us were at her funeral. Her kids were not there at all. Her friends were not there at all. She died alone, but we love her. I love her. She was my second mom, really, because she taught me a lot about a lot of things.

My dad and my mom, they were in and out. My mom works a lot and works hard, but every time we have a chance, we would go eat food together. I remember the small little hot pot restaurant, which in Thailand we call sukiyaki, even though they were pretty much shabu-shabu rather. Because we misunderstood what sukiyaki was, we call shabu-shabu sukiyaki, but that's what we love, because it's not sukiyaki in a Japanese style and it's not a shabu-shabu either. It's more like the Teochew / Chaozhou style hot pot with a lot of flavored meatballs and then flavored meats and fish. It's really lovely.

My mom, because she was also teaching at some university — like the University of the Thai Chamber of Commerce — every time she got extra money from teaching, she would take us to eat at a restaurant, because otherwise we would eat at home and Nanny Boun Som would cook food for us, which was fine too, because we got to eat on the same table every day, literally. I mean I love eating with my parents. I love sitting right there with them, sharing with them about my days, and I believe they love it too, because back then there was no TV, no phones, nothing — only one phone in the house, and nobody was allowed to use the TV during dinner time either. We were just chatting, talking about food, and Nanny Boun Som would just take care of all the dishes, even though later on I tried to help her quite a little bit too.

Later on, before she had to leave the house because she could no longer live a life with us, because she could no longer help herself, there was a time when I realized things are going to be very different. I think the first person who passed away was my *agung*, or my maternal grandfather. He was very strict. He is also very good at badminton. It's just very abrupt how he went from the strongest guy in a family, who could drive and smoke cigarettes, very heavy lifting, could climb trees and do all kinds of things, and could even do all kinds of fabric and garment stuff, like stitching using sewing machines, because he runs the shop in our rural hometown. Just one day, something happened. He could no longer swallow food by himself. He became weak, and then he lost his will to live and just didn't want to live anymore.

I think a couple of years later, my maternal grandmother also passed away, and then my dad, and then my *mater*, and then my paternal grandmother, and then my paternal grandfather in 2020 during COVID.
