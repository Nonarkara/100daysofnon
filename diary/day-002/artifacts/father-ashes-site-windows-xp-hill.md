# Artifact — Father's burial site, the "Windows XP screensaver hill"

**File:** `father-ashes-site-windows-xp-hill.png`
**Submitted:** 2026-05-25 (very late, after Non said he was going to sleep)
**Subject:** The location in rural Thailand where Non's family scattered his father's bones and ashes. Father (Kongkiet Arkaraprasertkul) chose the spot because the landscape resembled the iconic Windows XP *Bliss* wallpaper — rolling green hills, partly cloudy blue sky. He was particularly attached to that version of Windows.

---

## Non's framing (verbatim)

> This is another important location. This is a location where we spread the bones and ashes of my dad because he wanted to be in a place in a hill that looks like the screensaver from Windows XP. Somehow he was really attached to that version of Windows.
>
> We put him there, and I'm sure he's really happy. Lots of fortune tellers were telling us that they were seeing my dad having a good time traveling, having no strings attached, no problem with his lung anymore, and enjoying his afterlife. Those are good things to hear. Whether or not this is true, there's no way to prove it, but I'm happy to hear that my dad is no longer sick. He's no longer with us, but he's always gonna be with me.

## What the photo shows

A pavilion / sala building with a green sloped roof, set against rolling green hills under a soft-cloud sky. Foreground: paved road, manicured grass, scattered trees. The composition closely matches the Windows XP *Bliss* wallpaper (Charles O'Rear, 1996, Sonoma County) — rolling green foreground, hill rising to a cloudy blue sky horizon. The intentional choice of resting place echoes the wallpaper.

## The pop-culture detail

The Windows XP *Bliss* wallpaper (the rolling green hill against a blue sky with white clouds) was the default desktop background of Windows XP, shipped 2001. The photograph by Charles O'Rear was taken in 1996 in Sonoma County, California. It became one of the most-viewed photographs in history. Non's father's attachment to XP — and specifically to this image enough to want it as his resting landscape — anchors the father generationally as someone whose adult professional life coincided with the rise of personal computing in Thailand.

This connects back to Day 2 Message 3: father ran a Bangkok IT center in the 1990s. Windows XP (2001) was the operating system that defined the early-2000s Thai government IT modernization. His attachment is biographical, not just aesthetic.
