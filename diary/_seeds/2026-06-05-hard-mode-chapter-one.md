# HARD MODE — Chapter One: "The Noiseless Kind"

> First chapter. Written to be gripping from the first sentence, in his voice. The split-screen version lives in `/layers/`.

---

Let me ask you something before we begin.

Have you ever been in a room that was *exactly* the right temperature — not warm, not cool, just the precise degree at which your body stops noticing itself — and felt, not comfort, but a specific, almost clinical dread?

That is where I am right now. My apartment. Bangkok — or what Bangkok became, which I will get to — on a Tuesday that is essentially indistinguishable from all the other Tuesdays that have been indistinguishable from each other for longer than I can comfortably remember.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us, as algorithms do, finding in the topology of our combined data some constellation of compatibility I cannot quite locate in the room when she is in it. She is — and I say this without cruelty, only precision — exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex, the kind that is technically correct in every parameter and delivers, for both of us, something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times now. The fourth will probably be the last, not because anything goes wrong, but because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

---

When I was forty-four years old — in the life I want to tell you about — I read somewhere that Albert Camus opened his most famous essay by saying there is only one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide. He was right, though he was asking the wrong version of the question. The interesting version is not *whether* to continue but *which layer to continue in* — and the reason nobody asks that version is that, for most of human history, there was only one layer to be in. You were born, you suffered, you died. The options were stark but uncomplicated.

I am going to tell you a story that starts forty-four years into one life and ends, maybe, at the beginning of another. I should warn you: I am Thai, I am forty-four, I am tired, I am not dead, and I have recently come to suspect that none of those facts mean what I thought they meant. Also — (pun not intended) — I may be inside a simulation. But let me get to that in a moment.

---

The message came on a Tuesday, which is significant only because the day before had been Monday, and Mondays are when I answer the week's accumulated messages from people who emailed me on the weekend about smart city projects, governance dashboards, digital twins. I help cities understand themselves, which is a sentence that sounds more poetic than it is, and which I have been saying for about ten years since I stopped being a professor and started being whatever I am now. A practitioner. An itinerant consultant. A Thai man showing up in rooms where people do not expect him.

Her name was Pui.

She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, in an architecture school so far out of Bangkok that you took the BTS to Ladkrabang station and then a train from there, and I did that for exactly one week before I moved into an apartment my father rented for me near campus. She was one of maybe eight women in our entire cohort — architecture schools in that part of Bangkok in the late nineties were not known for their gender balance — and she was funny, and she knew exactly what she was doing in a crit, and she had this way of making a model out of materials nobody else would have thought of. We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been closer if we had been anywhere else, or had tried a little harder, or had been a little less afraid.

I had been writing on her Facebook wall every year on her birthday for maybe four years. A few words. Happy returns. Something like that. You do this for people you were not quite close enough to call, which is most people, eventually.

That Tuesday I wrote something on her wall. "Twenty-five years since Ladkrabang. Hope you're well." And within two hours I had a message from Kamashit, who had been close to her, who I had not spoken to in longer than I can reconstruct, and the message said:

*Non. You didn't know. She died. Last year. Cancer.*

---

I scrolled. The way you do.

There were the posts from friends in Thai, the kind that accumulate on a dead person's wall and never quite know what tone to take. There were photographs from a ceremony I did not recognize. There was a date, which I will not give you, because it is hers and not mine to share.

And then, two weeks before all of that, there was her last post.

It was not what you would write if you knew you were dying. It was too calm for that, too specific, too much like something she had been thinking about for a long time and had decided, in that particular window, to write down. The Thai was careful. Clear. I will translate it for you, roughly:

*"To whoever reads this first after I'm gone — don't waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you'll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P."*

I read it three times.

Then I put my phone face-down on the table and looked at the wall — the exact-right-temperature air, the date in an hour, the city outside that had stopped surprising me — and I felt something I had not felt in years.

I felt the hair on my arms.

Because here is the thing I am not going to explain to you tonight, the thing that sat in my chest like a key finding a lock it did not know it had been cut for.

I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in any way I could have told you, even a minute before reading her last post. But *in my body*, in the specific, subterranean way you know a place you have been to in a dream so vivid it left a residue — I already knew. Left, not right. Ask before the memory goes.

The algorithm-matched date arrived at seven and rang the buzzer and I let her up.

We had a drink. We had sex — the technically correct kind. She fell asleep before ten. I lay on my back in the exact-right-temperature dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: *How many times have I done this before?*

Not the sex.

The whole thing.

---

*— chapter one of Hard Mode, a novel being written across one hundred days*
