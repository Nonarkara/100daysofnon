## Premise (logline)

A Thai academic-turned-practitioner named Non, who has won everything a mind can win and still does not know what any of it was *for*, begins to suspect that his exhausting forty-four years are a chosen simulation — and sets out to prove which world is real, in a future where the only way to test the question can kill you for good.

## The world (his rules, organized)

- **The real world** is post-scarcity and deathless. Quantum manufacturing prints anything; organs are replaced, stem cells run age backward; you can have as many children as you want. Nobody does. When everything you could wish for is granted, the one thing left is **boredom**, and boredom at the scale of forever is the only real disease.
- **The cocoons.** People check into a chair-like cocoon and live a full hard life — ~70 years of suffering that takes **half an hour to an hour** of real time, indistinguishable from reality. You choose the scenario: era, country, body, even species (down to a plankton). Human is the hardest setting, because of "the part of the brain that won't stop."
- **The addiction loop.** You come out swearing *never again*. Two months later the perfect world is unbearable again and you go back in. Suffering becomes the drug; the clean world is the comedown.
- **Suicide is the exit code.** It is locked into the sim as something read out of *every religion* — because **religion itself is the lock**: a set of codes built to irrationally tie players to values so they don't quit the hard life too early and spoil the point. Hence in-game suicide-prevention everywhere.
- **The horror at the center.** Some players become so convinced the sim is realer than the real world that, back outside, they try to suicide *out of the real world* too — and there is no layer below it, so they **die for real.** Which raises the question the whole book turns on: *is there a layer below? Are they escaping, or just dying?*
- **The multiplying universes.** As energy tech advances, more simulations run at once, more varied so they don't collide; the asymptote is **one customized universe per person** — but that's flagged as too costly, and it doesn't fix the real problem (no new babies). Utopia tips into dystopia: infinite abundance, almost nobody left to use it.
- **The released shadow** *(handle with care)*. In the deathless utopia, with religion gone and extinction looming, the old restraints fall away — money is meaningless, sex is public and even encouraged (anything to make babies). The hard-sims become the **moral sandbox**: the only place left where humans can meet suffering, loss, cruelty, and their own darkest desires — safely, consensually, and then forget. A utopia with no way to metabolize the shadow sends its citizens *down* to feel it. This is a serious, uncomfortable theme, not a titillation; if it ever surfaces publicly it needs Non's hand and a careful frame.

## The protagonist — Non

Has everything academically; lost since the lectureship; gives **100% of every paycheck to his mother** (an economist) and wishes he'd been successful enough that she could "just count money." Carries one real, dangerous line: ***"It would be okay if I die today."*** In this world that sentence is no longer equanimity — it's the **exit code**, one keystroke from either waking up or dying for real.

## The engine — the detective spine **[push]**

Make it a detective story of **exits**. Non investigates people who *left* — and the first case, **Case Zero, is Pui**: a college classmate who died young of a cancer she seemed almost to *allow*, because the game had gone banal for her around thirty. Her too-clean death is the thread he pulls. Each subsequent "case" is another player who found the seam.

The clues are the things from his real life, re-read as evidence:
- **Dreams** = bleed-through from his other scenarios (the brain recovering data between lives).
- **The luggage in Christchurch** = the program's narrative-smoothing: bad events that resolve *too* neatly (the broken bag → insurance cash → both the new bag and the repaired one). When the world keeps paying you back, you're not lucky — you're being *written*. **[push]** His real "everything-happens-for-a-reason" (Mantra 5) becomes, in the fiction, literal proof of authorship.
- **Recurring people** = other players cast into the same Thailand-scenario.

The stakes are lethal and clean: the only way to *test* which world is real is to take the exit — and if you've guessed wrong about which layer you're standing in, you don't wake up. The detective's final move is the one he can't take twice.

## Themes → this is Nonism, in fiction

The book is the fictional vessel for what Non actually believes — which is what will make it *his* and not anyone else's sci-fi:
- **Mantra 6 (simulation; work with the symbols anyway):** the spine. Religion-as-code is Mantra 6 turned inside out.
- **Mantra 5 (everything happens for a reason; suspend the catastrophe verdict):** the luggage, the verdict that always reverses — recast as evidence of a Writer.
- **Buddhism, his substrate:** the memory-wipe is *anatta* (no fixed self); choosing your next birth is literal rebirth; the cocoon-loop is *saṃsāra*; suffering-as-the-only-aliveness is the First Noble Truth made into a plot. *(implicit, never preached — per §12)*
- **Camus:** "the one serious philosophical problem is suicide" — here it is the literal mechanic.
- **The mother, and the deepest question [push]:** he doubts even his love for his mother — is it real, or just oxytocin, "nanobots firing a trillion times a second"? The Nonist answer, and the book's resolution, should be: *the chemistry is not the opposite of the love — it is the love.* The symbol is the thing. You don't escape the simulation to find meaning; **you make meaning by choosing to stay inside it and love anyway.** "Which world is real" turns out to be the wrong question. That is the both-at-once (§12.6), and it is the only ending that is actually him.

## Continuation — the real world, the body, the mother (2026-06-05, ii)

### The real world, in close-up *[his]*
Non has run this maybe half a dozen times or more — he is in his 40s out there too — and goes back in every few years because there is nothing else to do. Out there he had a beautiful wife and everything; they no longer live together. He lives alone in a perfect house, takes algorithm-matched dates, has sex, sometimes meets again (so few people left), tires of it, never again. Round and round. *(Fiction — the real-world frame. **Do not fold into the biography:** the real Non chose not to marry.)*

### The numbness — the body that utopia erased *[his + push]*
Maybe a thousand years after 2026. Humanity has evolved *out of* sex. Only ~1% still reach orgasm. Touch, sweat, the kiss, the bite still register — but the genitals have gone quiet; the act happens, the arrival doesn't. With the arrival gone, the chromosome-deep drive to reproduce drains away — which is why the species is dying inside its own abundance. **[push]** This is the somatic floor under the boredom: utopia didn't only go dull, it went **numb.** The cocoon is where the body still *works* — the two hours of it, the sweat, the mess, the cheats you pay your own dry-cleaning for, the moment one of them actually arrives. People don't go down into the hard lives only to suffer. They go to **feel.**

**[push] — the reality test is sensation.** This reframes the search. "Which world is real" cannot be settled by logic; both look real. It can only be answered by the body: **you are real where you can still feel.** And that is already pure Non — Mantra 1, *"the fun is in the flow"*; the present moment; wabi-sabi's love of the worn, sweating, impermanent body. The numb perfect world fails the only test that matters. The detective's instrument is not a clue-board. It is his own skin.

### The mother-search — the heart *[his + push]*
Death stops frightening him, because death = waking next to Pui. And then the real quest: **find the woman who played his mother.** Maybe she was a character — a Westworld host with no one inside her. Maybe she was a real player, and out there she is now a stranger in her twenties. He wants to find her and ask one thing: *how was it, to have me as a son?* **[push]** Pair it with Pui-as-Case-Zero: **Pui is the grief that opens the case; the mother is the love that drives it to the end.** And it closes the oxytocin question — if she was "only" a character, was the love false? The book's answer holds: no. It was the realest thing in any layer. The chemistry was never the opposite of the love; it was the love.

### Discretion / register
The sexual material is, in his voice, frank and graphic. For any public or literary form the register is **his call** — the theme (embodiment, anhedonia, the body as the last real thing) is profound and keepable; the anatomical catalogue is not the point and should be handled with restraint. Not public until he calibrates it.
