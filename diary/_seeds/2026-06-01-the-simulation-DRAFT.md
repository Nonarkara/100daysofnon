I am forty-four, turning forty-five, on the balcony again, and the smoke is doing the thing it does, which is to loosen the wall between the question I carry around all day and the question underneath it. The day-question is the ordinary one: what am I doing, am I doing it well, who needs what from me by Friday. The one underneath has not changed in ten years — not since I lost the lectureship, which at the time felt like the end of a life and now looks like a pastime I used to have. The question underneath is just: what is this for. I have not answered it. I have, in the meantime, accidentally built a career — from the academic who could argue anyone into the ground on the strength of nothing, to a practitioner with a title in a government agency and a seat in rooms with mayors and governors and ministers from countries that are not mine, teaching five times a week to people who are not students, standing there as the Thai man you were not supposed to be able to undermine. I did not think I would survive the lost decade. I did. That is not the same as knowing what it was for.

So here is the thing the smoke handed me tonight. What if I die — at some decent age, having lived the whole thing out — and I simply wake up. Not as a baby, not as a light. In this exact body, at exactly this age, which is, I suspect, the prime of me. And I understand, the way you understand a dream the instant it ends, that the life I just finished — seventy-eight years of it, say — took about half an hour. That this, the waking, is the real world. And that everyone else has just woken up too.

And the first person I meet is Pui.

She was my classmate, twenty-five years ago, in an architecture school so far out of Bangkok you took a train to it and then gave up and lived there. She was, when we met in 1999, a slightly heavy, very funny girl, and then over the years she became beautiful in the specific way the country rewards — pale, fine-skinned, composed — and because there were so few women out there in the middle of nowhere, she became the star of the place, and I liked her the way you like someone you are too young and too far from home to do anything sensible about. In the world I just left, she died. I found out the way you find out about everyone now: I wrote on her wall for her birthday, because the wall is still there, and a friend texted me — you didn't know? She died last year. Cancer, fast; I was overseas, as I always was; I did not go to the funeral.

In the waking world she gets up a few seconds before me, because she left the game a little early. And she tells me what I have started to suspect: that the real world is unbearably good. Nobody is sick. Nobody has accidents. Nobody dies. You can live forever, which is the problem — because the only thing left to do, once nothing can go wrong, is to stay sane. So you check yourself into a hard life. You choose a scenario, a century, a country, a body; you can be anything, down to a single plankton turning in the sea, feeling its small amount and forgetting it. If you choose to be human — the hardest setting, because of this part of the brain that will not stop — they wipe your memory on the way in, so that you actually live it. And once you are in, you cannot leave until it ends. That is the rule. That is the whole point of the rule.

Pui left early because, for her, it stopped being interesting around thirty — the work, the family, the lovely banality of it — so she let the cancer have its way, which is allowed, because there is always a cause, and you knew the cause going in, so you are not permitted to be surprised. I stayed the full term because, by some accident of casting, I got to spend my one hard life doing the only thing I have ever genuinely loved, which is to sit in a library and read until the years go quick. It was exhausting. It was heavy with feeling I did not ask for. I would not do it again. And the moment the real world gets perfect enough, I will do it again.

Which is the door this whole biography has been walking toward without saying so. Because the dreams — I think the dreams are the leak. The brain recovering, in the dark, scenes from the other lives it has run. And if that is true, then the question underneath the question is not *what is this life for.* It is: if this is not the real one, and the real one is too good to be true — or true, and unbearable — then where is the thing that is neither. That is the part I cannot see yet. That is the part we go and find. Murakami got to the door and described the wallpaper in the hallway. I want to know what the hallway is *for.*

It would be okay if I woke up tomorrow.
