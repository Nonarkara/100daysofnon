## The Chinese characters — 發祥

- **發 (fā / fat / hoat)** — *to issue forth, prosper, flourish.* Single most common shop-name character in Sino-Thai commercial signage; carries connotations of 發財 (*fā cái* — "get rich").
- **祥 (xiáng / cheung / siong)** — *auspicious, fortunate, propitious.*
- **Together: 發祥** — literally *"origin of prosperity"* / *"flourishing-auspicious."* Standard auspicious shop-name compound. Also functions as a verb-phrase meaning "to arise / originate from a place" (as in 發祥地, "place of origin").

The pairing 發祥 is widely used by Sino-Thai trading families as a *hao* (號) — the shop-style auspicious name distinct from the family surname. Tens of shops across the Chinese diaspora carry this exact name.

## On Non's claim that "the Thai name is a translation of the Chinese name"

Trust first, verify second.

Non's framing is that **สุวรรณพานิช (Suwan Panit, "Gold Commerce") is a translation of 發祥 (Fā Xiáng, "Prosperous Auspicious").** Examined literally character-by-character, the pairing isn't a direct lexical translation:
- 發 (prosper / issue forth) ≠ สุวรรณ (gold, from Sanskrit *suvarna*)
- 祥 (auspicious) ≠ พานิช (commerce, from Sanskrit *vanik*)

But Non's framing is almost certainly correct at the level it's meant — **the names are paired as the same auspicious-trading-house identity, semantically translated rather than literally.** Sino-Thai shop registration in the 1960s commonly took this pattern: the family kept the Chinese 號 on the signboard for cultural continuity, and chose a Thai registration name that *evoked the same auspicious-prosperous-trading-house register* rather than transliterating phonetically. *Suwan Panit* and *Fa Xiang* are both "an auspicious shop that sells gold/commerce" — the spirit-translation, not the dictionary-translation.

This is documented in Sino-Thai naming-convention scholarship (e.g., Skinner's *Chinese Society in Thailand*, 1957; later Tong Chee-Kiong & Chan Kwok Bun on Sino-Thai business identity). It's not a Non-specific quirk; it's how the families *meant* the names to relate.

So: **the framing is right.** The names are translations of each other — the way Thai-Chinese families meant translation in 1969. Not the way a Google Translate API means translation in 2026.

## The address discrepancy — 186 vs ๑๓ — explained

The previous artifact (the registration certificate) listed the address as บ้านเลขที่ ๑๓ หมู่ที่ ๔. The current photograph shows the house numbered **186** in red paint on the wall.

**Non confirmed 2026-05-25:** *"The number of the houses has changed a lot over time because of the postal system that was changed over time."*

So both numbers are correct, separated by ~50+ years of Thai municipal renumbering. The 1969 registration was issued under the older numbering scheme (บ้านเลขที่ ๑๓ หมู่ที่ ๔); the current 186 reflects subsequent Thai postal-system reforms (Thailand modernized its postal addressing through the 1970s–90s, and many rural districts saw multiple renumbering passes as villages grew). The shop is the same physical building.

Logged as ✓ — same address, different epochs of the numbering system.
