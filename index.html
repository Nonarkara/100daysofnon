Hard Mode · Chapter One · the door is on the left

A man who has won everything a mind can win, and still does not know what any of it was for, sets out to find the one person who can tell him — in the only world where the roles come off.

Chapter One — “The Noiseless Kind”

Let me ask you something before we begin.

Have you ever been in a room that was exactly the right temperature? Not warm. Not cool. The precise degree at which your body stops noticing itself.

In a room like that, you do not feel comfort. You feel a specific, almost clinical dread.

That is where I am right now. My apartment. Bangkok — or what Bangkok became, which I will get to. A Tuesday like all the others.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us. She is exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex. The kind that is technically correct and delivers, for both of us, something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times. The fourth will probably be the last. Not because anything goes wrong. Because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

I used to think the only serious question was whether to continue. I have since revised my opinion. The real question is which layer to continue in. Nobody asks that version. For most of human history, there was only one layer. You were born. You suffered. You died.

Something happened recently that made me think there might be more.

The message came on a Tuesday. Her name was Pui.

She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, at a school at the far edge of the city. Far enough that you took two trains to get there. I did that for one week before finding a room near campus. She was one of a handful of women in our cohort — funny, exact, with a way of making something beautiful out of materials nobody else would have thought of.

We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been closer if we had been a little less afraid.

I had been writing on her Facebook wall every year on her birthday for four years. A few words. You do this for people you were not quite close enough to call. That is most people, eventually.

That Tuesday I wrote: “Twenty-five years. Hope you're well.”

Within two hours, a message from a mutual friend. He had been close to her.

Non. You didn't know. She died. Last year. Cancer.

I scrolled. The way you do.

Posts that accumulate on a dead person's wall and never quite know what tone to take. Photographs from a ceremony I did not recognize. A date I will not give you, because it is hers.

And then, two weeks before all of that: her last post.

It was not what you would write if you knew you were dying. Too calm. Too specific. It read like something she had been sitting with for a long time and had finally decided to put down.

I will translate it roughly:

”To whoever reads this first after I'm gone — don't waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you'll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P.”

I read it three times.

Then I put my phone face-down on the table.

I looked at the wall. The exact-right-temperature air. The date in an hour. The city outside that had stopped surprising me.

I felt something I had not felt in years.

I felt the hair on my arms.

Because I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in any way I could have told you a minute before reading her post. But in my body. The way you know a place from a dream so vivid it left a residue.

Left, not right. Ask before the memory goes.

She arrived at seven. Rang the buzzer. I let her up.

We had a drink. We had sex — the technically correct kind. She fell asleep before ten.

I lay on my back in the exact-right-temperature dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: How many times have I done this before?

Not the sex.

The whole thing.

Chapter Two — "Twelve Minutes"

The instructions ran to fourteen pages.

I sent them at half past four, which is when the city outside stops sounding like anything in particular and starts sounding like itself. I sent them to the system that had been building things on my behalf for the past six hours — asking permission every twenty minutes to enter some new corner of the machine, and me half-awake each time to say yes, yes, proceed.

The work was what I have started calling white coating. Someone else's phrase, probably, but it has become mine now. You talk to the machine. The machine codes. The machine is better at this than you are, and you have stopped pretending otherwise. What you are still better at is knowing what to ask for.

I had asked for a satellite dashboard. The kind of thing I used to spend three weeks building; the machine does it in twelve hours, and it is technically better, though it lacks the particular wrongness that I used to put into things that made them mine. I am still deciding how much that matters.

Mali was asleep across my left foot. Expo had taken the chair.

I lay down on the floor beside my desk — there is a specific kind of tired that only gets worse in a bed — and I thought: twenty minutes. The tokens would replenish. The system would need another permission at twenty-two.

I had not been sleeping the way people sleep. I had been sleeping the way water moves through sand: a few hours here, a few there, the boundary between asleep and awake no longer a hard line. This is a side effect of the work. The work runs at all hours because the machine runs at all hours. And when you have calibrated yourself to a machine's rhythm, something shifts in you that does not easily shift back.

I closed my eyes.

And I was somewhere I had been before.

Not somewhere I remembered. Somewhere I recognized — which is a different thing entirely. The body knows places the mind has edited out.

There was no Bangkok here. No exact-right-temperature air. No algorithm. The light was the particular gold of a place I could not name, and I was younger in it, or older, depending on which direction you were counting from.

Pui was there.

She was not the person from the Facebook photographs. She was not the person from the architecture school twenty-five years ago. She was — I do not have a word for what she was. Prior, maybe. She was prior.

She was waiting. She had been waiting a long time, but she did not seem impatient. She seemed like someone who had finished a book and was sitting with it.

You came, she said.

I said something back. I do not remember what. What I remember is that it seemed unnecessary, because she already knew.

I couldn't wait, she said. You know how I am.

I did know how she was. I had always known how she was, even when I didn't know her. Even in the years we spent in the same building and never quite made it to a real conversation. Even across four years of birthday messages so brief they barely counted as contact. I had always known. It was the kind of knowing that does not come from experience.

You'll understand the door, she said. When you get there, you'll know which one.

Left, I said.

Yes.

She was leaving again. I could feel her going the way you feel a word you know perfectly and then inexplicably cannot find — not a loss exactly, but a kind of suspension.

The script, I said. It was not a question.

She made a sound I recognized as: more or less.

Is it the same?

Mostly. A pause. A little faster each time.

A little faster each time. I had felt this. I had felt this without knowing I felt it, the way you stop noticing the angle of light in a room you have been in too often. The way I could find my way through a city I had never been to. The way certain people seemed not like strangers but like people who had gone ahead and turned around to wait. The way I had arrived at certain rooms — certain meetings, certain hours in the library, certain nights with no one there — and felt not surprise but recognition. Oh, I had thought, each time. This again.

Is it always the same end? I asked.

She thought about this.

You always get out, she said. But never the same door.

I woke up to Mali standing on my chest.

The system had sent a permission request. The screen said 04:42.

Twelve minutes.

I lay there for a moment in the dark, the dog's weight on my sternum, and I tried to hold what I had been holding a second ago. It was already going. The quality of the light in that place. The sound of her voice. What I had felt — not emotion exactly, more like information. The kind of thing the body takes in and the mind edits immediately, because the mind does not know what to do with it.

Left, not right.

Ask before the memory goes.

I pressed the permission. The system proceeded.

Outside, the city had gone quiet in the way it goes quiet at almost-five — not silent, just held. Like something taking a breath.

Mali settled.

I looked at the ceiling.

I thought about the first time I walked into a room I had definitely never been in and knew precisely which chair was mine. I thought about the professor at MIT who looked at me on the first day and said, without preamble, you've done this before. I had laughed. I had been twenty-three. I thought he was being encouraging.

I thought about Pui's last post. The door is always on the left.

I thought about how many times I had arrived somewhere and known, in that prior way, exactly how the room would end.

A little faster each time.

I pressed the permission again.

The machine kept building.
