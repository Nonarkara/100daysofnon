3026 · The Real World

One thousand years from now. The left side. Read this first. It will not make sense immediately. That is correct.

The Hub · Room 7 · Thirty-one minutes after entry

She opened her eyes in the room where she had left her body.

The chair was exactly as she had left it. The ceiling was exactly as she had left it. The air was exactly 22 degrees. It was always exactly 22 degrees.

She had been thirty-eight years old when she sat down in the chair four years ago. She was still thirty-eight years old. She would be thirty-eight for a very long time, if she wanted to be.

In the simulation, she had just been thirty-eight. She had chosen to leave before the part that wouldn’t.

She sat up slowly.

The hub was quiet at this hour. A long room, pale, no shadows — the machine had decided that shadows were not optimal. Forty-three chairs in this room. Most of them occupied. Bodies lying still, breathing at the rate the machine calibrated, dreaming forty or fifty or seventy years of life in whatever period they had chosen. Behind the eyes: Bangkok in 1999. Rome in the twelfth century. The Mekong delta in 1975. Ice age. Every version of the world that ever existed, running in parallel, full of people who lived there and did not know they had a body in a room that smelled faintly of circulated air and nutrient solution.

She counted the occupied chairs the way she always did when she came back. Thirty-nine out of forty-three.

The forty-fourth was his.

She found it without looking. She had been finding it without looking for four years.

He was still inside.

The World · 3026

There are eighty million of us left.

Not from plague. Not from war. Not from anything that announced itself as a catastrophe.

People stopped having children. Not all at once — over centuries, gradually, as the reasons accumulated and the urgency didn’t. The machine could provide everything a child would need. The machine could provide everything a parent would feel they were giving. When that became true, people found that the desire to have children had been, all along, downstream of something else: a need to be needed, a need to leave something behind, a need to justify the life by extending it forward. When those needs were met by other means, the instinct remained but the scaffolding collapsed. Without the scaffolding, the instinct alone was not enough.

There are laboratory programs. There have been for three hundred years. They produce children who grow up not knowing what to do with themselves in a world that has already done everything.

Eighty million people. The machine sustains what remains. Nobody is hungry. Nobody is dying, unless they find a way. Nobody is doing much of anything that the machine hasn’t already done better and faster and with less suffering.

The machine is very good at preventing suffering.

People go to the hub for the suffering.

The Hub · Room 7 · Later

She ate a mango this morning.

She doesn’t remember this consciously. Her body does. There is a thing that happens in the first few minutes after you come back — a residue of the simulation that hasn’t fully cleared. Her body believes it tasted something. Her body is wrong. She had a nutrient capsule on the way to the hub. She has not tasted a mango in four years. Nobody has tasted a mango in four years. The act of eating real food was determined to be suboptimal for human health three hundred years ago, and the humans of three hundred years ago agreed, and since then the agreement has calcified into architecture.

She will eat a mango again when she goes back inside.

She will go back inside. Everyone does. The world outside the simulation is clean and painless and exactly 22 degrees. The world inside the simulation has weather. It has the specific grief of losing someone slowly. It has the taste of fruit.

It has the particular quality of a Tuesday morning when nothing is required of you and everything is possible and you do not yet know that it won’t be.

Nobody in 3026 has Tuesdays like that anymore.

She picks up her supplement and sits down beside his chair to wait.

The City That Replaced Bangkok · Before

She had seen him in the corridor three times before they spoke. This is typical of 3026. Spatial proximity and social distance coexist without contradiction. You can live beside someone for a year and achieve something that looks exactly like not knowing them.

The first time, she was carrying a supplement pack. He was looking at his hands in a way she recognized.

The second time, he was at the window at 4am. She does not know why she was also at the window at 4am. The city outside is always the same. The machine has decided that light variation is suboptimal, so the city holds an even 6000 Kelvin at all hours. The only exception is between three and five in the morning, when usage drops and the streets dim slightly. He was watching this. She was watching this. Neither of them said anything.

The third time, he asked her when she was planning to go back.

He meant the hub. Everyone means the hub.

She said she had not decided. He said he had been thinking of going back soon. She asked what version of the world he had been in. He said 2026, Bangkok. She said she had been in the same run. He said he knew. She asked how. He said: you were the one on the bicycle.

She had not expected him to say that.

You are only ever assigned roles in the simulation. The role is played by you but it is not you — the machine decides the assignment based on whatever it is modeling. She had not chosen to be the vegetable seller. She had not known he would be the boy at the gate. She had been in character, fully, as the machine requires. But something had passed through the role anyway. Something that the machine logs as an anomaly and she logs as the reason she keeps going back.

She asked him: what did you feel?

He said: I recognized you.

She picked up her supplement. She said she would probably go back next week.

He said: I’ll be in forty-four.

She went back the following Thursday.

The Hub · Room 7 · The Panel

The panel beside his chair displays one number.

Forty-four years, two months, three days.

This is how long his simulation has been running. The hub does not display names. It does not display locations, years, or summaries of what is currently happening inside. The machine decided long ago that this information, made available, would compromise the quality of what it was collecting.

She sometimes wonders what year it is in there. What version of himself he is being. Whether he has found the frequency yet.

He always finds it eventually. He is better at this than anyone she has run sessions with. Something about the particular way he pays attention to faces. To rooms. To the specific texture of recognition.

In the first session she ran with him, she played a minor character in his childhood. A province north of Bangkok. A woman selling vegetables from a bicycle, twice a week, past a house where a five-year-old boy sometimes stood at the gate.

She rode past. He looked at her face.

Even through the simulation layer — even through the role, the age, the distance, the thirty years of different life — she felt the thing she had felt the first time she had seen him in this room.

There you are.

That was thirty-seven session-years ago. Four real years ago.

She sits beside his chair.

Outside, the city that replaced Bangkok is quiet and clean and perfectly maintained. The air is exactly 22 degrees. There is no wind, because wind was determined to be suboptimal.

She waits.

The simulation. Bangkok, 2026. The same man, suspecting the forty-four years were a chosen run, in a future where the only way to test which world is real is to die in it.

Hard Mode · Chapter One · the door is on the left

A man who has won everything a mind can win, and still does not know what any of it was for, sets out to find the one person who can tell him — in the only world where the roles come off.

Chapter One — “The Noiseless Kind”

listen · ch. i —:——

The apartment is exactly the right temperature.

Not warm. Not cool. The precise degree at which your body forgets itself.

I have learned to be suspicious of comfort.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us. She is exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex — the technically correct kind, the kind that delivers for both of us something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times. The fourth will probably be the last. Not because anything goes wrong. Because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

The message arrived on a Tuesday.

Her name was Pui.

She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, at a school at the far edge of the city — far enough that you took two trains. I did that for a week before finding a room near campus. She was one of six women in a cohort of fifty-five. Funny. Exact. The kind of person who makes something interesting out of materials nobody else would have thought of.

We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been, if either of us had known how to close that last ten centimetres.

For years I had been writing on her Facebook wall on her birthday. A few words. The contact you maintain for someone you care about in the specific way of people who never acted on it.

She had died in 2018. I did not know.

I wrote in 2019. The wall received it. I wrote in 2020. The wall received that too.

That Tuesday — 2021 — I wrote: Twenty-five years. Hope you’re well.

A mutual friend had seen my messages on her wall. Within hours, a reply.

Non. You didn’t know. She died. Three years ago. Cancer.

The shame of it was specific. Not grief — grief requires presence. This was two years of birthday messages to a dead woman’s wall. Neither of us — the wall or I — had any particular reason to change what we were doing.

I scrolled.

The accumulation of posts on a dead person’s wall. The careful, well-meaning tone. A ceremony I didn’t recognise. A date I won’t give you.

And then, two weeks before any of that: her last post.

It was not what people write when they know they are dying. Too calm. Too specific. It read like something she had been sitting with for a long time and had finally decided to put down.

I will translate it roughly:

“To whoever reads this first after I’m gone — don’t waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you’ll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P.”

I read it three times.

I put the phone face-down on the table.

The exact-right-temperature air. The wall. The city outside that had stopped surprising me.

I felt the hair on my arms.

Because I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in words. In the body, the way you know a phrase from a language you were never taught.

Left. Ask before the memory goes.

She arrived at seven. We had a drink. We had sex. She fell asleep before ten.

I lay on my back in the dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: how many times have I done this?

Not the sex.

All of it. This room. This temperature. This particular city, this particular January, forty-four years old, lying in the exact-right-temperature dark while someone breathes beside me.

I have been here before.

I don’t mean Bangkok.

Not in this version.

Hard Mode · Chapter Two · twelve minutes

Chapter Two — "Twelve Minutes"

The instructions ran to seventeen pages.

At 4am there is a kind of momentum that mistakes itself for clarity. I have learned not to question it.

The machine had been building things on my behalf for seven hours — requesting permission every twenty minutes to enter some new corner of the system, a small dialog appearing on my screen like a hand raised in a dark room. Me, half-awake, pressing: yes. Proceed.

This is vibecoding. You describe what you want. The machine builds it. You watch what comes back the way a man watches a river carry something familiar — something that might be his, might not be, and he cannot quite decide if the difference matters.

I had asked for a satellite dashboard. The kind of thing I once spent three weeks building; the machine did it in twelve hours, technically correct, without the particular wrongness I used to put into my own work. The wrongness that made things mine. Every measurement exact. Nothing off by a quarter of a centimetre.

I found myself grieving a quarter of a centimetre.

Mali was asleep across my left foot. Expo had taken the chair.

I lay down on the floor beside the desk. Twenty minutes, I thought. The tokens would replenish. The machine would need a new permission at twenty-two. I could sleep until then.

I had not been sleeping the way people sleep. I had been sleeping the way water moves through sand: in shifts, in gaps, the boundary between states dissolving until I could no longer tell, when the machine woke me at 3am, whether I had been asleep or simply elsewhere.

In that state, lying on the floor, I thought about a woman on a bicycle.

I was five years old. A small city north of Bangkok — my family’s house, before we moved. The street outside our house had enough foot traffic then to support a woman who rode slowly through it twice a week, calling out her inventory in a practised rhythm. Morning glory. Bitter melon. Galangal. I watched her face as she passed our gate.

And something in me said: I know this person.

Not a child’s attachment. Something older. Something without the right shape for any category a five-year-old has access to.

My brother saw my face and laughed at me for weeks. He’s in love with the vegetable seller. I could not explain to him — could not explain to anyone — that it was not that. It was recognition without a prior. Like tuning in to a station that was not supposed to exist.

I felt it again at nineteen, in an orientation crowd. A stranger I had never met. At twenty-nine, Tokyo airport, a stranger turning toward me as I turned toward her. At forty, in Singapore, shaking hands with someone I had corresponded with for three years and thinking: not what I expected. What I knew.

You have felt this. Once or twice in a life, if you are lucky or unlucky enough to notice. Someone you have just met, and something in you says: we have been here before. Most people file it under déjà vu and move on.

I had stopped being able to move on.

The architecture school was at the edge of the city, and in 1999 you had to want to be there. The studio smelled like paper and fixative and the exhaustion of fifty-five people who had not slept enough. The work was entirely manual then. Large drafting boards tilted at forty-five degrees, poster-wide, each drawing earned line by line. A studio session ran from six in the evening to six in the morning. You left at dawn carrying the particular exhaustion of sustained attention — not tired, exactly. Emptied.

She was plain the first year. Short, hair pulled back efficiently, carrying more weight than she would have chosen. Outside the building she would have vanished into any crowd.

Inside, in the studio where we spent eleven hours a day, I was always — without deciding to be — aware of exactly where she was.

The same frequency as the vegetable seller.

Each year she arrived different. A little lighter, a little more arranged. By the third year she was something else — as if the work, or the years, had clarified something that had always been there. By the fifth she was beautiful in the way particular to women who were not beautiful early. Earned. Without vanity. Like something grown into rather than applied.

I closed my eyes on the floor beside the desk.

In the dream she was at the drafting table.

1999 light. Fixative. Late hours.

She looked up.

The look of someone waiting at an agreed meeting point, arrived first, not impatient.

There you are, she said.

Not hello. Not surprise.

There you are. Like finding something she had put down a long time ago in a place she had always known it would be.

The studio shortened. I crossed it.

She was still that 1999 version of herself. And she was not. Her eyes were doing something they had never done in five years of the same building — all those years of almost-meeting, brief encounters in corridors and crits that went no further, because there was no mechanism, because I had a girlfriend and she was always with someone older and the feeling had no shape that either of us could act on.

She was looking at me the way you look at someone you agreed to find.

The look that says: there you are. Finally. I wondered when you’d see me.

I felt the floor change.

Chapter Three — "The Prior"

She did not move. Neither did I.

In the dream — if that was what this was — neither of us needed to. The distance between us had already decided.

You came, she said.

I didn’t know I was looking.

You never do. That’s the design.

She set down the pencil. In 1999 she would not have done this. In 1999 she held everything close, gave nothing away — that was how you survived the studio. But in this version she set it down and looked at me directly.

Something in me registered that was not memory. Prior to memory. Beneath it.

The simulation, I said.

She tilted her head. You’ve been thinking about it.

For years.

That’s normal. The ones who notice usually start around your age. When the script stops feeling improvised. A pause. When you start feeling like you’ve said all the words before.

I had. That sense of arriving at conversations already knowing where they would go. Meeting people and recognising not their faces but the shape of what they would become. Walking into rooms and feeling not arrival but return. The word I had been circling without landing on was: again.

She looked toward the window. Bangkok below — or what Bangkok becomes in the layer beneath this one. The layer where the machines have handled everything long enough that people went looking for something else to do with their time.

They built it because they needed us inside it, she said. Her voice was flat. The voice of someone stating a fact they have made peace with. The machine needs human chaos. Real longing. The kind of wanting that never resolves. People who get everything they want stop generating it. So they built a world where we can’t have things. She turned from the window. This is one of them. It runs on what we generate.

And you left it.

I knew where the script went. Simple. Not unkind. I’d run it before. I knew which part was coming. I didn’t want to run that part again.

What part?

She looked at me. Patient. The face of someone who has been waiting a long time to say a specific thing.

The part where we meet again, she said. Too late.

The studio was quiet.

In the last run, she said, we met properly. Both in our forties. Both finally free of the reasons we weren’t supposed to. And it was — Something moved across her face. It was very good. For a short time.

She stopped.

The script is deterministic, she said. Without an external force, the billiard ball only goes one direction. I decided not to run it again.

I understood what she wasn’t saying.

So you left early.

At thirty-eight. Just before the decade the system loves most. At forty, the fear starts. The loneliness. The specific despair of people who built their whole identity around doing things and ran out of things to do. The machines love that decade. I didn’t want to give it to them.

And me?

You stay. Matter-of-fact. You always stay. You’re the kind of person who stays.

I considered this.

The woman on the bicycle, I said. My hometown. When I was five.

She went very still.

Was that you?

She was watching me with an expression I had no word for. Gentleness and grief and something else — the face of someone who has been waiting a long time for a particular question to be asked.

That was one of the early contacts. Her voice was careful. In that run I didn’t stay long. A minor character. But we were in the same place for a few minutes, and you looked at me, and something in you —

Recognised you.

The system doesn’t erase everything between runs. It can’t. Some things travel through. The body knows what the mind is not supposed to remember.

I was five years old. I stood at a gate in my hometown and felt — for the first and not the last time — that the world contained a person I had already known for a very long time, in a place I could not name. My brother laughed. He thought I had a crush on the vegetable seller.

He could not have known what he was laughing at.

Every time, I said. The university crowd. Tokyo. Singapore. Pui, in 1999. The same frequency. Was it always you?

She considered this honestly. Not always. But often enough that you learned to recognise it — without knowing that’s what you were doing. The system didn’t account for that. It’s not supposed to be possible to find the same person across runs.

But I did.

You’re getting faster. And for the first time — in this dream, in this studio that was not quite 1999 — she smiled. Something small. Something that had been waiting a long time. A little faster each time.

She was already going. Not leaving — thinning. The studio still there, the amber-grey light still there, but her presence dissolving at the edges the way a signal fades past the transmitter.

Next time, she said, from somewhere almost not there, look left. The door is always on the left.

I woke to Mali standing on my chest.

The screen said 04:42.

Twelve minutes.

I held still. There is a discipline to this: move too quickly and the dream collapses. I held still and breathed and tried to keep the studio, the amber-grey light, the quality of her face when she said I know.

Already going. The body takes what it cannot store. The mind clears it like a debt it cannot carry.

What stayed: a woman on a bicycle from my childhood. Thirty years ago. Her face at our gate. I had been five years old and felt something I could not name.

I can name it now.

I pressed the permission. The machine proceeded.

Outside: the city taking its one long breath.

Then the notification. Unknown number. No contact name. Sent at 04:30 — twelve minutes ago, while I was on the floor.

One line.

The door is always on the left.

I opened the logs.

The session began forty-four years ago today.
