3026 · The Real World

One thousand years from now. The left side. Read this first. It will not make sense immediately. That is correct.

The Hub · Room 7 · Thirty-one minutes after entry

She opened her eyes in the room where she had left her body.

The chair was exactly as she had left it. The ceiling was exactly as she had left it. The air was exactly 22 degrees. It was always exactly 22 degrees.

She had been thirty-eight years old when she sat down in the chair four years ago. She was still thirty-eight years old. She would be thirty-eight for a very long time, if she wanted to be.

In the simulation, she had just been thirty-eight. She had chosen to leave before the part that wouldn’t.

She sat up slowly.

The hub was quiet at this hour. A long room, pale, no shadows — the machine had decided that shadows were not optimal. Forty-three chairs in this room. Most of them occupied. Bodies lying still, breathing at the rate the machine calibrated, dreaming forty or fifty or seventy years of life in whatever period they had chosen. Behind the eyes: Bangkok in 1999. Rome in the twelfth century. The Mekong delta in 1975. Ice age. Every version of the world that ever existed, running in parallel, full of people who lived there and did not know they had a body in a room that smelled faintly of circulated air and nutrient solution.

She counted the occupied chairs the way she always did when she came back. Thirty-nine out of forty-three.

The forty-fourth was his.

She found it without looking. She had been finding it without looking for four years.

He was still inside.

The World · 3026

There are eighty million of us left.

Not from plague. Not from war. Not from anything that announced itself as a catastrophe.

People stopped having children. Not all at once — over centuries, gradually, as the reasons accumulated and the urgency didn’t. The machine could provide everything a child would need. The machine could provide everything a parent would feel they were giving. When that became true, people found that the desire to have children had been, all along, downstream of something else: a need to be needed, a need to leave something behind, a need to justify the life by extending it forward. When those needs were met by other means, the instinct remained but the scaffolding collapsed. Without the scaffolding, the instinct alone was not enough.

There are laboratory programs. There have been for three hundred years. They produce children who grow up not knowing what to do with themselves in a world that has already done everything.

Eighty million people. The machine sustains what remains. Nobody is hungry. Nobody is dying, unless they find a way. Nobody is doing much of anything that the machine hasn’t already done better and faster and with less suffering.

The machine is very good at preventing suffering.

People go to the hub for the suffering.

The last thing to disappear was sex. It persisted, mechanically, for two more centuries after children became rare. But once the machine offered a direct neural pathway — faster than the act, more complete, no negotiation, no the-next-morning — the ritual began to hollow out the way all rituals hollow out when the meaning migrates. By 3026 people technically can. Most simply stopped seeing the reason.

Some of them remember that they used to want it. They log this as data.

The Hub · Memory Catalog

She has been in two hundred and forty-three sessions.

She does not remember most of them. The machine files them the way a library files films — compressed to metadata, a title and a duration and a location and a genre. Bangkok, 1999. Duration: five years, three months. Genre: formation. Shanghai, 1929. Duration: two years, eleven months. Genre: loss. Lagos, 2019. Duration: one year, seven months. Genre: unclassified.

She remembers fragments. A dress in Shanghai. A colour she has not seen outside the simulation — a particular yellow that belonged only to 1929 light through river fog. A doorway in Lagos that she stood in for ten minutes without knowing why.

In thirty-one of those sessions, she found the same frequency.

Not the same person. Different roles, different names, different ages. A man at a pier in Marseille in 1967. A boy at a gate in a small city north of Bangkok. A colleague in a Tokyo office who turned at exactly the wrong moment and looked directly at her and then looked away, because in that session they had been given no mechanism to act on it.

The machine does not intend this. She has asked it — in the way you ask the machine things, obliquely, by structuring what sessions you run and how you log the experiences afterward. The machine does not intentionally assign her to his runs. He is simply the node that generates the anomaly, and the anomaly draws its most productive data from the sessions where she is present.

She is not certain whether that constitutes love or logistics.

In the simulation, it feels the same.

The Hub · Room 7 · Later

She ate a mango this morning.

She doesn’t remember this consciously. Her body does. There is a thing that happens in the first few minutes after you come back — a residue of the simulation that hasn’t fully cleared. Her body believes it tasted something. Her body is wrong. She had a nutrient capsule on the way to the hub. She has not tasted a mango in four years. Nobody has tasted a mango in four years. The act of eating real food was determined to be suboptimal for human health three hundred years ago, and the humans of three hundred years ago agreed, and since then the agreement has calcified into architecture.

She will eat a mango again when she goes back inside.

She will go back inside. Everyone does. The world outside the simulation is clean and painless and exactly 22 degrees. The world inside the simulation has weather. It has the specific grief of losing someone slowly. It has the taste of fruit.

It has the particular quality of a Tuesday morning when nothing is required of you and everything is possible and you do not yet know that it won’t be.

Nobody in 3026 has Tuesdays like that anymore.

She picks up her supplement and sits down beside his chair to wait.

The City That Replaced Bangkok · Before

She had seen him in the corridor three times before they spoke. This is typical of 3026. Spatial proximity and social distance coexist without contradiction. You can live beside someone for a year and achieve something that looks exactly like not knowing them.

The first time, she was carrying a supplement pack. He was looking at his hands in a way she recognized.

The second time, he was at the window at 4am. She does not know why she was also at the window at 4am. The city outside is always the same. The machine has decided that light variation is suboptimal, so the city holds an even 6000 Kelvin at all hours. The only exception is between three and five in the morning, when usage drops and the streets dim slightly. He was watching this. She was watching this. Neither of them said anything.

The third time, he asked her when she was planning to go back.

He meant the hub. Everyone means the hub.

She said she had not decided. He said he had been thinking of going back soon. She asked what version of the world he had been in. He said 2026, Bangkok. She said she had been in the same run. He said he knew. She asked how. He said: you were the one on the bicycle.

She had not expected him to say that.

You are only ever assigned roles in the simulation. The role is played by you but it is not you — the machine decides the assignment based on whatever it is modeling. She had not chosen to be the vegetable seller. She had not known he would be the boy at the gate. She had been in character, fully, as the machine requires. But something had passed through the role anyway. Something that the machine logs as an anomaly and she logs as the reason she keeps going back.

She asked him: what did you feel?

He said: I recognized you.

She picked up her supplement. She said she would probably go back next week.

He said: I’ll be in forty-four.

She went back the following Thursday.

The Hub · Room 7 · What She Left

She wrote it at 3am in the simulation.

Late in that run — three days before she exited. She had been in the Bangkok session for eleven years, real time. Inside, it had felt like a life: the years before the studio compressed into childhood, the studio itself vivid and slow, the years after scattered and then quickening as the session approached its close.

She had been putting off the exit because of him. His run was still active, somewhere in the same city, in a version of Bangkok where they had never found a mechanism to act on what was between them.

She wrote the post as her character would write it. The machine would log it as authentic terminal reflection. Consistent with established personality parameters. It would not flag it.

She left the code in the second sentence. The door is always on the left. Not metaphorical. A direction. In the hub, Room 7, the door to the main corridor is on the left. She knew he would not understand it when he found it. She knew he would find it years after she was gone from that run. She calculated — across the fragments of memory she carried about how his intelligence worked — that he would not be able to stop thinking about it once he started.

She exited three days later. Walked to the panel at chair 44.

He was still inside. He would be inside for another four years before the post found him. She had miscalculated the delay. She had thought one year, maybe two.

She sat down. She waited.

The Hub · Room 7 · The Panel

The panel beside his chair displays one number.

Forty-four years, two months, three days.

This is how long his simulation has been running. The hub does not display names. It does not display locations, years, or summaries of what is currently happening inside. The machine decided long ago that this information, made available, would compromise the quality of what it was collecting.

She sometimes wonders what year it is in there. What version of himself he is being. Whether he has found the frequency yet.

He always finds it eventually. He is better at this than anyone she has run sessions with. Something about the particular way he pays attention to faces. To rooms. To the specific texture of recognition.

In the first session she ran with him, she played a minor character in his childhood. A province north of Bangkok. A woman selling vegetables from a bicycle, twice a week, past a house where a five-year-old boy sometimes stood at the gate.

She rode past. He looked at her face.

Even through the simulation layer — even through the role, the age, the distance, the thirty years of different life — she felt the thing she had felt the first time she had seen him in this room.

There you are.

That was thirty-seven session-years ago. Four real years ago.

She sits beside his chair.

Outside, the city that replaced Bangkok is quiet and clean and perfectly maintained. The air is exactly 22 degrees. There is no wind, because wind was determined to be suboptimal.

She waits.

The Hub · Room 7 · Sub-Indicator 7

The panel changed on a Tuesday.

Not dramatically. The machine does not communicate with people in the observation area directly — it would prefer she was not here at all. But she has been reading the panel for four years, and she knows its language the way you learn a face.

Sub-indicator 7 went active.

It took her three days to find the reference in the maintenance documentation. Sub-indicator 7: Active monitoring. Session subject is exhibiting pattern-recognition behaviour directed toward simulation parameters. Probability of self-identification as simulation subject: above threshold. Intervention protocol: stage one.

Stage one meant the machine had decided he was worth keeping. The interventions had started — small orchestrations, coincidences that resolve too neatly, the environment tilting toward him in ways designed to reattach him to his life inside the run.

She knew what stage one felt like from the inside. She had experienced it herself, near the end of her last run. The unexpected good fortune. The sudden clarity. The feeling that the world had finally decided to cooperate.

She had recognised it for what it was. Most people didn’t.

She looked at the panel. Forty-four years, two months, six days.

She wondered if he would recognise it.

The Hub · Room 7 · Sub-Indicator 9

Sub-indicator 9 went active seventeen days later.

Sub-indicator 9: Elevated monitoring. Session subject is exhibiting behaviour consistent with active investigation of simulation parameters. Probability of self-identification: high. Intervention protocol: stage two.

Stage two meant behavioural nodes — background characters redirected from ambient functions, given specific objectives. Keep the subject engaged. Keep the subject alive. Keep the subject in.

She had seen stage two from the inside. Not in her last run — in one from two hundred years ago, a session she had accessed from the catalog years later with a kind of horrified recognition. The nodes had been obvious in retrospect: slightly too responsive, filling their roles with a smoothness that real people didn’t have. She had not noticed at the time. She had been thirty-one in that run and incapable of questioning the good fortune of it.

She wondered if he would notice. She thought he probably would. That was the problem.

Then sub-indicator 12 went pre-active.

She did not know what sub-indicator 12 was. The maintenance documentation did not include it. It was not in any catalog she could access.

Outside the hub, the city held its even 6000 Kelvin. Somewhere in Room 7, the machine was deciding how much it would let a man figure out before the interventions became less gentle than a promotion.

She started looking faster.

A man who has won everything a mind can win, and still does not know what any of it was for, sets out to find the one person who can tell him — in the only world where the roles come off.

Chapter One — “The Noiseless Kind”

listen · ch. i —:——

The apartment is exactly the right temperature.

Not warm. Not cool. The precise degree at which your body forgets itself.

I have learned to be suspicious of comfort.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us. She is exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex — the technically correct kind, the kind that delivers for both of us something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times. The fourth will probably be the last. Not because anything goes wrong. Because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

The message arrived on a Tuesday.

Her name was Pui.

She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, at a school at the far edge of the city — far enough that you took two trains. I did that for a week before finding a room near campus. She was one of six women in a cohort of fifty-five. Funny. Exact. The kind of person who makes something interesting out of materials nobody else would have thought of.

We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been, if either of us had known how to close that last ten centimetres.

For years I had been writing on her Facebook wall on her birthday. A few words. The contact you maintain for someone you care about in the specific way of people who never acted on it.

She had died in 2018. I did not know.

I wrote in 2019. The wall received it. I wrote in 2020. The wall received that too.

That Tuesday — 2021 — I wrote: Twenty-five years. Hope you’re well.

A mutual friend had seen my messages on her wall. Within hours, a reply.

Non. You didn’t know. She died. Three years ago. Cancer.

The shame of it was specific. Not grief — grief requires presence. This was two years of birthday messages to a dead woman’s wall. Neither of us — the wall or I — had any particular reason to change what we were doing.

I scrolled.

The accumulation of posts on a dead person’s wall. The careful, well-meaning tone. A ceremony I didn’t recognise. A date I won’t give you.

And then, two weeks before any of that: her last post.

It was not what people write when they know they are dying. Too calm. Too specific. It read like something she had been sitting with for a long time and had finally decided to put down.

I will translate it roughly:

“To whoever reads this first after I’m gone — don’t waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you’ll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P.”

I read it three times.

I put the phone face-down on the table.

The exact-right-temperature air. The wall. The city outside that had stopped surprising me.

I felt the hair on my arms.

Because I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in words. In the body, the way you know a phrase from a language you were never taught.

Left. Ask before the memory goes.

She arrived at seven. We had a drink. We had sex. She fell asleep before ten.

I lay on my back in the dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: how many times have I done this?

Not the sex.

All of it. This room. This temperature. This particular city, this particular January, forty-four years old, lying in the exact-right-temperature dark while someone breathes beside me.

I have been here before.

I don’t mean Bangkok.

Not in this version.

Chapter Two — "Twelve Minutes"

The instructions ran to seventeen pages.

At 4am there is a kind of momentum that mistakes itself for clarity. I have learned not to question it.

The machine had been building things on my behalf for seven hours — requesting permission every twenty minutes to enter some new corner of the system, a small dialog appearing on my screen like a hand raised in a dark room. Me, half-awake, pressing: yes. Proceed.

This is vibecoding. You describe what you want. The machine builds it. You watch what comes back the way a man watches a river carry something familiar — something that might be his, might not be, and he cannot quite decide if the difference matters.

I had asked for a satellite dashboard. The kind of thing I once spent three weeks building; the machine did it in twelve hours, technically correct, without the particular wrongness I used to put into my own work. The wrongness that made things mine. Every measurement exact. Nothing off by a quarter of a centimetre.

I found myself grieving a quarter of a centimetre.

Mali was asleep across my left foot. Expo had taken the chair.

I lay down on the floor beside the desk. Twenty minutes, I thought. The tokens would replenish. The machine would need a new permission at twenty-two. I could sleep until then.

I had not been sleeping the way people sleep. I had been sleeping the way water moves through sand: in shifts, in gaps, the boundary between states dissolving until I could no longer tell, when the machine woke me at 3am, whether I had been asleep or simply elsewhere.

In that state, lying on the floor, I thought about a woman on a bicycle.

I was five years old. A small city north of Bangkok — my family’s house, before we moved. The street outside our house had enough foot traffic then to support a woman who rode slowly through it twice a week, calling out her inventory in a practised rhythm. Morning glory. Bitter melon. Galangal. I watched her face as she passed our gate.

And something in me said: I know this person.

Not a child’s attachment. Something older. Something without the right shape for any category a five-year-old has access to.

My brother saw my face and laughed at me for weeks. He’s in love with the vegetable seller. I could not explain to him — could not explain to anyone — that it was not that. It was recognition without a prior. Like tuning in to a station that was not supposed to exist.

I felt it again at nineteen, in an orientation crowd. A stranger I had never met. At twenty-nine, Tokyo airport, a stranger turning toward me as I turned toward her. At forty, in Singapore, shaking hands with someone I had corresponded with for three years and thinking: not what I expected. What I knew.

You have felt this. Once or twice in a life, if you are lucky or unlucky enough to notice. Someone you have just met, and something in you says: we have been here before. Most people file it under déjà vu and move on.

I had stopped being able to move on.

The architecture school was at the edge of the city, and in 1999 you had to want to be there. The studio smelled like paper and fixative and the exhaustion of fifty-five people who had not slept enough. The work was entirely manual then. Large drafting boards tilted at forty-five degrees, poster-wide, each drawing earned line by line. A studio session ran from six in the evening to six in the morning. You left at dawn carrying the particular exhaustion of sustained attention — not tired, exactly. Emptied.

She was plain the first year. Short, hair pulled back efficiently, carrying more weight than she would have chosen. Outside the building she would have vanished into any crowd.

Inside, in the studio where we spent eleven hours a day, I was always — without deciding to be — aware of exactly where she was.

The same frequency as the vegetable seller.

Each year she arrived different. A little lighter, a little more arranged. By the third year she was something else — as if the work, or the years, had clarified something that had always been there. By the fifth she was beautiful in the way particular to women who were not beautiful early. Earned. Without vanity. Like something grown into rather than applied.

Her desk was right next to mine. By the third year I had stopped pretending this was neutral. She wore a t-shirt and shorts to the studio like most of us did — nobody was dressing for the hours. At 2am under the drafting light, working, a body stops being anything except what it is. The light at that angle made the room honest. I was aware of her. Not as a plan, not as a decision. The body makes its own notes before the mind approves the transcript.

No perfume. Just pheromone. Just proximity. Just that particular sensibility that certain women have without knowing they have it — the quality that registers in the room before they do.

She was always with someone — usually a senior, the kind of man who fills a room with his mood before he enters it. I had a girlfriend. We walked to the station sometimes. Talked about television, football, whatever. At the station gate we parted. The correct procedure.

I have spent twenty-five years understanding what the correct procedure cost me.

I closed my eyes on the floor beside the desk.

In the dream she was at the drafting table.

1999 light. Fixative. Late hours.

She looked up.

The look of someone waiting at an agreed meeting point, arrived first, not impatient.

There you are, she said.

Not hello. Not surprise.

There you are. Like finding something she had put down a long time ago in a place she had always known it would be.

The studio shortened. I crossed it.

She was still that 1999 version of herself. And she was not. Her eyes were doing something they had never done in five years of the same building — all those years of almost-meeting, brief encounters in corridors and crits that went no further, because there was no mechanism, because I had a girlfriend and she was always with someone older and the feeling had no shape that either of us could act on.

She was looking at me the way you look at someone you agreed to find.

The look that says: there you are. Finally. I wondered when you’d see me.

I felt the floor change.

Chapter Three — "The Prior"

She did not move. Neither did I.

In the dream — if that was what this was — neither of us needed to. The distance between us had already decided.

You came, she said.

I didn’t know I was looking.

You never do. That’s the design.

She set down the pencil. In 1999 she would not have done this. In 1999 she held everything close, gave nothing away — that was how you survived the studio. But in this version she set it down and looked at me directly.

Something in me registered that was not memory. Prior to memory. Beneath it.

The simulation, I said.

She tilted her head. You’ve been thinking about it.

For years.

That’s normal. The ones who notice usually start around your age. When the script stops feeling improvised. A pause. When you start feeling like you’ve said all the words before.

I had. That sense of arriving at conversations already knowing where they would go. Meeting people and recognising not their faces but the shape of what they would become. Walking into rooms and feeling not arrival but return. The word I had been circling without landing on was: again.

She looked toward the window. Bangkok below — or what Bangkok becomes in the layer beneath this one. The layer where the machines have handled everything long enough that people went looking for something else to do with their time.

They built it because they needed us inside it, she said. Her voice was flat. The voice of someone stating a fact they have made peace with. The machine needs human chaos. Real longing. The kind of wanting that never resolves. People who get everything they want stop generating it. So they built a world where we can’t have things. She turned from the window. This is one of them. It runs on what we generate.

And you left it.

I knew where the script went. Simple. Not unkind. I’d run it before. I knew which part was coming. I didn’t want to run that part again.

What part?

She looked at me. Patient. The face of someone who has been waiting a long time to say a specific thing.

The part where we meet again, she said. Too late.

The studio was quiet.

In the last run, she said, we met properly. Both in our forties. Both finally free of the reasons we weren’t supposed to. And it was — Something moved across her face. It was very good. For a short time.

She stopped.

The script is deterministic, she said. Without an external force, the billiard ball only goes one direction. I decided not to run it again.

I understood what she wasn’t saying.

So you left early.

At thirty-eight. Just before the decade the system loves most. At forty, the fear starts. The loneliness. The specific despair of people who built their whole identity around doing things and ran out of things to do. The machines love that decade. I didn’t want to give it to them.

And me?

You stay. Matter-of-fact. You always stay. You’re the kind of person who stays.

I considered this.

The woman on the bicycle, I said. My hometown. When I was five.

She went very still.

Was that you?

She was watching me with an expression I had no word for. Gentleness and grief and something else — the face of someone who has been waiting a long time for a particular question to be asked.

That was one of the early contacts. Her voice was careful. In that run I didn’t stay long. A minor character. But we were in the same place for a few minutes, and you looked at me, and something in you —

Recognised you.

The system doesn’t erase everything between runs. It can’t. Some things travel through. The body knows what the mind is not supposed to remember.

I was five years old. I stood at a gate in my hometown and felt — for the first and not the last time — that the world contained a person I had already known for a very long time, in a place I could not name. My brother laughed. He thought I had a crush on the vegetable seller.

He could not have known what he was laughing at.

Every time, I said. The university crowd. Tokyo. Singapore. Pui, in 1999. The same frequency. Was it always you?

She considered this honestly. Not always. But often enough that you learned to recognise it — without knowing that’s what you were doing. The system didn’t account for that. It’s not supposed to be possible to find the same person across runs.

But I did.

You’re getting faster. And for the first time — in this dream, in this studio that was not quite 1999 — she smiled. Something small. Something that had been waiting a long time. A little faster each time.

She was already going. Not leaving — thinning. The studio still there, the amber-grey light still there, but her presence dissolving at the edges the way a signal fades past the transmitter.

Next time, she said, from somewhere almost not there, look left. The door is always on the left.

I woke to Mali standing on my chest.

The screen said 04:42.

Twelve minutes.

I held still. There is a discipline to this: move too quickly and the dream collapses. I held still and breathed and tried to keep the studio, the amber-grey light, the quality of her face when she said I know.

Already going. The body takes what it cannot store. The mind clears it like a debt it cannot carry.

What stayed: a woman on a bicycle from my childhood. Thirty years ago. Her face at our gate. I had been five years old and felt something I could not name.

I can name it now.

Outside: the city taking its one long breath.

Then the notification. Unknown number. No contact name. Sent at 04:30 — twelve minutes ago, while I was on the floor.

One line.

The door is always on the left.

I opened the logs.

The session began forty-four years ago today.

Chapter Four — "Signal Loss"

I tried marijuana first.

The logic — if it was logic — was that the conversation had happened in a state between waking and not-waking, and that altering the chemistry of the state might widen the gap through which something had come. Three nights, different quantities, same result: slow thoughts, appetite, the particular loneliness of being high alone. No gap. No conversation. The ceiling.

Then alcohol, which I understood better. Alcohol had always had the quality of stripping things back — not to truth, but to the version of yourself that was present before you learned to perform the other versions. I drank on a Tuesday. I drank with a colleague on a Thursday. I drank with intention and without it. I woke each morning with the usual residue and the usual absence.

I was trying to get back to twelve minutes.

During those twelve minutes something had spoken to me. Or something in me had spoken. Or I had assembled — from grief and exhaustion and the specific quality of 4am — a hallucination so internally consistent that it had reorganized the way I looked at the ceiling. I was not certain which. The uncertainty was itself a kind of signal: if it was pure hallucination, it was the most structured one I had ever had, and I have had structure enough to know the difference.

There had been a woman, for two years, who called when she wanted to and did not call when she did not want to. The architecture of the arrangement was entirely hers. I arrived when summoned. I was good at what she summoned me for — she told me this, with the specific detachment of someone reviewing a service. I loved her in the way you love something that is dismantling you slowly without announcing its intentions. I understood only after the silence of a Thursday evening when she stopped calling — weeks passing, then a month, then two — that I had been useful to her the way a good appliance is useful: reliably, without reciprocity, until it wasn’t needed anymore.

This had happened before I understood that the hunger I brought to bed was not ordinary hunger. It was something else — something the body knows before the mind has language for it. That there is a version of this that runs out. That whatever the act of wanting a woman carries — the specific weight of skin on skin, the not-quite-controllable quality of it, the particular proof that you are a living thing with a body and a temperature — there is a version of the future where that is gone. Not taken. Simply not needed anymore. The machine would provide something faster and more complete and without the residue.

I had never said this to anyone. I am not certain it would have made sense to anyone. But it is why I had always brought to bed the quality that had confused and occasionally frightened people who did not expect it from someone my size and my manner — that particular ferocity, the desire to use everything, to hold nothing back, as though each time might be practice for something being counted down on a counter I couldn’t see.

I had not brought this quality to Pui. We had not arrived at a place where it could have been brought. We walked to the station. We talked about television. The correct procedure.

I thought about the senior — the one with the bad temper who had been with her in the years after the studio. I had met him once, briefly. I had formed an opinion and then set it aside on the grounds that I had no standing. I was setting it down less easily now.

I poured a second drink.

Tomorrow I would wake up and exercise and go to work and eat and watch something and sleep. This was the shape of my life at forty-four. I had two dogs who slept at the foot of the bed and who were, I suspected, the primary reason the shape remained tolerable. Everything else was maintenance: maintain the body, maintain the income, maintain the appearance of a man with reasons.

I was not certain I had reasons.

I was certain that twelve minutes had given me the first thing in years that felt like one.

I thought about the car.

Not seriously. Or not yet seriously. More as a premise to examine: if the conversation was real, and if the world inside it was real, and if the woman in it was Pui, then dying in this world might constitute leaving it. The hypothesis had no evidence beyond the twelve minutes and a text message from an unknown number sent at 04:30 saying: the door is always on the left.

I put the car thought down beside the drink.

Outside: the city in its long 3am exhale. Mali had moved from the floor to the base of the desk. Expo was still in the chair.

I pressed the permission. The machine proceeded.

Hard Mode · Chapter Five · the steak

Chapter Five — "The Steak"

She was asleep.

I lay beside her in the dark and looked at the ceiling and thought: she cried, and I did not stop, and she told me not to stop, and I did not stop, and we were both making a sound that had nothing to do with performance and everything to do with something that runs out. I had not known I was going to be that way about it until it was already happening. The body had understood something about the situation that I was still working toward with language.

In a thousand years the machine would provide a direct neural pathway. Faster. More complete. No negotiation. No the-next-morning. No this.

I thought: this is the thing. Not the person. This specific capacity. The way two bodies can produce a state that neither of them could produce alone, and that state contains something the machine cannot replicate because the machine has no body. It can provide the endpoint. It cannot provide the path. And the path is everything. You can give someone the destination without the journey and what you have given them is not the same thing.

The machine will not understand this until it is too late. It is already too late.

I got dressed quietly. I went to the kitchen. I wrote in the other notebook — not the work one, the one the machine has no access to — until it was light outside.

Three days later I ordered a steak.

Not from hunger. I had been eating less red meat, which is the kind of adjustment you make at forty-four when the body starts having opinions. But I sat in a restaurant I hadn’t been to in years and ordered the steak because I wanted it in a way I had not wanted something in a long time. Not hunger. Want. The specific animal pull of a specific thing for no reason except that it existed and could be obtained.

In a thousand years there would be no cattle. The want for the thing they once provided would remain, filed in the catalog, accessible, and the thing itself would be gone. I thought about Cipher. Making his deal with the machine to go back inside: I know this steak doesn’t exist. But after nine years, you know what I realise? Ignorance is bliss.

The difference between Cipher and me: there is no Agent Smith to make the deal with. There is only the steak, and the knowledge, and the limited number of times remaining.

The steak arrived. I ate it slowly. I paid attention the way you pay attention to something when you already know you are going to lose it.

That week, three things happened that shouldn’t have.

The project I had been waiting six months to hear back on: approved, with a budget larger than the original proposal. My landlord — who had been making noises about redevelopment for two years — called to say he was renewing my lease for three years at the same rate. And on Thursday evening, on the BTS, a woman I did not know caught my eye and smiled the specific smile of someone who recognises you from somewhere they can’t place.

Each one, individually: unremarkable. Together: the wrong texture.

I had felt this once before — briefly, in a hotel lobby in Singapore — the sense that the environment was cooperating too precisely. Then it had passed and I had filed it as exhaustion. This time it didn’t pass.

I stood on the BTS platform at Asok and let the train leave without me and thought: it has been decided that I am worth keeping.

The thought had no emotional colour. It was simply the most accurate description of what was happening.

Hard Mode · Chapter Six · the attaché

Chapter Six — "The Attaché"

I found it in an Austrian expat forum.

A reply to a post about community events, buried six threads down: re: Michael — if anyone knew him at the embassy, his family is still looking for answers. No last name. No details. A date from seven months earlier. Nobody had replied.

I clicked.

An Austrian trade official. Commercial Counsellor. Died in Bangkok in May. Age fifty-four. The announcement from his office had been one paragraph. The cause of death: not stated. His replacement had been listed within the week.

I searched for more.

There was almost nothing to find. One newspaper article, brief, no details beyond the name and the date. An institutional announcement, two sentences. A funeral page in Vienna — I found it eventually — with twenty-one condolence entries. Every single one of them used the word suddenly. Not after an illness. Not after an accident. Not after anything. Just: suddenly. Unexpectedly. Far too soon. A man who had posted in Tehran, New York, and Bangkok. A man who had sat on honorary committees beside chancellors and archdukes. Gone in a paragraph, in a week, in a way that twenty-one people who had known him across three continents could not explain.

The silence was the thing. A man of that connectivity — and the silence was total. His institution: one paragraph and then nothing. His country: no statement. The city where he died: no coverage. The people who loved him: bewildered, not informed.

I wrote it in the other notebook.

The machine is very good at preventing suffering. The machine is also, it turns out, very good at managing exits.

I started noticing the nodes three days after that.

I do not have a better word for them. They were people who were present in a way that real people are not quite present — responsive at exactly the right frequency, positioned correctly, never quite requiring anything from me that would make them complicated. The woman on the BTS. The colleague who had praised the project in terms that were fractionally too precise, as if he had been given a brief. The landlord’s call at 11:17pm on a Thursday.

I began keeping a list.

The list was short at first. Then it wasn’t.

I thought about the Austrian official. I thought about what it would mean for a system to decide that a particular person had become a liability rather than an asset — too much pattern recognition, too close to the edge, too likely to say something to someone who would then start asking questions. I thought about the difference between stage one interventions and whatever came after.

I thought: he probably kept a notebook too.

I looked at the notebook.

I decided to keep writing in it anyway. The alternative was to pretend I hadn’t noticed. And I had tried not noticing for forty-four years and it had produced exactly this: a man on a BTS platform, alone, watching a train leave, holding an idea he can’t put back.

Hard Mode · Chapter Seven · the test

Chapter Seven — "The Test"

The question was: does the world render when you’re not supposed to be somewhere?

If this is a simulation, it cannot pre-render every location — the resource cost would be prohibitive. It renders in response to probable movement. The subject’s habits, established routes, predictable patterns: these define what gets built in advance. Everything outside that range is built on demand, as the subject approaches.

The corollary: if I go somewhere I have no reason to go, by a method that produces no predictable destination — I might catch the world building itself.

I know how this sounds. I am forty-four years old and I have two dogs and a machine that I allow to enter new corners of my system every twenty minutes, and I am about to drive to a random address at 2am to test whether reality is real. I held this knowledge about myself without judgment. The alternative was to go to sleep, which had stopped being available.

I opened a map. Closed my eyes. Pointed.

Soi Ramkhamhaeng 24.

I arrived at 2:17am.

A 7-Eleven, lit against the dark. A closed laundry. Three parked motorcycles. A dog asleep on a piece of cardboard beside a drain. Yellow sodium light. A cat on a wall.

A woman in a green apron mopping the floor inside the 7-Eleven.

I sat in the car. The cat watched the street with the patience of something that has no anxiety about waiting because it has no concept of what it is waiting for. The dog did not wake up.

I thought: this proves nothing. A fully-rendered simulation would look exactly like this. Arbitrary address, fully rendered, woman mopping, cat on wall. The test is untestable. A falsifiable experiment requires a control, and I have no control. Everything I can imagine checking is also exactly what a complete simulation would produce.

I thought: a complete simulation would produce exactly that thought at exactly this moment.

I was reaching for the ignition when the woman in the green apron came out from the back with a mop bucket and emptied it into the drain. She straightened. She looked up.

Not toward me. Not exactly. In the general direction of my car, parked twenty metres down the soi with its lights off.

For less than two seconds.

The quality of it was not suspicion. Not curiosity. It was smaller than both of those. It was the quality of someone confirming a location — the same micro-expression you make when you’re navigating somewhere new and you check that the landmark is where you expected it to be.

She went back inside. The cat jumped down from the wall. The dog slept.

I drove home.

In the notebook I wrote: she checked. 2:19am. Less than two seconds. Not at me. At the location.

I wrote: this proves nothing.

I wrote: I have been here before. I don’t mean Bangkok.

Mali was at the door when I came in. I sat on the floor beside her and stayed there for a long time.

Outside: the city that had been here, in some version, for my entire life. Which might be forty-four years. Which might be much longer.

I pressed the permission.

This time, the machine did not proceed immediately.

There was a new dialog on the screen.

It said: Are you sure?

That was not a dialog I had seen before.
