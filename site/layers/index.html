The Novel · Hard Mode

The invented layer. The same man, suspecting the forty-four years were a chosen simulation, in a future where the only way to test which world is real can kill you for good.

A man who has won everything a mind can win, and still does not know what any of it was for, sets out to find the one person who can tell him — in the only world where the roles come off.

Chapter One — “The Noiseless Kind”

Let me ask you something before we begin.

Have you ever been in a room that was exactly the right temperature — not warm, not cool, just the precise degree at which your body stops noticing itself — and felt, not comfort, but a specific, almost clinical dread?

That is where I am right now. My apartment. Bangkok — or what Bangkok became, which I will get to — on a Tuesday that is essentially indistinguishable from all the other Tuesdays that have been indistinguishable from each other for longer than I can comfortably remember.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us, as algorithms do, finding in the topology of our combined data some constellation of compatibility I cannot quite locate in the room when she is in it. She is — and I say this without cruelty, only precision — exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex, the kind that is technically correct in every parameter and delivers, for both of us, something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times now. The fourth will probably be the last, not because anything goes wrong, but because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

When I was forty-four years old — in the life I want to tell you about — I read somewhere that Camus opened his most famous essay by saying there is only one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide. He was right, though he was asking the wrong version of the question. The interesting version is not whether to continue but which layer to continue in — and the reason nobody asks that version is that, for most of human history, there was only one layer to be in. You were born, you suffered, you died. The options were stark but uncomplicated.

I am going to tell you a story that starts forty-four years into one life and ends, maybe, at the beginning of another. I should warn you: I am Thai, I am forty-four, I am tired, I am not dead, and I have recently come to suspect that none of those facts mean what I thought they meant. Also — (pun not intended) — I may be inside a simulation. But let me get to that in a moment.

The message came on a Tuesday. Her name was Pui. She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, in an architecture school so far out of Bangkok that you took the train to Ladkrabang and then another from there, and I did that for exactly one week before my father rented me an apartment near campus. She was one of maybe eight women in our entire cohort — architecture schools in that part of Bangkok, late nineties, were not known for their gender balance — and she was funny, and she knew exactly what she was doing in a crit. We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been closer if we had been anywhere else, or had tried a little harder, or had been a little less afraid.

I had been writing on her Facebook wall every year on her birthday for four years. A few words. You do this for people you were not quite close enough to call, which is most people, eventually. That Tuesday I wrote: “Twenty-five years since Ladkrabang. Hope you're well.” Within two hours, a message came from Kamashit, who had been close to her, who I had not spoken to in longer than I can reconstruct:

Non. You didn't know. She died. Last year. Cancer.

I scrolled. The way you do.

There were the posts from friends in Thai that accumulate on a dead person's wall and never quite know what tone to take. Photographs from a ceremony I did not recognize. A date I will not give you, because it is hers.

And then, two weeks before all of that, her last post.

It was not what you would write if you knew you were dying. It was too calm for that, too specific, too much like something she had been thinking about for a long time and had decided, in that particular window, to write down. The Thai was careful. Clear. I will translate it for you, roughly:

”To whoever reads this first after I'm gone — don't waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you'll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P.”

I read it three times. Then I put my phone face-down on the table and looked at the wall — the exact-right-temperature air, the date in an hour, the city outside that had stopped surprising me — and I felt something I had not felt in years. I felt the hair on my arms.

Because here is the thing I am not going to explain to you tonight, the thing that sat in my chest like a key finding a lock it did not know it had been cut for.

I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in any way I could have told you, even a minute before reading her last post. But in my body, in the specific, subterranean way you know a place you have been to in a dream so vivid it left a residue — I already knew. Left, not right. Ask before the memory goes.

The date arrived at seven and rang the buzzer and I let her up. We had a drink. We had sex — the technically correct kind. She fell asleep before ten. I lay on my back in the exact-right-temperature dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: How many times have I done this before?

Not the sex.

The whole thing.

— chapter one of Hard Mode · the novel being written across one hundred days
