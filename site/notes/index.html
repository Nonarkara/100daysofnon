Expo 2020 Dubai: An Ultimate Guide
12 annotations · grammar-edited · original 2,744 words
EDITOR'S NOTE — This post makes the most factual claims of any in the corpus. The World's Fair "invention mythology" is a well-documented category of popular misinformation. The author appears to have absorbed it wholesale, with confidence.

Every five years,1 nations gather in one location for a six-month event. The host city invites these nations to build "pavilions" — physical structures housing exhibitions that showcase their unique qualities. This gathering is massive. To give you a sense of its scale, the previous two events drew nearly 100 million visitors combined.2

Among the many names it is given, "World's Fair" and "International Exhibition" stand out, as does its official name "Expo" — an abbreviation for "exposition." There has never been a single event where global firms, individuals, and countries have been more present than the Expo.3 The prefix "World" is sometimes used to distinguish between the main event, which occurs every five years and lasts six months, and "Specialized Expos," which last three months and occur more frequently.

It is where inventors get a sneak peek at what may become the latest technology affecting the world. For example, Expos were the first to debut technological innovations such as fax machines, telephones, mobile phones, X-ray machines, and flatscreen televisions, to name a few.4 Food entrepreneurs look back on Expos where the hamburger, ice cream, hot dogs, peanut butter, iced tea, and ketchup were first introduced to the world.5

For architects, Expo is a "mecca of new ideas." Many inventive architectural achievements — the Eiffel Tower,6 the Atomium, the Space Needle, capsule hotels,7 and the geodesic dome8 — used the Expo to gauge public interest.

This year's Expo takes place in Dubai, United Arab Emirates. Though the global pandemic pushed the event back a year to 2021, organisers kept its original name, "Expo 2020 Dubai." It represents the first time that the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia have held a major event of this scale.9

Back in November 2013, Dubai won the bid10 with the theme "Connecting Minds, Creating the Future." Nations compete to host the Expo, much like the Olympic Games. Apart from the prestige of hosting the world's most important gathering, Expo is a great opportunity to create "stories" about an emerging city and economy.

Looking at an overhead view of this massive 4.38-square-kilometre Expo site in Dubai, the design represents three characteristics of modern UAE: speed, scale, and seriousness. The site could easily be mistaken for a vast artificial city built on vision, wealth, and power.

Dubai made sure that preventative measures were strict and effective. Even with a ticket, every visitor was required to present a vaccination certificate. Expo staff from any nation received free vaccinations and tests throughout the event should they suspect exposure.

Based on our conversations with organisers, staff, and visitors, there are three reasons why nations participate in these Expos. For starters, nothing beats a face-to-face meeting, especially for investors and entrepreneurs looking to measure themselves against their rivals — the so-called "Expo Magic." Even after 170 years,11 Expo still serves as both a platform and an excuse for a physical encounter.

According to World Expo historian Charles Pappas, the Expo's nearly two centuries of history can be summarised as the "3Ps": Products, Progress, and Preparation. The idea of a "great exhibition" was first conceived in the mid-nineteenth century when entrepreneurs held a large event in London to display their competitive products. Two catastrophic world wars prompted the official organising body — the Bureau International des Expositions (BIE) — to shift the focus from products to the promotion of "mutual progress."

An aerial view of Expo '70 in Osaka, Japan, where mobile telecommunications were introduced to the world for the first time.12

Expos have created compelling stories for host cities. Many cities in recent memory have made their mark on the global landscape after hosting the Expo: Seattle (1962), Montreal (1967), Osaka (1970), Seville (1992), and Shanghai (2010). Unlike previous Expos, where pavilions and most buildings were erected only for the duration of the proceedings, Expo 2020 plans to keep at least two-thirds of the buildings and transform the site into "District 2020" — a world-class innovation hub for startups and global players in advanced technology.

The expansive site is divided into three main districts named after the event's sub-themes: Mobility, Opportunity, and Sustainability. These three districts fan out from a central "palm": the world's largest single-span dome, dubbed "Al Wasl Plaza." "Al Wasl" is an intentional pun — it means "connecting" and is also the historical name for Dubai, alluding to both the Expo's theme and the host city.

Each of the three districts has a "signature" pavilion designed by world-famous architects. The UAE Pavilion was designed by Santiago Calatrava. Grimshaw Architects from the United Kingdom designed the "Terra" sustainability pavilion, a 130-metre-wide structure covered in 1,055 solar panels. Foster + Partners designed the Mobility Pavilion "Alif," whose characteristic stainless-steel fins are unmistakable. The Netherlands' "Biotope" pavilion functions as a complete ecosystem, using solar power to harvest water from the air and produce plant-based food. Singapore's "City in Nature" pavilion, designed by the renowned WOHA, aims for net-zero building architecture packed with plants and fans powered by clean energy.

12 ANNOTATIONS — EXPO 2020 DUBAI
1
OVERSIMPLIFIED
"Every five years, nations gather…"
The five-year cycle was codified only after the 1928 Paris Convention on International Exhibitions established the Bureau International des Expositions. Before that, the early fairs ran on no fixed schedule: 1851, 1862, 1867, 1873, 1876, 1878, 1889, 1893, 1900 — that is nine fairs in 49 years, unevenly spaced. "Every five years" describes the post-1928 era, not the institution's full history. The sentence is true today; it was not always true.
2
VERIFIED (barely)
"the previous two events drew nearly 100 million visitors combined"
Expo 2015 Milan: 21.5 million official visitors. Expo 2010 Shanghai: 73.1 million. Combined: 94.6 million. "Nearly 100 million" requires generous rounding, but the sum is in the right neighbourhood. Worth noting: Shanghai's 73 million figure has been disputed by analysts who observed that multi-day passes could be scanned multiple times, inflating the headline count. The real unique-visitor figure may be significantly lower.
3
CONTRADICTED
"There has never been a single event where global firms, individuals and countries have been more present than the Expo."
The Olympic Games would like a word. The 2020 Tokyo Olympics featured 206 National Olympic Committees — more than the 192 countries at Expo 2020 Dubai. The FIFA World Cup 2018 attracted an estimated 3.5 billion television viewers across its run, dwarfing any Expo broadcast reach. The UN General Assembly has 193 member-state delegations convening annually. The statement is defensible only under a very specific reading: "most countries simultaneously occupying bespoke physical structures in a single location." Even that criterion has never been tested against the data.
4
PARTIALLY TRUE
"fax machines, telephones and mobile phones, X-Ray machines, and flatscreen televisions"
Telephone ✓ — Alexander Graham Bell demonstrated his telephone at the 1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exposition. Emperor Dom Pedro II of Brazil reportedly dropped it in astonishment. Mobile phones ✓ — NTT and Bell Labs demonstrated early mobile phone prototypes at Expo '70 in Osaka. Fax machine ✗ — Giovanni Caselli's pantelegraph was in commercial operation between Paris and Lyon from 1865, pre-dating the major Expo era (though it was exhibited at the 1867 Paris Expo). X-ray machine ✗ — Wilhelm Röntgen discovered X-rays in his laboratory in November 1895 and published his findings directly; they were not debuted at a World's Fair. Flatscreen television ✗ — Large flatscreen displays were unveiled at CES (the Consumer Electronics Show), not Expos. The sentence is true for two of the five examples. Three are false or misleading.
5
CONTRADICTED
"the hamburger, ice cream, hot dogs, peanut butter, iced tea, and ketchup were first introduced to the world"
These are the "World's Fair food invention" myths — almost all sourced to the 1904 St. Louis World's Fair and almost all false. Hamburger: The Delmonico's restaurant menu of 1837 lists "Hamburg steak." By the 1880s, Hamburg-style patties were common in American cookbooks. Ice cream cone: The wafer cone was likely popularised at 1904 St. Louis, but the cone itself was patented earlier and multiple vendors claim the same invention simultaneously. Ice cream is far older. Hot dogs: Frankfurters arrived in America with German immigrants in the 1860s. Peanut butter: Patented in Canada by Marcellus Edson in 1884; a process patent held by John Harvey Kellogg from 1895 — nine years before St. Louis. Iced tea: Recipes appear in American household cookbooks from the 1870s. Ketchup: Tomato ketchup recipes exist in American recipe books from 1812; the condiment's roots trace to 17th-century Southeast Asian fish sauces. The 1904 Fair popularised several of these foods for American middle-class consumers. Popularisation is not invention.
6
VERIFIED
"the Eiffel Tower"
The Eiffel Tower was constructed as the entrance arch for the 1889 Exposition Universelle in Paris, commemorating the centennial of the French Revolution. Gustave Eiffel's company won the design competition in 1886. At the time, it was savaged by French artists — Guy de Maupassant called it "a gigantic black factory chimney." The 300-metre tower was the world's tallest structure from 1889 until the Chrysler Building surpassed it in 1930. Originally intended for demolition after 20 years, it was saved by its usefulness as a radio transmission tower. ✓
7
CONTRADICTED
"capsule hotels"
The first capsule hotel — Capsule Inn Osaka — opened in 1979, designed by Kisho Kurokawa. It did not debut at an Expo. The Metabolism architectural movement, which inspired the capsule concept, did have a famous presence at Expo '70: Kurokawa's Takara Beautilion was a landmark structure of the fair, built from stainless steel capsule components. But the commercial capsule hotel came a full nine years after Expo '70. Kurokawa used the Expo to exhibit an architectural philosophy, not a hotel product. The hotel followed from the philosophy — not from the fair.
8
CONTEXT
"the geodesic dome"
Buckminster Fuller built and patented geodesic dome structures from 1948 onwards. By 1954, the US Marine Corps had ordered 300 geodesic domes for field deployment. The dome most associated with Expos was Fuller's US Pavilion at Expo '67 in Montreal — a 76-metre sphere that became an iconic image of the age. But by then, geodesic domes had already been standing on American soil for nearly 20 years. The Expo gave the geodesic dome its most celebrated platform; it did not introduce the concept.
9
OVERSIMPLIFIED
"the first time that the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia have held a major event of this scale"
This collapses three regions into one claim. Africa hosted the 2010 FIFA World Cup in South Africa — by television audience, the largest sporting event the continent had hosted. South Asia: India hosted the 1982 Asian Games in New Delhi and the 2010 Commonwealth Games. Middle East: The claim holds more tightly here — no prior World Expo or Olympics, though Dubai had hosted World Expo for the first time. The sentence is true for the specific event-type (World Expo), but "major event of this scale" is broad enough to be false across all three regions simultaneously.
10
VERIFIED
"Back in November 2013, Dubai won the bidding"
Dubai was selected on November 27, 2013, at the 154th General Assembly of the Bureau International des Expositions in Paris. Dubai defeated three other candidates: Ekaterinburg (Russia), Izmir (Turkey), and São Paulo (Brazil). In the final round, Dubai received 116 votes against Ekaterinburg's 47. ✓
11
VERIFIED
"Even after 170 years"
The first World's Fair — the Great Exhibition of the Works of Industry of All Nations — opened in Hyde Park, London, on May 1, 1851. Expo 2020 Dubai ran from October 2021. That is 170 years. The Crystal Palace that housed the exhibition was an extraordinary feat of engineering: 563 metres long, built from prefabricated cast-iron and plate glass. It housed 14,000 exhibitors and attracted 6 million visitors — roughly one-third of Britain's entire population at the time. ✓
12
OVERSIMPLIFIED
"Osaka, Japan, where mobile telecommunications were introduced to the world for the first time"
NTT and Bell Labs did demonstrate early mobile phone and videophone prototypes at Expo '70 in Osaka — a legitimate point. However, "introduced to the world for the first time" overstates it considerably. The concept of mobile radio telephony had been under development through the 1940s–60s. The first practical handheld mobile phone call was made by Martin Cooper of Motorola on April 3, 1973 — three years after Expo '70. What Osaka showed was a demonstration model in a controlled exhibition environment, not a product entering the world. The demonstration and the introduction are not the same event.
Day 21 · 23 July 2025 · Shanghai
Cicero, On Aging
11 annotations · grammar-edited · original 3,376 words
EDITOR'S NOTE — The author's reading of De Senectute is genuine and close. Where the text becomes unreliable is in three places: a dramatised "quote" of Cicero that is actually Dr Non; a goldfish-memory claim that is among the most thoroughly debunked myths in pop science; and a claim about human cell renewal that requires significant qualification. The Confucius and wabi-sabi sections are handled with care.

"I find four reasons why old age appears to be unhappy: first, that it withdraws us from active pursuits; second, that it makes the body weaker; third, that it deprives us of almost all physical pleasures; and, fourth, that it is not far removed from death. Let us, if you please, examine each of these reasons separately and see how much truth they contain."1
— Marcus Tullius Cicero2 (106–43 BC) in De Senectute (On Old Age), written in 44 BC3

I begin this post with this timeless wisdom of Cicero, who — despite this pessimistic opening of his little book called De Senectute (originally translated as On Old Age, although the newest translation by Princeton University Press has retitled it How to Grow Old,4 which is a fair enough title) — would, in fact, come around to debunk the hollowness of getting old. He would, at the end of the book, remind us of the charm of ageing.

One of which — spoiler alert — is that we would be free from the desire to engage in sexual activities. While many of Cicero's fellow Romans thought of ageing as the embarrassing end of manhood, Cicero thought of it as a gift.

"Alas," said the great man Cicero, "I could be free from the naturalistic instinct that has for many years chained me to unproductive anxiety — and now I can be free from it to do more important things, say, thinking and writing."5 That is just one of his "three golden teachings," which resonates with me most deeply: when can I actually be free from the natural impulse to want to have sex?!?! Damn it!

We will come back to the other two teachings Cicero has given us from more than two millennia ago. Cicero himself has been known more as a statesman6 — the master of political thinking who inspired generations of philosophers, especially during the Enlightenment — and less as a philosopher. But he was a philosopher.

His thinking on ageing is almost the same as that of Confucius, who famously wrote what may be the world's shortest autobiography:

"At 15, I set my heart on learning. At 30, I know where I stand. At 40, I have no more doubts. At 50, I know the will of Heaven. At 60, my ears are attuned. At 70, I follow my heart's desire without crossing the line."7
— Confucius, as recorded by his disciples8 in the Analects

So, I am going to weave my reading of Cicero with my own take on the morality of ageing. It will be hard for me to pinpoint which is my own thought and which is his, as I think my original take on ageing is quite similar to his already. The reason for this is that I have seen my father age and watched how he coped with it — both elegantly and clumsily — and I have derived my understanding of ageing from that direct, intimate, and painful experience of living, for 28 years, with the man I loved most.

I once wondered whether my goldfish would be bored swimming in a small bowl his entire life, until my veterinarian friend told me that goldfish have very short memories — like, very, very short9 — so they would never get bored, because they would be excited by everything they encountered again and again. At the moment I thought: wouldn't it be nice if we were all like that?

Our body changes every day. From a physiological perspective, our cells renew themselves,10 allowing our body to be reshaped into a slightly different form. In this sense, both the Buddha and David Hume were right to say that there is no self.11 The more we try to find the essence of the self, the more we find nothing — since even the very basic physiological underpinning of selfhood is never in a static state we can pin down.

Ageing, to me, is an exciting process because we live in a different body every day, and this different body allows us to experience new things, even when the experience itself may not seem so different from what we have known before. We are like the goldfish — we actually never get to read the same book twice.

Aging therefore gives me a sense of constant renewal of the agents that dictate my experience of the physical world and therefore my sensibility. This is precisely the reason why I do not think getting old is a fearful thing. Every day when I get up in the morning, I feel thankful for another day in a life.

Here is when the second teaching of Cicero may add some depth to my argument. He said that getting old — and looking old — reminds us of our responsibility to give attention to something beyond just the body. A much greater care is due to the mind and soul. It is the time when how you look may no longer attract the wrong kinds of people into your life. Your attraction and contribution will solely be your mind and soul, and that, though it may not be immediately appealing, is what Cicero believes to be the truest reward one can receive in life.

When Confucius said that "at 70, I follow my heart's desire without crossing the line," what he meant was that he had arrived at the stage where he could truly be himself. Life, to him, was never about quantity but quality.

The first time I heard about the ancient Japanese philosophy called wabi-sabi,12 which is often described as finding beauty in the imperfect, impermanent, and incomplete, I thought: that is it — that is my philosophy. I was 18 at the time. The moment I discovered wabi-sabi, I became a changed man. This philosophy appreciates the marks that time, space, experience, and weather leave behind.

The third teaching of Cicero about ageing is that age is not a barrier to remaining engaged with life. Being free from the burden of looking good and being "sexually active" just to fulfil a bodily desire you cannot control — we will have more time to be intellectually, physically, and socially engaged with a community of true friends.

I am turning 34 in a few days, and I want to live as long as I can in a healthy body. I always think that people who are obsessed with how they look are people who have never lost someone they truly love. When you have lost someone that important to you, you change — you become much more sensitive to what really matters, and less concerned with superficial things. My father was only in his early 60s when he passed away. I, at least, want to live as long as he did, and have a life as meaningful as his.

11 ANNOTATIONS — CICERO, ON AGING
1
VERIFIED
The four reasons why old age appears unhappy
This is the actual opening of De Senectute. The four reasons — withdrawal from active pursuits, physical weakness, deprivation of pleasures, proximity to death — are enumerated in the very first substantive section of the dialogue. Cicero then spends the rest of the book systematically rebutting each one. The translation here is close to the standard W.A. Falconer (Loeb Classical Library) rendering. ✓
2
VERIFIED
"Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC)"
Cicero was born on January 3, 106 BC in Arpinum (now Arpino), a hill town in Latium. He was murdered on December 7, 43 BC on orders of Mark Antony, during the proscriptions following Caesar's assassination. He was 63 years old — precisely the age at which he was writing about the dignified acceptance of death. The poignancy is not coincidental. His severed head and right hand were displayed on the Rostra in the Roman Forum, as punishment for the speeches he had delivered against Antony. ✓
3
VERIFIED
"De Senectute written in 44 BC"
Cicero wrote De Senectute in 44 BC, during one of the most turbulent years of his life. Julius Caesar had just been assassinated on March 15 of that year. The Republic was unravelling. Cicero was 62 and effectively in political exile from Rome. The book's philosophical serenity — written at a moment of genuine personal and political crisis — is either the product of deep wisdom or a performance of it. Possibly both. ✓
4
VERIFIED (with a note)
"Princeton University Press has retitled it How to Grow Old"
Philip Freeman's 2016 Princeton University Press translation is titled How to Grow Old: Ancient Wisdom for the Second Half of Life. Freeman is a classicist at Luther College; his edition includes the original Latin facing the English. The "retitled" framing is slightly imprecise: How to Grow Old is a new translator's interpretive title for a new edition, not a renaming of the original. De Senectute remains De Senectute, and "On Old Age" remains a valid translation. ✓
5
DISPUTED
"Alas," said the great man Cicero, "I could be free from the naturalistic instinct…"
This is not a Cicero quote. It is a dramatisation — a plausible reconstruction of Cicero's emotional register, written in Dr Non's own voice. De Senectute does contain Cicero's endorsement of Sophocles' famous remark about being freed from "a mad and cruel master" in old age (Section XIV, §47). Cicero writes approvingly of this. But Cicero's actual words are philosophical and measured, not exclamatory. The "Alas" is Dr Non's; the sentiment is Cicero's. The distinction matters: this post is about fact-checking, and the author has quoted himself as Cicero.
6
GRAMMAR
"Cicero himself has been known more as a statement"
The original blog text says "statement" where the word is clearly "statesman." Cicero served as consul in 63 BC — the highest elected office of the Roman Republic — during which he crushed the Catilinarian conspiracy and earned the honorific pater patriae ("father of the fatherland"). As a statesman, yes. As a proofreader, Dr Non could apply the same rigour he directs at others' facts to his own text.
7
VERIFIED (translation note)
Confucius autobiography, Analects 2:4
The passage is genuine and from Analects 2:4. D.C. Lau's Penguin Classics translation (1979): "At fifteen I set my heart on learning; at thirty I took my stand; at forty I had no doubts; at fifty I understood the will of Heaven; at sixty my ear was attuned; at seventy I follow my heart's desire without overstepping the mark." Dr Non's version is closest to Lau. "I know where I stand" (vs Lau's "I took my stand") is a subtle but defensible variant. "Without crossing the line" vs "without overstepping the mark" is semantically identical. ✓
8
GRAMMAR
"recorded by his deciples"
The original text reads "deciples". The word is "disciples." Corrected silently in the text above. Two grammar corrections in one post suggests the author was writing fast. Cicero was writing in the middle of a civil war, which may excuse him. Dr Non was writing in Shanghai, which may not.
9
CONTRADICTED
"goldfish have very short memory (like, very very short)"
The "three-second goldfish memory" is one of the most durable myths in popular science — and one of the most thoroughly disproved. A 2003 study by Plymouth University trained goldfish to press levers for food; the fish retained the skill for months. A widely cited 2008 experiment by Australian student Rory Stokes showed goldfish learned a maze and remembered it for over a month. Goldfish can form associative memories, recognise their feeders, and anticipate feeding times based on environmental cues. The three-second figure appears to have been invented, as myths often are, from nothing in particular. The veterinarian friend was wrong. The goldfish, if it could respond, would remember this annotation.
10
OVERSIMPLIFIED
"all the cells renew themselves, allowing our body to be reshaped into a slightly different form"
Different cell types have radically different lifespans. Skin cells: ~2–3 weeks. Red blood cells: ~120 days. Liver cells: ~12–18 months. Cardiac muscle cells: approximately 1% per year — meaning roughly 40% of your heart's muscle cells from birth will still be present when you die. Neurons in the cerebral cortex: essentially a lifetime. You are, in a very real sense, still running the original hardware. Lens cells of the eye never regenerate at all. The commonly cited "your body completely renews itself every 7 years" is a myth; the truth is that the body is a patchwork of timelines ranging from weeks to a lifetime. The argument for impermanence is philosophically valid — the cellular evidence for it is much more complicated than this paragraph implies.
11
CONTEXT
"both the Buddha and David Hume were right to say that there is no self"
Both did argue against a fixed, permanent self — but from such different premises that the comparison is philosophically loaded. Buddha's anattā (non-self) doctrine, as articulated in the Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta, holds that what we call a "self" is a collection of five aggregates (form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness) with no underlying soul. Hume's bundle theory, from A Treatise of Human Nature (1739), holds that the self is merely a bundle of perceptions — there is no owner behind the impressions. They reach similar conclusions by very different routes. Modern cognitive neuroscience — particularly Thomas Metzinger's Being No One (2003) — largely supports both. ✓ as a synthesis, though Hume would likely have resisted the metaphysical framing, and the Buddha the empiricist one.
12
CONTEXT
"wabi-sabi, which literally means 'imperfect, impermanent, and incomplete'"
"Imperfect, impermanent, and incomplete" is Leonard Koren's formulation from his 1994 book Wabi-Sabi for Artists, Designers, Poets & Philosophers — an interpretation, not a literal translation. Literally: wabi (侘) originally described the desolation of solitude in nature, evolving to mean rustic simplicity and unforced beauty. Sabi (寂) describes the quiet beauty that comes with age, weathering, and use. Together they describe an aesthetic sensibility rather than a doctrine — there is no wabi-sabi text, no founding philosopher. Koren's triad became so dominant in Western design culture that it is now routinely cited as the definition. Dr Non adopted the interpretation and described it as etymology. The edit above changes "literally means" to "is often described as." That is the appropriate register. The philosophy remains exactly as beautiful.
