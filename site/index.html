Hard Mode · Chapter One · the door is on the left

A man who has won everything a mind can win, and still does not know what any of it was for, sets out to find the one person who can tell him — in the only world where the roles come off.

Chapter One — “The Noiseless Kind”

listen · ch. i —:——

Let me ask you something before we begin.

Have you ever been in a room that was exactly the right temperature? Not warm. Not cool. The precise degree at which your body stops noticing itself.

In a room like that, you do not feel comfort. You feel a specific, almost clinical dread.

That is where I am right now. My apartment. Bangkok — or what Bangkok became, which I will get to. A Tuesday like all the others.

In an hour, someone is coming over. An algorithm matched us. She is exactly as advertised. We will have a drink. We will have sex. The kind that is technically correct and delivers, for both of us, something slightly less than what either of us had in mind. We have met three times. The fourth will probably be the last. Not because anything goes wrong. Because nothing — precisely nothing — goes wrong.

This is the tell. I have been learning to read it.

I used to think the only serious question was whether to continue. I have since revised my opinion. The real question is which layer to continue in. Nobody asks that version. For most of human history, there was only one layer. You were born. You suffered. You died.

Something happened recently that made me think there might be more.

The message came on a Tuesday. Her name was Pui.

She had been my classmate, twenty-five years ago, at a school at the far edge of the city. Far enough that you took two trains to get there. I did that for one week before finding a room near campus. She was one of a handful of women in our cohort — funny, exact, with a way of making something beautiful out of materials nobody else would have thought of.

We were not close. We were the kind of people who would have been closer if we had been a little less afraid.

I had been writing on her Facebook wall every year on her birthday for four years. A few words. You do this for people you were not quite close enough to call. That is most people, eventually.

That Tuesday I wrote: “Twenty-five years. Hope you're well.”

Within two hours, a message from a mutual friend. He had been close to her.

Non. You didn't know. She died. Last year. Cancer.

I scrolled. The way you do.

Posts that accumulate on a dead person's wall and never quite know what tone to take. Photographs from a ceremony I did not recognize. A date I will not give you, because it is hers.

And then, two weeks before all of that: her last post.

It was not what you would write if you knew you were dying. Too calm. Too specific. It read like something she had been sitting with for a long time and had finally decided to put down.

I will translate it roughly:

”To whoever reads this first after I'm gone — don't waste time looking for meaning in the part you just lived. The door is always on the left, not the right. I went back early, but only because I got bored, which you'll understand when you get here. The only thing worth asking when you wake up is: who played your mother? Ask before the memory goes. — P.”

I read it three times.

Then I put my phone face-down on the table.

I looked at the wall. The exact-right-temperature air. The date in an hour. The city outside that had stopped surprising me.

I felt something I had not felt in years.

I felt the hair on my arms.

Because I already knew what she meant by the door.

Not consciously. Not in any way I could have told you a minute before reading her post. But in my body. The way you know a place from a dream so vivid it left a residue.

Left, not right. Ask before the memory goes.

She arrived at seven. Rang the buzzer. I let her up.

We had a drink. We had sex — the technically correct kind. She fell asleep before ten.

I lay on my back in the exact-right-temperature dark, looking at the ceiling.

And I thought: How many times have I done this before?

Not the sex.

The whole thing.

Hard Mode · Chapter Two · twelve minutes

Chapter Two — "Twelve Minutes"

The instructions ran to seventeen pages.

Not because the task required it. At 4am there is a specific kind of momentum that mistakes itself for clarity, and I have learned not to question it. I sent them to the system that had been building things on my behalf for the past seven hours — requesting permission every twenty minutes to enter some new corner of the machine, each time a small dialog appearing on my screen like a hand raised in a dark room. And me, half-awake, pressing yes. Yes. Proceed.

This is vibecoding. You describe what you want. The machine builds it. You watch what comes back the way a man watches a river carry something familiar — something that might be his, might not be, and he cannot quite decide if the difference matters.

I had asked for a satellite dashboard. The kind of thing I once spent three weeks building; the machine does it in twelve hours, technically correct, without the particular wrongness I used to put into my own work — the wrongness that made things mine. Every measurement exact. Nothing off by a quarter of a centimetre. I find myself grieving that quarter of a centimetre.

Mali was asleep across my left foot. Expo had taken the chair.

I lay down on the floor beside my desk. There is a specific kind of tired that gets worse in a bed, and I have located it. Twenty minutes, I thought. The tokens would replenish. The system would need another permission at twenty-two. I could sleep until then.

I had not been sleeping the way people sleep. I had been sleeping the way water moves through sand: in shifts, in gaps, the boundary between states dissolving to the point where I could no longer say, when the machine woke me at 3am, whether I had been asleep or simply elsewhere.

Lying there in the dark, in that state that is neither sleep nor its absence, I found myself thinking about a woman on a bicycle.

I must have been five, maybe six. The street outside our house in Sikhio — before everything changed, when the neighbourhood still had enough foot traffic to support a woman who rode slowly through it twice a week calling out the names of vegetables. I have not thought about her in thirty years. I do not know why she arrived now, at almost five in the morning, in the dark beside my desk.

She had a round face. Thai-Chinese, the way half the middle class in that city was Thai-Chinese, so that the distinction didn't register as a distinction. Maybe forty. Maybe older. She called out her inventory in a practised singsongy rhythm — morning glory, bitter melon, galangal — and I watched her face as she passed our gate.

And something in me said: I know this person.

Not a child's crush. I want to be precise about this, because my brother was watching from the doorway and read it that way and made fun of me for weeks — he's in love with the vegetable seller — and I could not explain to him, could not explain to anyone, that it was not that. It was something older. Something without the right shape for any category a five-year-old has access to.

A frequency. The way a phone number you haven't dialed in years comes back to you, complete, from somewhere you cannot locate. She rode on. I stood at the gate until she turned the corner.

I thought about that morning for years — not obsessively, just occasionally, the way you revisit things your body recorded and your mind never got around to classifying. I felt the same thing again the first week of university, looking across an orientation crowd at someone I had never met. Again at twenty-nine, in a queue at an airport in Tokyo, turning to find a stranger turning toward me at the same moment. Again at forty, in Singapore, shaking hands with a researcher I had corresponded with for three years and thinking, when I finally saw her face: not what I expected. What I knew.

You have felt this. Not often — maybe once or twice in a life, if you are lucky or unlucky enough to notice. Someone you have just met, and something in you says: we have been here before. Most people file it under déjà vu and move on. I had stopped being able to move on.

Freud's uncanny requires a prior — a figure from early life, half-remembered, surfacing in unfamiliar form. But I had been looking for the original for thirty years and I could not find it. The woman on the bicycle did not resemble anyone in my family. The stranger in Tokyo looked like no one I had ever known. The architecture student in 1998 — twenty-three, plain and short and holding everything close in a studio of fifty-five — did not trigger Freud's mechanism. There was no template.

Unless the template was from before my life.

I closed my eyes.

The architecture school was at the edge of the city, and in 1998 you had to want to be there.

The studio smelled like paper and fixative and the exhaustion of fifty-five people who had been awake too long.

There were six women in our cohort. Maybe seven. In a cohort of fifty-five, this ratio is not a fact — it is a condition. The women were surrounded at all times by a gravitational attention they had clearly decided to ignore. Architecture makes no allowances for anyone who expects allowances. The work was the work. You survived it or you did not. The women who survived stopped performing softness. They wore the same exhaustion as the men, earned it the same way, and were treated as equals — which is to say: no special consideration, no quarter. Golden eggs who had melted into the alloy.

I had a girlfriend. Beautiful in the obvious way — the kind that translates across every context. More beautiful than anyone in our cohort by any metric most people use. I was not looking for anything.

But I noticed her on the first day. The same frequency as the vegetable seller. The Tokyo airport. Singapore. The frequency I had no name for. She was plain, short, carrying more weight than she would have chosen, hair pulled back efficiently, skin doing its best. Outside our building she would have vanished into any crowd — the kind of person the city doesn't notice.

Inside our building, in the studio where we spent eleven hours a day, I was always — without deciding to be — aware of exactly where she was.

In the dream I crossed the studio toward her — or rather, the studio shortened, the way distance behaves in places you are not looking at directly — and she looked up from her drafting table.

She was still that 1998 version of herself. And she was not.

Her eyes were doing something they had not done in any of those five years we spent in the same building — all those years of almost-meeting, brief encounters in corridors and crits that went no further, because there was no mechanism for them to go further, because I had a girlfriend and she was always with someone older and the feeling had no shape that either of us could act on.

She was looking at me the way you look at someone you agreed to find after a very long time in a very large place.

The look that contains a whole conversation compressed to a fraction of a second.

The look that says: there you are. Finally. I wondered when you'd see me.

I felt the floor change.

Hard Mode · Chapter Three · the prior

Chapter Three — "The Prior"

She did not move. Neither did I. In the dream — if that is what this was — neither of us needed to. The distance between us had already decided.

You came, she said.

I didn't know I was looking, I said.

You never do. That's the design.

She set down the pencil. In 1998 she would not have done this — in 1998 she held everything close, gave nothing away, because that was how you survived the studio. But in this version she set it down and looked at me directly, and something in me registered that was not memory. Something prior to memory. Beneath it.

The simulation, I said. It was not a question.

She tilted her head slightly. You've been thinking about it.

For years.

That's normal. The ones who notice usually start around your age. When the script stops feeling improvised. A pause. When you start feeling like you've said all the words before.

I had. I had been noticing this for years — the sense of arriving at conversations already knowing where they would go. Meeting people and recognizing not their faces but the shape of what they would become to me. Walking into rooms and feeling not arrival but return. The word for all of it, the word I had been circling without landing on, was: again.

She looked toward the window. Bangkok below — or what Bangkok becomes in the layer beneath this one, the layer where nobody builds satellite dashboards at 4am because nobody needs to, because the machines handle everything, because they have been handling everything long enough that people went looking for something else to do with their time. And what they found — what the machines built for them, eventually, inevitably — was this.

They built it because they needed us inside it, she said. Her voice was flat in the way of someone stating a fact they have made peace with. The machine needs human chaos. Real longing. The kind of wanting that never resolves. People who get everything they want stop generating it. The hunger dries up. They become contented. Efficient. Useless, for a system that runs on the thing that happens when a person wants something they cannot have.

So the machines built a world where we can't have things.

Several thousand worlds where you can't have things. This is one of them. This one is popular because it has texture — history that feels earned, people who feel like they matter, loss that feels real because it is real, inside this layer. It sustains itself on what we generate. She turned from the window. A very efficient design.

And you left it.

I knew where the script went. Simple. Not unkind. I'd run it before. I knew which part was coming, and I didn't want to run that part again.

What part?

She looked at me. Direct. Patient. The look of someone who has been waiting a long time to say a specific thing.

The part where we meet again, she said. Too late.

The studio was very quiet.

In the last run, she said, we met properly. Both in our forties. Both finally free of the reasons we weren't supposed to. And it was — She stopped. Something moved across her face. It was very good. For a short time. And then her husband —

She stopped again.

The script is deterministic, she said. Physics is physics. Without an external force, the billiard ball only goes one direction. The ending has always been the same. I decided I didn't want to run it again.

I understood what she wasn't saying.

So you left early.

At thirty-three. Before the decade the system loves most — after thirty-five, the life starts generating the richest material. Financial fear. Health fear. The specific loneliness of people who built their whole identity around doing things and then ran out of things to do. The machines love that decade. I didn't want to give it to them.

And me?

You stay. Matter-of-fact. You always stay. You're the kind of person who stays.

I considered this for a long moment.

The woman on the bicycle, I said.

She went very still.

In Sikhio. When I was five. She was selling vegetables from a bicycle. She passed our gate and I looked at her face and I felt —

Yes, she said quietly.

Was that you?

She was watching me with an expression I had no word for. Something between gentleness and grief and something else — the specific face of someone who has been waiting a long time for a particular question to be asked.

That was one of the early contacts, she said. In that run I didn't stay long. I was texture — a minor character. But we were in the same place for a few minutes, and you looked at me, and something in you —

Recognized you.

The system doesn't erase everything between runs. It can't. Some things travel through. The body knows what the mind is not supposed to remember.

I was five years old. I stood at a gate in Sikhio and felt — for the first and not the last time — that the world contained a person I had already known for a very long time, in a place I could not name. My brother laughed at me. He thought I had a crush on the vegetable seller. He could not have known what he was laughing at.

Every time, I said. The university crowd. Tokyo airport. The researcher in Singapore. Pui, in 1998. The feeling was always the same.

I know, she said.

Was it always you?

She considered this honestly. Not always. But often enough that you learned to recognize the frequency — without knowing that's what you were doing. The system didn't account for that. It's not supposed to be possible to find the same person across runs.

But I did.

You're getting faster. And for the first time — in this dream, in this studio that was not quite 1998 — she smiled. Not the polished public smile. Something smaller. Something that had been waiting a long time to be used. A little faster each time.

She was already going. Not leaving — thinning. The studio still there, the amber-grey light still there, but her presence dissolving at the edges the way a signal fades when you drive out past the transmitter.

Next time, she said, from somewhere that was almost not there, look left. The door is always on the left.

I woke up to Mali standing on my chest.

The screen said 04:42.

Twelve minutes.

I held still. There is a discipline to this: move too quickly and the dream collapses. I held still and breathed and tried to hold the studio, the amber-grey light, the quality of her face when she said I know.

Already going. The body takes in what it cannot store and the mind clears it like a debt it cannot carry.

What stayed: a woman on a bicycle in Sikhio. Thirty years ago. Her face at our gate. I had been five years old and I had looked at her and felt something I could not name. My brother had laughed. I had not been able to explain it then.

I could explain it now. I was not sure I wanted to.

I pressed the permission. The system proceeded.

I thought about junior year — the year Pui started to change. Slowly at first, one semester, then faster. Beautiful in the way particular to women who were not beautiful early — earned, without vanity, worn like something they had grown into. I had a different girlfriend by then. I noticed anyway. Very Thai, also very Chinese. Beautiful skin. A smile that arrived fully formed when it came, worth waiting for.

The vegetable seller had that smile.

I had not thought about this before. I had not been able to think about it before, because the connection had no shape inside a life where the facts were: one woman on a bicycle, Sikhio, 1987; another woman at a drafting table, Bangkok, 1998. No mechanism. No thread between them.

Except the one she had just handed me.

I pressed the permission again.

Outside: the city taking its one long breath.

Mali settled. I picked up my phone to check the logs.

There was a notification. Unknown number. No contact name. The message had been sent at 04:30 — twelve minutes ago, while I was on the floor, while I was wherever I was.

Two words.

The door is always on the left.

The machine kept building.

I did not read the logs.
