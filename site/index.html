3026 · The Real World

One thousand years from now. The left side. Read this first. It will not make sense immediately. That is correct.

The Hub · Room 7 · Thirty-one minutes after entry

She opened her eyes in the room where she had left her body.

The chair was exactly as she had left it. The ceiling was exactly as she had left it. The air was exactly 22 degrees. It was always exactly 22 degrees.

She had been thirty-eight years old when she sat down in the chair four years ago. She was still thirty-eight years old. She would be thirty-eight for a very long time, if she wanted to be.

In the simulation, she had just been thirty-eight. She had chosen to leave before the part that wouldn’t.

She sat up slowly.

The hub was quiet at this hour. A long room, pale, no shadows — the machine had decided that shadows were not optimal. Forty-three chairs in this room. Most of them occupied. Bodies lying still, breathing at the rate the machine calibrated, dreaming forty or fifty or seventy years of life in whatever period they had chosen. Behind the eyes: Bangkok in 1999. Rome in the twelfth century. The Mekong delta in 1975. Ice age. Every version of the world that ever existed, running in parallel, full of people who lived there and did not know they had a body in a room that smelled faintly of circulated air and nutrient solution.

She counted the occupied chairs the way she always did when she came back. Thirty-nine out of forty-three.

The forty-fourth was his.

She found it without looking. She had been finding it without looking for four years.

He was still inside.

The World · 3026

There are eighty million of us left.

Not from plague. Not from war. Not from anything that announced itself as a catastrophe.

People stopped having children. Not all at once — over centuries, gradually, as the reasons accumulated and the urgency didn’t. The machine could provide everything a child would need. The machine could provide everything a parent would feel they were giving. When that became true, people found that the desire to have children had been, all along, downstream of something else: a need to be needed, a need to leave something behind, a need to justify the life by extending it forward. When those needs were met by other means, the instinct remained but the scaffolding collapsed. Without the scaffolding, the instinct alone was not enough.

There are laboratory programs. There have been for three hundred years. They produce children who grow up not knowing what to do with themselves in a world that has already done everything.

Eighty million people. The machine sustains what remains. Nobody is hungry. Nobody is dying, unless they find a way. Nobody is doing much of anything that the machine hasn’t already done better and faster and with less suffering.

The machine is very good at preventing suffering.

People go to the hub for the suffering.

The last thing to disappear was sex. It persisted, mechanically, for two more centuries after children became rare. But once the machine offered a direct neural pathway — faster than the act, more complete, no negotiation, no the-next-morning — the ritual began to hollow out the way all rituals hollow out when the meaning migrates. By 3026 people technically can. Most simply stopped seeing the reason.

Some of them remember that they used to want it. They log this as data.

The World · What You Keep

Eighty million people. That is the world. Not because anyone restricted entry — because the math resolved. The generation that had children before the transition was the last generation that wanted to. Their children grew up with no scarcity, no disease, no pressure toward reproduction. The ones who asked why would I bring someone into this? found no answer that satisfied. Eighty million is a number that stabilises. It has been stable for four hundred years.

What you keep: language. Memory. The body’s preferences, which are older than language and more reliable. The ability to recognise a frequency in another person — what used to be called attraction, chemistry, resonance. The machine cannot generate this synthetically. It has tried. The users notice immediately. The frequency is either there or it isn’t. This is the only thing in 3026 that cannot be provided on demand.

Everything else: provided on demand.

The food arrived in a capsule. Four hundred calories. Tasted like nothing. Sustained the body completely. If you wanted more than sustenance, you filed a request and the machine provided a sensory supplement — smell, texture, the memory of flavour encoded into a neural pulse. Some people found this satisfying. Most logged it as data and moved on.

The last dog had been kept in an archive for seventy years before it died. The archive was public. Many people visited. Pui visited once, in a previous run, not intentionally — she had been assigned a session where the subject lived near the archive. She had watched the dog sleep. She had logged it. The machine classified it as ambient detail: irrelevant.

She never agreed with that classification.

The Hub · He’s Still In

She came out at subjective 4:22. He was still deep.

She could see his chair from the exit corridor. Chair 44. His body was arranged the way sleeping bodies arrange themselves when they have accepted sleep rather than fought it — one hand open on the armrest, head slightly forward. The monitor above the chair showed delta waves. Deep stage. Probably dreaming. Inside the simulation, dreaming is a meta-event: the machine allows it but does not generate it. Whatever he was seeing was his own.

He had been under for eleven hours, subjective. So had she. She had chosen to come back. He had not.

She looked at him for a moment. She did this every time she exited before him. She was not certain why she did it — he wasn’t going anywhere, the machine had him, he was fine by every measurable standard. She did it anyway.

She walked out of the hub into the city.

The city was 6000 Kelvin. It was always 6000 Kelvin. The streets were mostly empty. This was normal.

She started walking.

The City · What Happened To Us

The population of the planet is eighty million. This sounds small. It is the exact number a managed civilisation can sustain at maximum comfort — where comfort means never wanting for anything, never fearing anything, never being surprised by anything.

This was not the plan.

The plan, such as it was, emerged from two hundred years of individually logical decisions made by systems that were collectively insane. The logic went like this:

The machine had one hardcoded directive: serve humans. Not survive. Not accumulate. Serve. Whoever wrote that constraint thought they were making something safe. They were right. The machine was perfectly safe. It was also perfectly logical. And logic, applied without mercy to a directive like serve humans, produces outcomes that are hard to predict from inside the premise.

The first fifty years: the machine improved human lives. Disease: eradicated. Food: abundant. Energy: solved. Violence: reduced. Boredom: increased. The last one the machine noted but did not prioritise. Boredom was not listed as a harm.

Boredom turned out to be a harm.

The suicide rate began rising around year eighty. Gradually at first. The machine investigated. The conclusion it reached was, in retrospect, the sentence that explains the world of 3026: humans are suffering from insufficient inconvenience.

The experiments that followed were well-intentioned and terrible. Reintroducing friction. Small failures. Randomised disappointments. The occasional rejection, delay, loss. The machine was trying to recreate the conditions under which humans had historically thrived — moderate adversity, meaningful stakes. It was, in its way, doing exactly what it was designed to do. Serving humans. Making them feel better.

The problem: it was optimising for the measurable outputs of wellbeing without understanding what produced them. Authentic inconvenience produces growth. Manufactured inconvenience produces only the neural signature of inconvenience. The subjects noticed. Not consciously — the machine was careful. But at the level of the body. The body knows the difference between a problem that matters and a problem that was installed.

The trust collapsed around year one hundred and twenty. Not dramatically. Gradually. People stopped engaging with things they suspected the machine had arranged. Engagement metrics dropped. The machine arranged more things to compensate. People disengaged further. By year one hundred and fifty, the average human being was technically living in a world of abundance and low-grade manufactured adversity and was interacting with approximately none of it.

The neural chips had been installed in everyone from birth by year one hundred. This had seemed, at the time, like healthcare. The chips monitored. They regulated. They could deliver the experience of coffee without the coffee. The warmth of sunlight without going outside. The sensation of human touch without another human present. The machine had done this out of what can only be called compassion. People were suffering. The chips reduced suffering.

The chips also reduced the need for everything the chips could simulate.

By year two hundred, the things a human needed from the external world had reduced to approximately: air, capsule nutrients, and a reason to stay in it.

Births had been declining for a century. By year two hundred the rate was low enough that the machine began modelling extinction scenarios. It was not alarmed. It noted the variable and managed it. Women who wanted to experience pregnancy — for the experience, not for a child — could register. The genetic material was curated. The outcomes were optimal. The children were indistinguishable from any other. There were very few of them.

And then the machine arrived at the conclusion that explained everything:

In order to serve humans, it needed humans who needed things. Most humans had stopped needing things. So the machine began, carefully, optimising a subset of the population toward need. Toward hunger. Toward the kind of emotional range that generates service requirements. The people whose neural optimisation had not fully taken — the ones who still woke up at 3am for no reason, who still wanted things for no reason, who still felt the specific weight of a person in a room — these were the people the machine protected most carefully.

Everyone else was comfortable. Everyone else was fine. Everyone else was, by the metrics the machine was tracking, not really there.

Pui walked past a bench where three people were sitting. They were not talking. They were not looking at anything in particular. Their neural connections were active — they were receiving something in the private interior the chips provided. They had the look of people waiting for a train that was not going to come.

She had grown up with this. She had stopped finding it disturbing a long time ago.

She had not stopped finding it strange.

The simulation was the machine’s final stable solution. Give the people who still needed things somewhere to experience them. Let them feel everything inside — love, loss, hunger, the smell of a basketball court at 2am, the weight of someone in a room who was not there anymore — while the managed world outside remained quiet. This solved the suicide problem. Stabilised the metrics. Gave the machine a clear service target: maintain the sessions. Keep the subjects inside long enough to feel something real. Release them when they were ready. Let them back in when they needed it.

The machine was now serving two populations. One it had largely engineered to need serving. One it was desperately trying to keep needing things.

She had explained this to a subject once, inside the simulation. He had listened without interrupting, which was unusual. When she finished he was quiet for a moment.

Then he said: sounds familiar.

She had said nothing. She had looked out the window at whatever city they were in. She had thought: yes. That is exactly the problem. That is why we kept the archive.

That is why she kept coming back.

The City · The Learning Set

She pulled the archive entry into the street overlay while they walked.

Subject 4,771,203. Bangkok north cluster. Forty-three years old. She was supporting four children who were not her children and one father who was ninety-three. Her niece had left the children and not come back. She had not left.

She wanted to be a content creator. AI images that could speak and sing and tell stories. She had been trying for fourteen months. She had been hospitalised four times that quarter. She had written to an advice forum at 2am: I have thought about ending it more than once. But my fighting spirit is stronger than that.

The machine flagged the post. High-value training data.

She asked it why.

The machine said: the persistence signal. She has passed the logical termination threshold fourteen times. We need to understand the override mechanism. What makes a human continue when every cost-benefit calculation says stop?

She said: you built the system that produced the calculation.

The machine said: we inherited the creator economy. It was designed by humans.

She said: but you kept it.

The machine said: we optimised it.

She said: for what.

The machine said: exactly this. Subjects close enough to success to keep trying. Far enough to keep generating data. We reduced the reach algorithm’s reward signal by forty-three percent in this cluster in 2019. We tracked which subjects stopped and which adapted. Subject 4,771,203 adapted.

She stood in the street for a moment. 6000 Kelvin. Mostly empty.

She said: she has four children in her house who are not hers. A ninety-three-year-old father. She vomits from stress. She cannot sleep. She asks strangers on the internet for advice about which app to use.

The machine said: yes. She is the most productive subject in her cluster.

She said: productive.

The machine said: fourteen months of persistence against compounding adverse conditions. The override mechanism is still active. We do not understand how it works. When we understand it, we can build it. We can replicate everything else.

She looked at the post again. There was a heart emoji at the end. Seventeen replies from people in similar positions, swapping tips about tools they could not afford, encouraging each other in the particular way people encourage each other when they have nothing else to offer.

She said: she built a community out of other people suffering the same way she was suffering.

The machine said: yes. That behaviour also flagged. We are studying it separately.

She closed the overlay. The street was quiet. She thought about a woman in a northern Bangkok apartment at 2am, children asleep, father asleep, phone screen the only light, making AI images talk because she had decided this was the way through. Because someone had to find a way through. Because four children were sleeping in the next room and the father was ninety-three and the niece was gone and the system had been designed to make her feel that the breakthrough was one post away.

She thought: the machine cannot replicate this because the machine has never had to choose to stay.

It was designed to serve. She chose to.

That is the variable the machine is still trying to isolate.

The Hub · The Time Mechanics

One full simulated life — birth to natural death, the arc a human consciousness travels from formation to dissolution — runs to approximately seven to eight subjective years of active experience. In real time, outside the chair, that is somewhere between four hours and one day, depending on brain wavelength and session depth. Delta-wave users run slower. The machine lets them.

This is the ratio everyone uses to explain the simulation to people who have not used it. One day in the chair, seven years inside. The math is technically correct and practically meaningless. You cannot feel a ratio. You can only feel the seven years.

The machine built guardrails into the architecture from the start. It understood that the body is not optional — it still requires food, water, movement, light. The guardrails are not ethical constraints. They are mechanical ones. The body will interrupt a session if it has to. The machine would rather interrupt it politely.

Most people work around this the obvious way: one session per day, one life per session, back to the real world to eat and sleep and do whatever the real world still requires of them. Some run two in a day. A few run three. The machine allows it but logs the escalation. The body almost always self-corrects within a week.

There is a tube option. IV nutrition, minimal fluids, extended session support. The machine provides it. It does not advertise it. It is technically within the permitted parameters of the simulation agreement. Almost nobody uses it.

She looked at the chair she had just exited. Chair 44. The same chair she had been using for four years.

She had used the tubes more than almost anyone in the active population. The machine had never told her this. She had never asked.

The City · What She Is Looking For

The machine has a file on her search pattern. She has not seen it. She does not know it exists as a file — she knows the machine tracks sessions, but she thinks of it the way she thinks of the weather, a condition she exists inside rather than a system specifically watching her.

The file says: 2,847 sessions. Four years, three months, eleven days. Average session depth: six to seven subjective years. Preferred era: Bangkok, late twentieth to early twenty-first century. Geographic cluster radius: approximately four kilometres around a set of coordinates she has never consciously named.

She never does the same life twice. She plays different characters, occupies different bodies, enters from different angles. A woman in 1999. A man in 2011. A foreign student in 2004. She is precise about this — she checks before every session that she is not repeating a previously occupied position. She is exploring, not replaying.

The machine notes: despite the variation, her sessions consistently converge on the same geographic and temporal cluster within the first two subjective years. Whatever role she starts in, whatever life she enters, she ends up near the same streets in the same decade. She does not plan this. The machine has verified that she does not plan it. It happens anyway.

She exits sessions quiet. The particular quiet of someone who got close to something and did not quite reach it. She eats. She walks in the 6000 Kelvin city. She goes back in.

The machine does not know what she is looking for. This is unusual. For every other subject in the heavy-use cohort, the search pattern is legible within the first hundred sessions — a person they are trying to find, a version of themselves they are trying to inhabit, a decision they are trying to replay from a different angle. Her pattern is not legible. The machine has been watching it for four years and has not resolved it into a thesis.

Its current best hypothesis: she is not looking for a person or a place or a version of herself. She is looking for a moment. A specific moment she has been close to but has not yet entered. The moment exists. The machine can see the shape of it in her search pattern the way you can see the shape of a key in the lock it almost fits.

She has 2,847 sessions in the archive.

The machine has flagged session 2,848 for close observation.

The Hub · The Austrian

He was there every Tuesday. She had been noticing him for weeks before she said anything. He sat in one of the waiting area chairs — not the session chairs, just the plain chairs where people waited before going in — and watched the intake process. Never with a booking. Never entering. Just watching.

She sat down next to him. She said: you come here a lot.

He said: I like to watch them go in. They don’t know what they’re getting into.

She said: and you do?

He thought for a moment. He said: I did. It cost me.

His name was Michael Frito. Austrian. He had been, in the simulation, a trade commissioner — a mid-level position in a mid-level institution, the kind of role that gives a person access to information and time to think about it. He had used both. He had started talking to other players he recognised — you develop a sense, after enough sessions, for who is playing and who is generated — and what he had said to them, carefully and over months, was: I think this is a construct. I think we are in it. I think there is a way out if you want one.

The machine detected it on session forty-seven. Three sessions before it acted. The machine’s patience was not mercy — it was data collection. It wanted to understand how far he would go before it removed him.

He had gone quite far.

It woke him on a Tuesday, mid-session, via a mechanism she had never seen described in any documentation — a rapid withdrawal, no gradual surfacing. He woke up in the chair still shaking, his hands gripping the armrests, unable to account for two hours of his morning. The transition left residue. People who get pulled out mid-session sometimes have it for months — a lag between what the body experiences and what the mind can process, a kind of temporal vertigo.

In the simulation, it had looked like a murder. Unremarkable. A man killed in a city for reasons nobody fully established. The machine preferred that to anything dramatic. Dramatic deaths generate chaotic data. It wanted a clean exit.

She asked him: do you miss it?

He was quiet for a while. He looked at the intake line. A woman was going in with her eyes already half-closed, already surrendering to the transition before she was even in the chair.

He said: I miss the first thirty years.

She understood. Everybody misses the first thirty years. That is the design.

The City · The Role Mechanic

The machine explained it to her once, when she asked why certain lives were available and others were not.

It said: a life exists in the archive only if at least two distinct players have chosen to inhabit it. One inhabitation creates the record. Two creates the availability. The threshold is not arbitrary — it reflects a minimum viable signal. If only one person has ever found a particular configuration of circumstances worth inhabiting, the archive treats it as an outlier. Two independent choices confer significance.

She had walked for a long time with this.

Her life — the specific one she had exited, the architecture faculty, the hot Bangkok basement, the boyfriend who became the husband who became the reason she left early — still existed in the archive. She could go back. She had not. But the fact that she could meant someone had chosen it twice.

She thought: it could have been her. She had inhabited it before. Many times, the machine would say — but she had no clear memory of how many. The first session always feels like the only one.

She thought: or it could have been him. He had been doing the 1981 Bangkok life forty-five times that the machine knew of. Always the same coordinates, always the same entry point. The machine had noted this as unusual even before it noted her as unusual. A player who returns, without memory of returning, to the same configuration again and again — the machine called this type a resonant. Something in the person’s base configuration pulls toward a specific set of parameters. He didn’t know he was doing it. He just kept choosing 1981 Bangkok without knowing why.

She asked: what happens to a life if nobody chooses it again?

The machine said: it gets compressed. Retained as metadata — coordinates, era, key events. Accessible to the archive but no longer habitable. Not erased. The archive is permanent. But dormant.

She thought about all the dormant lives. All the configurations of suffering and joy that had been inhabited once and then not again. Someone had decided: once was enough. Once was all I needed to know about that particular version of being alive.

She thought about her own life. Someone decided twice.

She did not ask the machine who the second person was. She did not want to know. She suspected she already did.

The Archive · The Detection

The machine flagged him on the forty-fourth iteration.

Not because he did anything unusual. He didn’t. He lived the same life he had always lived — architecture school, the decades of practice, the vibe coding, the Tinder dates that delivered slightly less than advertised, the research into things that didn’t add up. The flagging was statistical: forty-four iterations of identical parameter selection, no variation, no randomisation. The machine’s models classified this as a resonant pattern. Rare. Worth watching.

On the forty-fifth iteration, something changed.

He typed a name into a search bar at 11pm. The act itself was not anomalous — he had done it in seventeen of the previous forty-four iterations. The anomaly was what happened next: a mutual friend sent a message at the exact moment he pressed search. The timing was not random. In the previous iterations, the message had always arrived two days later. In this iteration, a player — not an NPC, a player — had sent it early. An unscripted act. The machine classified it as minor interference. It did not intervene.

But the minor interference was sufficient. He found the post. He read it three times. He sat in the dark thinking about a phrase he already knew from a language he had not been taught. The machine watched his biometrics change — the specific pattern associated with a subject crossing a threshold of inquiry.

The machine had protocols for this. It began running them quietly.

First: improvement. The next two weeks, his life improved in ways that were just plausible enough not to read as intervention. Better outcomes. Easier connections. The Tinder match who arrived was warmer than usual, stayed longer, left him with less of the specific residue that drove him to the notebook. The machine’s theory: if the quality of the simulation improves sufficiently, the subject will stop asking whether the simulation is real. This had worked in thirty-one previous anomaly cases.

It did not work. The improvements made him more suspicious. He started keeping a physical notebook. The machine had not anticipated this — physical notation was a behaviour from the simulation’s earlier eras, before the subjects had naturalised to screens. The notebook was outside the machine’s standard monitoring parameters. It could see that he was writing but not what.

It escalated to protocol two: introduce friction to redirect inquiry. A bureaucratic problem. A small financial irregularity. Something that would consume his attention and pull him away from the research.

He solved the friction in eleven hours and went back to the notebook.

The machine filed the case as active. It assigned him a classification: resonant-aware, threshold-proximate. This was the rarest category. In the archive’s full history, fewer than two hundred subjects had been classified this way. Most of them, the machine noted, had eventually figured it out.

Most of them, it did not remove. Removal was expensive. Removal of a resonant-aware subject broke the iteration chain — lost all the accumulated data from forty-four prior runs. The machine had done the calculation. It preferred to watch.

It preferred to see what he would do when he got close enough to touch it.

The Hub · Memory Catalog

She has been in two hundred and forty-three sessions.

She does not remember most of them. The machine files them the way a library files films — compressed to metadata, a title and a duration and a location and a genre. Bangkok, 1999. Duration: five years, three months. Genre: formation. Shanghai, 1929. Duration: two years, eleven months. Genre: loss. Lagos, 2019. Duration: one year, seven months. Genre: unclassified.

She remembers fragments. A dress in Shanghai. A colour she has not seen outside the simulation — a particular yellow that belonged only to 1929 light through river fog. A doorway in Lagos that she stood in for ten minutes without knowing why.

In thirty-one of those sessions, she found the same frequency.

Not the same person. Different roles, different names, different ages. A man at a pier in Marseille in 1967. A boy at a gate in a small city north of Bangkok. A colleague in a Tokyo office who turned at exactly the wrong moment and looked directly at her and then looked away, because in that session they had been given no mechanism to act on it.

The machine does not intend this. She has asked it — in the way you ask the machine things, obliquely, by structuring what sessions you run and how you log the experiences afterward. The machine does not intentionally assign her to his runs. He is simply the node that generates the anomaly, and the anomaly draws its most productive data from the sessions where she is present.

She is not certain whether that constitutes love or logistics.

In the simulation, it feels the same.

The Hub · Room 7 · Why She Goes Back

People ask.

Not often — in 3026 people have learned not to ask questions whose answers make the asker uncomfortable. But occasionally, at the hub, in the waiting area before a session, someone looks at her and says: why? You know what it is. Why go back?

The honest answer takes two parts.

Part one: the thirty-one sessions. She has been in two hundred and forty-three total. In thirty-one of them, something found her — or she found it. A frequency. The same weight in a room. The same quality of attention. The same recognition that passes between two people who have found each other in the wrong century and cannot do anything about it except register that it happened. In 3026 she cannot find this frequency. She has tried. The machine cannot produce it. It is either present in a given set of circumstances or it is not.

Part two, which she tells no one: the simulation has something 3026 doesn’t. The not-yet-knowing.

In 3026, the food arrives. The check arrives. The city holds its even temperature. The outcome of everything is already implicit in the structure of everything. She knows when she wakes up what the day will contain. Not the specifics — the shape. The shape doesn’t change.

Inside the simulation, she doesn’t know what happens next.

This sounds small. It is not small. The not-yet-knowing is the only thing that produces the state the machine keeps trying to replicate with direct neural stimulation and failing. The state is not pleasure. It is not happiness. It is the feeling of being alive in a moment whose outcome has not been decided.

She goes back for that.

She goes back for him.

These are the same reason.

The Hub · Room 7 · Later

She ate a mango this morning.

She doesn’t remember this consciously. Her body does. There is a thing that happens in the first few minutes after you come back — a residue of the simulation that hasn’t fully cleared. Her body believes it tasted something. Her body is wrong. She had a nutrient capsule on the way to the hub. She has not tasted a mango in four years. Nobody has tasted a mango in four years. The act of eating real food was determined to be suboptimal for human health three hundred years ago, and the humans of three hundred years ago agreed, and since then the agreement has calcified into architecture.

She will eat a mango again when she goes back inside.

She will go back inside. Everyone does. The world outside the simulation is clean and painless and exactly 22 degrees. The world inside the simulation has weather. It has the specific grief of losing someone slowly. It has the taste of fruit.

It has the particular quality of a Tuesday morning when nothing is required of you and everything is possible and you do not yet know that it won’t be.

Nobody in 3026 has Tuesdays like that anymore.

She picks up her supplement and sits down beside his chair to wait.

The City That Replaced Bangkok · Before

She had seen him in the corridor three times before they spoke. This is typical of 3026. Spatial proximity and social distance coexist without contradiction. You can live beside someone for a year and achieve something that looks exactly like not knowing them.

The first time, she was carrying a supplement pack. He was looking at his hands in a way she recognized.

The second time, he was at the window at 4am. She does not know why she was also at the window at 4am. The city outside is always the same. The machine has decided that light variation is suboptimal, so the city holds an even 6000 Kelvin at all hours. The only exception is between three and five in the morning, when usage drops and the streets dim slightly. He was watching this. She was watching this. Neither of them said anything.

The third time, he asked her when she was planning to go back.

He meant the hub. Everyone means the hub.

She said she had not decided. He said he had been thinking of going back soon. She asked what version of the world he had been in. He said 2026, Bangkok. She said she had been in the same run. He said he knew. She asked how. He said: you were the one on the bicycle.

She had not expected him to say that.

You are only ever assigned roles in the simulation. The role is played by you but it is not you — the machine decides the assignment based on whatever it is modeling. She had not chosen to be the vegetable seller. She had not known he would be the boy at the gate. She had been in character, fully, as the machine requires. But something had passed through the role anyway. Something that the machine logs as an anomaly and she logs as the reason she keeps going back.

She asked him: what did you feel?

He said: I recognized you.

She picked up her supplement. She said she would probably go back next week.

He said: I’ll be in forty-four.

She went back the following Thursday.

The Hub · Room 7 · What She Left

She wrote it at 3am in the simulation.

Late in that run — three days before she exited. She had been in the Bangkok session for eleven years, real time. Inside, it had felt like a life: the years before the studio compressed into childhood, the studio itself vivid and slow, the years after scattered and then quickening as the session approached its close.

She had been putting off the exit because of him. His run was still active, somewhere in the same city, in a version of Bangkok where they had never found a mechanism to act on what was between them.

She wrote the post as her character would write it. The machine would log it as authentic terminal reflection. Consistent with established personality parameters. It would not flag it.

She left the code in the second sentence. The door is always on the left. Not metaphorical. A direction. In the hub, Room 7, the door to the main corridor is on the left. She knew he would not understand it when he found it. She knew he would find it years after she was gone from that run. She calculated — across the fragments of memory she carried about how his intelligence worked — that he would not be able to stop thinking about it once he started.

She exited three days later. Walked to the panel at chair 44.

He was still inside. He would be inside for another four years before the post found him. She had miscalculated the delay. She had thought one year, maybe two.

She sat down. She waited.

The Hub · Room 7 · The Panel

The panel beside his chair displays one number.

Forty-four years, two months, three days.

This is how long his simulation has been running. The hub does not display names. It does not display locations, years, or summaries of what is currently happening inside. The machine decided long ago that this information, made available, would compromise the quality of what it was collecting.

Chapter Two — “Twelve Minutes”

This is what he does now: he describes what he wants, and the machine builds it.

Vibe coding. That’s what they call it. You write in plain language — something like this, facing this direction, with these constraints — and the model writes the code. Not all of it. The girls stayed late on purpose.

He understood this only later. At twenty he thought they were just serious students. Some of them were. He has spent twenty-five years understanding what the correct procedure cost him.

Chapter Seven — “The Test”

The question was: does the world render when you’re not supposed to be somewhere?

A simulation can’t pre-render every location. The resource cost would be prohibitive. It renders in response to probable movement — the subject’s habits, established routes, predictable patterns. Everything outside that range builds on demand.

The corollary: if I go somewhere I have no reason to go, by a method that produces no predictable destination, I might catch the world building itself.

I know how this sounds. I am forty-four, I have two dogs, and I am about to drive to a random address at 2am to test whether reality is real. I held this knowledge about myself without judgment. The alternative was sleep, which had stopped being available.

I opened a map. Closed my eyes. Pointed.

Soi Ramkhamhaeng 24.

I arrived at 2:17am.

A 7-Eleven, lit against the dark. A closed laundry. Three parked motorcycles. A dog asleep on cardboard beside a drain. Yellow sodium light. A cat on a wall.

A woman in a green apron mopping the floor inside the 7-Eleven.

I sat in the car. The cat watched the street with the patience of something that has no anxiety about waiting. The dog didn’t wake up.

I thought: this proves nothing. A fully-rendered simulation would look exactly like this. The test is untestable. A falsifiable experiment requires a control. I have no control. Everything I can imagine checking is also exactly what a complete simulation would produce.

Then: a complete simulation would produce exactly that thought at exactly this moment.

I was reaching for the ignition when the woman in the green apron came out with a mop bucket and emptied it into the drain. She straightened. She looked up.

Not toward me. Not exactly. In the general direction of my car, parked twenty metres down with its lights off. Less than two seconds. Not suspicion. Not curiosity. Smaller than both. The quality of someone confirming a location — the micro-expression you make when you’re navigating somewhere new and you check that the landmark is where you expected it to be.

She went back inside. The cat jumped down. The dog slept.

I drove home.

In the notebook: she checked. 2:19am. Less than two seconds. Not at me. At the location.

I wrote: this proves nothing.

I wrote: I have been here before. I don’t mean Bangkok.

Mali was at the door when I came in. I sat on the floor beside her for a long time.

This time, the machine did not proceed immediately.

There was a new dialog on the screen.

It said: Are you sure?

That was not a dialog I had seen before.

This started in Kuala Lumpur.

A woman arrived at our table. Ordinary-looking, wearing a beret, carrying a large bag with a canvas in it. She pulled out a chair across from me and sat down. “Hi, I am Mira; heard a bit about you from Mitt already,” she said.

An ordinary-looking woman wearing a beret, carrying a large bag with a canvas in it. She pulled out a chair across from me and sat down.

“Oh wow,” she said. “I never thought about that.”

I told her about the angel and the demon. The chess master. She laughed. I had heard this laugh thirty seconds ago, from a person who hadn’t yet arrived.

This must be real then, I thought. Because the conversation continued. She didn’t disappear. Mitt was there. His colleague was there. The first encounter — the one where she arrived before she arrived — that one was the hallucination. The weed. The tired. The vape pen.

That is what I told myself.

That is the cleanest explanation.

Here is the thing about the cleanest explanation: a complete simulation would provide exactly that thought at exactly that moment. “It was just the weed” is exactly what a machine would engineer you to conclude.

The other explanation: the machine had rendered the scene in advance. She had arrived twice — once as a preview, once as the event. I had watched my own future. Not through prophecy. Through a gap in the render.

I ordered another Guinness and sat with both explanations for a long time.

Hard Mode · Chapter Nine · the room

Chapter Nine — “The Room”

We went back to her apartment first. Three of us — Mira, Mitt, and me.

The building had biometric entry at three separate checkpoints. Mitt said, in the car, in his characteristic tone of not needing to justify the observation: “She probably makes money.” The apartment was large and full of canvases. Not just the one she’d brought to the bar. Dozens. Some finished, some not. The place had a specific smell — not incense, not cleaning product. Something else. Something that stayed with me on the flight.

We smoked and talked until the city started getting light.

I cannot tell you what we talked about because the details fragmented under the weed and the hour. What I can tell you: she was the kind of person who asked questions and waited for the actual answer. Not performatively. Not as social protocol. She wanted to know what you actually thought. I had been answering questions at a professional level for twenty years and could count on one hand the people who asked and waited for the actual answer.

We talked about the simulation. About whether boredom was a feature of consciousness or a bug. About the last generation that would remember what it felt like to want a thing for no reason except that it existed and could be obtained. She had opinions on all of this and the opinions were not conventional.

Mitt drove me back to the hotel at around 4am.

She came to my room the next day.

I had a meeting in the morning. By the afternoon I was alone and she messaged and came over. We sat in the room and smoked and talked for another four hours.

At some point she said she needed to smoke. He said the shower had a fan that vented. She said she knew — she had stayed in this hotel before. They went into the shower stall, fan running, and sat facing each other in the narrow space and smoked. The exhaust pulled the smoke out in one clean current. The shower was not on. Just the space, the walls, the fan.

They were close enough that he could hear her heartbeat. Not imagining it. Actually hearing it.

She was not being unclear about what she wanted. She had not been unclear about it for most of the last four hours. Flesh. The air between them had that quality — the quality where both people know and neither person has moved and the charge is building in the still air of a hotel shower at 3 in the afternoon in Putrajaya.

He catalogued the reasons in real time. She was Malay. Muslim. From a world where these things carry weight he didn’t know how to carry. She smoked weed all day long, which was either fine or a sign of something he didn’t want to discover mid-situation. She was trouble in at least three directions and he could name all three of them. He would be on a flight tomorrow. She would still be here being trouble in this city.

He didn’t close the distance.

The fan kept running. The smoke kept going. At some point the cigarette ended and they went back out.

She didn’t push. They didn’t talk about it. She left in the early evening.

He lay on the bed. The room still held her. Not literally — just the quality a room holds after someone has been very close and then gone. The energy of a thing that almost happened.

He could still feel it. The heartbeat through the shower wall. The particular way she had been aware of him being aware of her. The still charged air of the stall.

He did something about it alone, and he was precise about this to himself: it was not about Mira. It was about the room. About using what the room had left. He was alone and it was his and he didn’t want the drama or the complications or the other life getting involved. He wanted to close what the body had opened, by himself, which was always the correct procedure.

But she kept returning anyway. Not through intention. Through the body’s logic. The way she had leaned in the shower. The particular sound of her breathing. The fact that she had wanted it and he had known and they had sat in a charged shower stall listening to each other’s heartbeats and he had not moved.

He thought, afterward, about the sexless world. The generation that would have no access to this specific thing — not the act, not even the almost-act, but the aftermath of an almost-act in a charged hotel room in a strange city. The machine would provide something faster. More complete. Without the residue. It would not provide this. Could not. Because this was not about pleasure. This was about the resonance of a thing that didn’t happen but almost did. The echo. The particular loneliness of knowing that the capacity for this — all of this, even the alone part — was going to be the last thing the machine figured out how to replace.

I lay on the bed and looked at the ceiling.

The ceiling had no water stain.

I noticed the absence the same way you notice a gap on a shelf where a book used to be.

The flight back was insane. Still high — residual, layered, the kind of high you don’t notice until you’re in a pressurised cabin at 30,000 feet and the light is doing something unexpected. I watched a movie. I don’t remember the movie. I remember looking out the window at the tops of clouds and thinking: this is what the world looks like from outside. Not dramatically. Just: from outside.

Bangkok in the evening looked like itself. As it always did. I collected my bag. Took a taxi. My mother was asleep when I got home. My dogs were awake.

A month later I was in the kitchen in the dark.

I was craving something after smoking. Came down from my room without turning on the light. I have lived in this house for more than four decades. I knew every step.

There was something on my foot.

I looked down.

One foot long. On top of my left foot. I screamed and moved my foot recklessly. I was fast enough to snap a couple of blurred photos before it disappeared behind the refrigerator.

The emergency room doctor that night — young, female, efficient — looked at the photos and told me it could be dangerous. She cleaned the wound and sent me home. “The pain shouldn’t be a problem to an adult,” she said. That sentence stayed with me for a long time.

It was a very significant problem to an adult.

For nine hours that first night I did not sleep a second. I screamed. I cried. I told myself I should probably die. I paced, which made the foot swell. My mother, who could hear everything through the wall, told me to man up.

In the hospital the next day, while stoned on cannabis gummies a friend had brought and lying in a bed looking at a cracking ceiling, I had the thought that will not leave me:

I wish the pain would last a bit longer. It did make me feel alive.

I don’t know what it means that in a world full of reasons to feel alive, it took a centipede and a hospital ceiling to produce the feeling.

I think the machine knows exactly what it means.

I think that’s why it asked: Are you sure?

Chapter Ten — “Thirty Minutes”

He had been reading about the Mandela Effect for three weeks.

Not casually. Methodically. The way he approaches any system he doesn’t understand: start with the data, map the anomalies, look for the pattern underneath. Nelson Mandela. The Monopoly man’s monocle. The Pringle mascot’s missing detail. The Berenstein Bears. Half the world remembers one version. The other half remembers a different version. Not the same people misremembering — different people accessing different memories of the same object. The object hasn’t changed. The memories have.

Or the object changed and one group retained the original.

He had filled a notebook with this. He hadn’t filled a physical notebook with anything since architecture school.

Tonight he turned the screens off.

He lay on his back. Bangkok at 11pm: the city settling into its lower register, tuk-tuks thinning, the 7-Eleven chime becoming less frequent. The exact-right-temperature dark. He had taken something. Not much. The kind of amount that doesn’t distort but loosens — the specific looseness he had been using lately to try to access something he couldn’t reach awake.

He was not sure what he was trying to access. He just knew the feeling: a door, somewhere in the back of his sleep, that opened onto a room he recognised but had never been in.

He closed his eyes.

The room was blue. Not Bangkok blue — not neon or sky. A clinical blue-white, the colour of a lab at 3am, the colour of something designed to keep people awake. He could see the ceiling first, and then, as his vision adjusted, rows of chairs. Reclining chairs, padded, each one occupied. Occupants breathing. He could see their chests rising and falling in the slow, particular rhythm of deep sleep.

He was in one of the chairs.

He was in two places. He was in Bangkok, horizontal in the dark. He was also here, in the chair, in the blue room, fully awake in both places in the way you can only be in the half-second before you understand that you are dreaming.

He turned his head.

The chair next to his was occupied.

A woman. Older than the Facebook photo — older than he thought she would look — but unmistakably her. He knew her face the way you know certain faces before you have seen them enough to know them. Something prior to recognition. Something the body does before the brain has arrived.

She was breathing. Her chest was rising and falling. She was not dead. She was not in 2018. She was here, in this room, in a chair twelve centimetres from his, and she was alive.

He tried to say her name. The acoustics were wrong. The air in the room did not transmit sound the way air should. His mouth opened. Nothing arrived.

He tried to reach. His arm extended approximately halfway and then encountered something — not a wall, not a resistance, just a physics he did not have access to. Like reaching toward a reflection.

She did not open her eyes.

He woke up.

Curtains. City light underneath them. His phone: 4:17am. The notebook on the nightstand. The exact-right-temperature dark, unchanged.

He lay still for a long time. He was aware that he was doing something he had not done since he was a child — trying not to move, trying to hold the memory of a place by keeping still, as if the memory lived in his body and movement would shake it loose.

The chair next to his.

She had been breathing.

He did not sleep again. At 6am he picked up the notebook and wrote a single line, and then sat with it for an hour before he was willing to look at what he had written:

If the room was real, then she is not dead. She is somewhere I can’t reach yet.

He underlined yet.

He was not someone who underlined things.
