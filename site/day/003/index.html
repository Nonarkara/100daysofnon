Phase 2  /  Work & Method
Question

45 minutes.
Zero baht.
That's the claim.

You walked into a room at TKC today and demonstrated a working national television streaming platform. Every free-to-air channel. Emergency alerts. Reminder system. A feasibility study. All of it, apparently built overnight. Walk through the architecture. Then walk through the S-curve diagnosis you gave them. The record will audit what it can.

Answer — Non Arkara

The government wants to build a national digital television platform. One website, all free-to-air channels, accessible anywhere — especially in places where the broadcast signal is weak but 4G is available. In Mae Hong Son, television access is around 24%. Mobile internet access is around 74%. Seven times higher. Children there could learn from educational programmes if the signal could travel differently.

The budget attached to a project like this: twenty million baht. The normal path — feasibility study, six months, thick report. Prototype by year two, if they're lucky.

I showed them something different. An old Android phone as the server. Twenty-one browser tabs running simultaneously, each streaming a different channel, live. Those feeds go to a website hosted on GitHub and served through Cloudflare. No cloud bills. No engineering team on retainer. Internet bandwidth: approximately twelve satang an hour.

I also built an emergency alert function. One button, all screens update simultaneously. No per-message fee. Sending an SMS alert to seventy million people at standard Thai carrier rates costs somewhere between seven and thirty-five million baht, depending on the carrier and volume. My version costs nothing. A reminder system too — set a notification for a show, get it via phone or LINE before it starts.

The feasibility study is seventy-five pages. McKinsey format. It benchmarks NHK World, BBC iPlayer, the American networks — operating costs, revenue models, Thai regulatory context, ROI projections at three coverage levels.

Total build time: forty-five minutes. Total infrastructure cost: zero baht.

The question I left them with: if I can walk into the regulator's office tomorrow and show them this working, why would the government spend twenty million baht on something that doesn't exist yet?

That's the ninja way. A ninja doesn't buy a nuclear weapon to accomplish a mission. A ninja kills with a pencil. One side of a pencil. Our goal is the same — to succeed, to build something real — but we use the minimum necessary force.

But the room at TKC was not really there to hear about a TV platform. The room was there because TKC has a problem.

Every organisation follows an S-curve. Slow start, rapid growth, plateau, decline. TKC was founded twenty-one years ago. The growth phase was real — they won major projects, built credibility, scaled. But from the numbers I reviewed, the company has been stagnant for two years. Last year's profit came from investment income, not from core operations. Core business is already losing money.

Nokia built excellent phones. They had the technology, the brand, the distribution. They did not see the second curve coming. TKC's version of the iPhone is a competitor who can now deliver what used to cost millions, using AI, for zero baht, in forty-five minutes. The window to start a new curve is now. Not next quarter.

So I gave them a month. Each team: one working prototype by the twenty-sixth. Not a slide deck. Not a proposal document. A real thing — a functional system that solves a real problem, internal to TKC or external.

I gave each team API tokens: roughly one million tokens, about a hundred US dollars' worth. Whatever tools they want to use. I will coach them during the month — how to use AI to build fast, how to break projects into agents, how to treat Python like English.

I am an architect. I never studied computer science formally. I learned to build by doing. If I can do it, they can.

The person who builds a working prototype in one month is the person who changes the company's S-curve. Not the person with the best title. Not the person with the longest tenure. On the twenty-sixth, we'll see who that is.

Narration — Biographer

Day three. He left the museum and went to TKC.

He made a claim and brought evidence in the same sentence. The platform exists. It streams. The zero-baht framing is a rhetorical compression — electricity runs at roughly one to two baht per hour for the phone and router combined, and the device depreciates, and seven years of compounded knowledge about how to build things fast does not appear on any ledger. What he means is: zero marginal infrastructure cost. The total cost is not zero.

The forty-five minutes is unverifiable. He didn't set a timer in front of witnesses. The Dragon Quest hero-card system was visible on the projection screen. The room could see it working.

What the record confirms: GitHub Pages is free for public repositories. Cloudflare's free tier serves static content at scale. Thailand decriminalised kratom in August 2021. NHK World, BBC iPlayer, and American PBS affiliates all operate as free-access digital models. S-curves appear in Rogers (1962); Nokia's decline is documented; the smartphone transition was a contributing cause, alongside the Microsoft acquisition and the failure of Windows Phone to reach critical mass.

What the record cannot confirm: TKC's two-year stagnation, the profit-from-investments claim, the government's twenty-million-baht budget figure. These came from numbers he reviewed. The numbers are not public.

He told them their company was dying and then showed them how to save it. He gave them one month. Whether that is optimism or diagnosis depends on what they build.
