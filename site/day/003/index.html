MSG 01rural hometown summers + the case for physicality + contemporary HR-advising reflection

Let's get back to the 100 days of Non thing. I don't remember what was the question for number three. I think we are going chronologically, right? We trace back to my parents, my grandparents, and as much as I remember about my great grandparents, my sort of rural hometown, because that's where my mom came from and that's where I spent every summer. I experienced the idea of being left with my grandparents and tons of kids like me who, like me, skipped a generation and lived under one roof. During the summer, when the school was off, they didn't have anyone to take care of me at home, so they would rather send me over to the rural area where lots of kids like me would spend time with their grandparents. That was actually a wonderful childhood that I had.

I thought it was really, really great because I got to hang out with friends, I got to learn about different genders because I have so many friends who were girls, and I got to learn how to eat local food, which I have grown to really love. Thai food is my number one food in the world. Like noodle, beef noodle, beef ball noodle with ice-cold water, and the kind of iced coffee with milk and sugar, like really dark one but with really dark sugar too, it just blended in really beautifully. It's really beautiful thing that I had as a child.

With our smartphones and only one TV in the entire neighborhood, we have to hang around together around a TV to watch together. It creates a different kind of physique and physical socialities that I'm sure is different from the kids today. One of the reasons why I don't really want to have kids today is probably because I'm not convinced that I can prevent them from having to be in a world where they don't really need physicality anymore, which I think is wrong, because I think that's the same way we get a lot from forest bathing. We get a lot of benefits from it, the same way every time we socialize, we actually get to live longer, not only because there's so much research to show that sociality is the essence of longevity, because you have a meaning to live for, you're not just living day to day.

I can see how living just like paycheck to paycheck and just hoping that you die at 60 so you don't have to try to find a way to get the retirement alone. I can feel that way, because now that I'm advising this company's HR, which is a publicly listed company that has been doing ICT and telecom for many years. I just feel like most people that were just there, they probably blame their parents for giving birth to them, because they only had good time, which they thought were bad times, and they were students. We all felt the same way: we felt caged, we felt controlled, we felt we were trained to comply, and that was the time when we were supposed to be on our skateboards and fall, and then learn to hurt ourselves and then go crazy and then release all this extra energy in terms of rebelling against the system and then understand why rebels always fail because we don't understand the structure of the system yet, right? That failing, constant failing itself, I believe, would have become the strength of that person and that person's character, which would be difficult if we don't really get to do it with people anymore face to face. That's why I believe that, as much as we need forest bathing, we also need to socialize with people as well.

MSG 02the father, opening with the Japan reunion (October 2006), the ACC funding loop via Kengo Kuma, the earlier Singapore trip (already wearing the MIT T-shirt), and the Changi wrong-luggage trip

Alright, you want to know about my dad. Let me tell you. It was kind of funny that I almost forgot how he looked anymore or how he smelled. I used to be really close to him, and the moment that I remember quite well was when I crossed paths with him in Japan. It was in 2006. I just finished my first year at MIT, and I had a chance to travel to Japan. It was in October 2006.

I think my parents just visited me in Boston earlier that year, and then they went back to Bangkok. My dad got to travel to Japan for a visit to some projects that he had to learn about. At the time, he was the deputy director general of the Department of Local Administration, which is a pretty important position in the Government of Thailand in the Ministry of Interior. He just got the news that he got promoted to Governor of Buriram Province, and it was maybe just right before October, because October would be when he resumed the position.

I was quite interested that I got to travel for the first time. I got a grant to go to Japan. They didn't even care whether I would be presenting or not. I thought I would be presenting because I submitted a paper, but at the end I was just a participant. It was a workshop and pretty much a tour of modern architecture. It was a small society of people who were interested in modern architecture in Asia. There was a name for it, I don't remember the acronym.

I met a colleague, Yasushi Zeno. He was a Columbia School of Architecture graduate and was probably doing a PhD back in Japan or something. He was the only one who would tend to be quite interesting in talking to me, because most Japanese are quite introverted. I got $1,000 to travel. It's called the Avalon Travel Fellowship or something like that, so I used it to travel to Japan. My dad was there at the same time, so I had to go see him. I went to see him after I had a long night of drinking with my senior from college. He was a few years above me. He was a top student, and I was a top student too of my class. We had a night out together at an izakaya where he bought me out and dropped me off at the hotel where my dad was supposed to stay. It turns out my dad stayed at another hotel, all the way in Chiba, which meant that it was another one-hour train ride for me to go out. Eventually I got to the hotel, got to see him in his room, and hugged him first thing when I saw him. That was 20 years ago already, funny enough.

We traveled together quite a bit. He didn't eat fish, but he would go into a sushi restaurant and watch me eat tuna because I love tuna. We were hanging out quite a lot before I had to part away and join the other team to go visit some of the architectural sites and so on. I also met with the architect Kenko Kuma, who was also an alumnus of the Asian Cultural Council, from whom I also get the funding to attend MIT for my degree in history or in theory and criticism of architecture. It was quite interesting that all these things are like a complete loop that kind of comes back together. That moment that I got to spend time in Tokyo with my dad was something that I will probably not forget. In addition to that, before that, I think a few years before that, we also went to Singapore together, and I was already wearing an MIT T-shirt without knowing that I would end up there. I got that T-shirt a long time ago.

There was another kind of a lonely duet trip with my dad, whereby the first thing we did was to pick up the wrong luggage, so we had to go back to Changhi Airport to pick up the right one. We didn't know the other guy who didn't get the luggage. What the hell did he do? Did he report or not? There was kind of funny, and that was a two-trip, but then I can go back a little bit more.

MSG 03the train trip to Malaysia that started it all, the "no wasted time" philosophy, the Dr. Poon $20K spot payment, the five family cars, dad teaching Non to drive in Sa Kaeo (1999), the KMITL thesis humiliation, the AFS year in Oklahoma City, the academic arc through MIT / Harvard / Oxford

Maybe I should go back a little bit more and talk about the trip that started it all: my interest in travelling and my interest in travelling by train in particular. I'm not sure how old I was, maybe eleven, twelve, or something like that.

The first trip we had away from home was to Malaysia, and we travelled in style, which meant that we travelled in a very old train. At least it was a sleeper. My dad arranged it and organised it, and we were able to get a sleeper to travel from Hualampong station in Bangkok all the way down to Padang Besar and then, of course, all the way to Malaysia and so on. I don't remember much. Did we make it to Kuala Lumpur? I don't remember. We definitely made it to Padang Besar, a lost star. I don't remember this name. We did spend a day or two, I guess, in Haad Yai, where I fell in love with Santia Suk Market because it had so many electronics. My dad should have known from that time that I was interested in electronics, but of course he didn't pick that up, so I ended up becoming an architect.

I spent, wasted, so many years of my life in the field that I could have had avoided and then gone right into anthropology or sociology or psychology. But then again I couldn't complain, because being an architect was always my dream. Had I not spent so many years wasting my time, I would not have learned that it was kind of wasteful, but my philosophy, the other philosophy that you should wire into your hard core system, is that I don't believe in time wasting. I believe that everything that I do, even if I have to walk three miles in the heat, I get vitamin D, right? I get exercise, I get to test, test, test, test out my tolerance. I get to do all kinds of things, or when I do these systems, I don't get paid now, but when I show it to people who might be interested and want to buy it, then the whole thing pays off, right?

When I was doing the system for fun and when my friend Dr. Poon from the Ministry of Higher Education, Research Science and Technology saw me and saw the dashboard that they built for fun, he said he has to pay. He has how much did he give me? Like $20,000. Yeah, he was like on the spot, just gave it to me. It's like, yeah, you go and buy your supercomputer and then build something more fun for me, and he only gave me a few bullet points of what he wanted to bring. I could get that all done. He gave me a year timeline. I did it for him in 45 minutes, so that was the kind of person I am today.

What I do is that I don't believe that there's any wasted time. Even when I was at MIT, I was alone a lot, but I learned a lot myself. When I was in college, I was alone a lot, but I spent lots of time in the library. That's why I was ahead of everybody in terms of knowledge, and that's how I got to become a professor right away after I graduated, which is really, really not common. Most of the time, people have to spend a year outside of university before they can come back in as a university lecturer, but I didn't have that transition. I moved in right away, so in a sense I was pretty special, and once I realized that I was pretty special, it was not easy to go back.

What happened was that we talked about train. We went to Malaysia, I think, and then I love train. I love the slowness, I love the hanging out when the train was moving and having fun talking to people, sleeping together when the train is moving, when we are actually going somewhere, going to a destination and not just staying there. We had three cars:
1. The first one was a small Toyota in yellow.
2. The second one was Toyota Corona.
3. The third one was Corona Altus, which my mother got from the bank when she retired. She bought it from the bank at a discounted price, and then we sold that and then we got the Ford Fiesta.

Oh yeah, we got the fourth one, and now I'm actually driving the fifth one, which is a BYD Adderall 3 EV. So my dad was the one who taught me how to drive a car, and it was our second car already, the Corona one. By that time, my dad already got his car that came with his title. It was not a good car, but when he was a vice governor, the car was a bit better. When he was the deputy director general, the car was really nice. It was Nissan, I think it was really big. Consumed lots of gasoline, though, but well, you know, he deserved it. He deserves a good car.

At the time, we had one car that didn't get used so much, which is the Corona that used to be our main car, so I asked my dad, and he was reluctant until he eventually let me drive it. Then I had to go to Satga Province, where he was a vice governor for quite a while, to learn how to drive a car from a teacher who was actually quite good. He got us on a pickup truck, and then we just drove around the city. We took turns. There were four of us in the same car. They were his students at the same time, and we just learned how to drive a car. It's so much fun when we took turns. The other two of us were just sitting in the back of the truck, like no seatbelts, nothing. It was just like a rolling 90s.

I think the year was 1999 when I was learning that. When my dad let me drive a car, it was all the way to, I think, my fourth year of college, when I was in my fifth year, in fact. I have to do my pre-thesis, whereby I have to transport myself here and there, doing interviews with national artists, because my thesis eventually would be about National Artists Hall of Fame. I embarrassingly only got a B from the thesis committee, even though I was the best student ever of the faculty and even though they knew that I did a lot of work in terms of their research. Even though the model didn't look so nice or the plates didn't look as great as they wanted, even though I put my heart and soul into the research because I thought that this was about the research, it turns out they didn't like it. They gave me a B, and most people got an A on that thesis. Lots of people made fun of my work to the point that I had to go and take my models back before I got destroyed by people whom I just wanted to step on it. I had a bad time in college, but my dad was by my side supporting me. He told me I didn't have to worry so much, because at the college that we went to, KMITL, between 1998 and 2004, I had a year of dropping out because I decided that I wasn't good enough that year. I decided that I wanted to go enjoy the American Fulbright service program and travel to the US for a year, and my dad supported me on that too. Even though at that time the Thai currency was really weak against the US, they pulled together their money and sent me to the US. That's where I met Victor Wong, with whom I spent a year in Oklahoma City. He was my host family, and he went to Dartmouth himself. He took me to Boston to visit his cousins and family friends, because he's a Cantonese family. They're all very smart, and that's how many of his cousins ended up in Boston, just like in the movie Love Story. Really, in the 90s, everything was so slow. Nobody had a phone, and they talked to each other in a bar. We discussed stuff, and it was really a romantic moment that kind of got me to fall in love with Boston.

Little did I know, back in 1999, that I would be going back there for 10 years as a Fulbright first master's student at MIT, visiting lecturer at MIT, and then six years of PhD at Harvard, including teaching Thai at Harvard for two years, and all kinds of things that happened. I still hold a visa to the US, and my dad has always been supportive of me in doing all these. My mom was a little bit less. She wanted me to work, maybe because she worked before she studied. Anyway, I ended up going this academic route all the way through Oxford and Harvard PhD, to the point that I don't think anyone could actually beat me academically. My dad has always been behind me every step because he loved academic stuff.

At the end, I wrote a really interesting thesis about how I like to argue that people like to think that skyscrapers are symbols of economic progress. That's why people have to go up, like in Carol Willis or a lot of people's works that argue that when lands are expensive, things tend to go vertical. In China, like Pudong where I studied, or back in the 1920s, the Bund, for instance, these were high-rises that were built even before the need to go up economically occurred. High-rises have some symbolic power, psychological power in fact. I was looking at this through the lens of culture and how architecture and culture intertwine. I was trying to be as clear as possible because good writing for Stan is clear writing. That's why I became interested in clear writing, even though I got swung a little bit by Michael Hirsfield, who later on became my advisor at Harvard. It's really good for me to get to talk to all these things, because it seems like, in my life, after my dad died, or even before he died, but maybe more so after my dad died, and I wouldn't know this until I talk this out, like I'm doing right now, is the fact that I'm always looking for a father figure.
