A 100-day biographical installation
WHERE MEMORY MEETS RECORD
Non Arkara, born November 1, 1981, in Bangkok. Forty-two years in the same suburban house while the city grew around it. Trained in architecture at the edge of infrastructure. Studied the mind at MIT. Studied the city at Oxford and Harvard. Spent two years in Shanghai's Yangpu District watching a former industrial zone become smart. Now: the subject of a 100-day biographical installation that surfaces what happens when an unreliable narrator meets an unreliable recording system. The installation is not about truth. It is about the friction between memory and record, between the story we tell and the traces we leave.

Geography of Memory

1981–1990
Suburban Bangkok. Nam Sai, the alleyway neighborhood. Three candies for a baht. A compound where his parents and aunts and uncles all built houses on land the maternal grandmother bought before anyone needed it. His father built their house himself. His mother worked nights at Thammasat to support her siblings.
FIG. 1 — Suburban Bangkok (Circa 1980s)
The accidental archive
Siam Intercontinental across the district—a hotel with a hat-shaped roof, Frank Lloyd Wright Prairie style. When it demolished in 2002, the land became what he described: an elephant-headed bridge, Sra Pathum Palace, beer gardens, the science center, three cinema houses. The city's infrastructure laid out in his childhood geography.
Television as portal
Shanghai Bund on Channel 3, dubbed Cantonese into Thai, ages 5–10. Twenty years later: a PhD dissertation on Shanghai. The childhood TV show becomes the spine of the academic career. The through-line drawn.

Shanghai & The Field

2009–2011: Yangpu
Two years in Shanghai's Yangpu District. Fieldwork for the dissertation. Watching a rust-belt industrial zone become the template for China's smart city agenda. Textile mills converted to tech parks. Workers' housing demolished. The mythology of progress enacted in real time.
From monument to method
The dissertation: how smart city rhetoric obscures material history. How the narrative of progress erases the narrative of labor. How technology is weaponized as story. The Yangpu data becomes the argument.
The childhood echo
Shanghai Bund on Channel 3 meets Shanghai Bund on foot. The TV show he watched in Bangkok becomes the city he studies. The through-line visible.
2012–Present
From fieldwork to practice. Smart cities consultant. Speaker at tech conferences. The dissertation's critique enters the room where smart city strategies are written. The insider who knows how the magic trick works.
Day 1: Where the unreliable narrator met the unreliable recorder
✗ CONTRADICTED
Non said he was born "near Siam Paragon" in Bangkok.
Siam Paragon didn't exist until 2005. The building standing there in 1981 was the Siam Intercontinental Hotel. Non's memory grabbed the nearest modern landmark. When shown the gap, he didn't defend the error—he gave the geography back: the Prairie-style hotel, its demolition, the infrastructure it anchored. His initial reference was a grammar error, not a memory error. The system was the unreliable narrator.
✗ CONTRADICTED
The transcription reads "Paris style of Frank Lloyd Wright."
Non said "Prairie style." The speech-to-text system heard French. The system, again, was the unreliable narrator. He corrected it the next day: BE 2512 (Thai Buddhist Era) = CE 1969, not 1953. On the only hard-record question the project has tested, Non was right. The machine was wrong. The contradiction pointed not to his memory but to the infrastructure doing the recording.
∞ UNRESOLVED
Non: "I have nothing to forgive. They cared for me. I was born well." / "I cannot forgive them for not realizing I was a genius."
Both statements sit in the same message, untouched by each other. Neither resolves into the other. This is the texture of the project. Both are true at once. The system logs them both. Day 76 will ask the question again and we will see if the contradiction hardens or softens or remains alive.
Emerging patterns: What the first day showed

Facts Verified

9
Claims checked

When Non gives dates, places, names, and infrastructure references, the record holds. Phyathai Hospital exists. Sra Pathum Palace is where he said. Pantip Plaza, the science center, the beer gardens—all confirmed. His family line traces through documented institutions: Thammasat, Mahidol, Khon Kaen University. The chain of migration through Sino-Thai commercial networks matches the academic literature. When pressed on specifics, his answers deepen rather than crack.
