THE LINEAGE ENCODED
Phase 1  /  Origin & Place
Question

Your mother.
Full name.
First.

Birth year, city, occupation when you were five. Then: what is the one story she tells about you most often? Not the proudest one. The one she actually tells.

Answer — Non Arkara

I never have a problem with not having enough love for my parents. I can always find my mom and my dad. I look up to them, of course. I look up to them too much that when my dad passed away, I didn't think I had a role model left to look up to anymore. That might explain how I became so much of myself as I am today.

For my mom, she was a strong person in the family because she was the breadwinner. She worked in the bank and in many banks, actually. She started off because she got an honor degree from the Faculty of Economics, which at the time was a really prestigious school that educated many famous bankers and economists and so on. She worked for Krung Thai Bank, which is pretty much the bank that the government set up in order to do all the government businesses. I get paid through that bank.

For the past seven years I worked for the Thai government, and she worked there for a few years until the money ran out — two kids, and she couldn't wait until she got promoted. In a state-owned bank, the salary usually doesn't rise really fast. Then the roaring 90s came in, and she had to move to a bank called the Bank of Asia. The famous Robot Building was the iconic headquarters, which is today still there, though changed in terms of functionality. Many banks got sold, and the Bank of Asia was sold to ABN AMRO and UOB, and it’s no longer a Thai bank.

Before that, my mom moved to the Thai Military Bank, which later on was renamed TMB because it wanted to erase the image of being a bank for the military. She had a really stable career and desired to early retire when my dad became governor of Buriram province first, and then he got moved to Nakhon Ratchasima, and then he was moved back to the ministry for his final year to work as Inspector General.

My dad's name on the birth certificate was actually a romanization of his Chinese name: Kimpo Saehaeng. As we know, Sae means last name — it’s the word xing in Chinese, but it got put in there because the person in charge of writing down the names thought the word Sae was always part of the last name. It’s actually not.

Kimpo Saehaeng became Kongkiet Arkaraprasertkul. He had to have a Thai name in order to go to Thai school. I believe that the Teochew dialect of Chinese must have been his first language, because when I was old enough to remember my paternal grandparents, they still spoke Thai with a really thick Chinese accent. That’s how my family grew up — and they happened to grow up in the northeastern part of Thailand. That’s maybe how my dad and my mom connected so easily when they were fellow students at the Faculty of Economics at Thammasat University, even though my dad switched and got his degree instead in Political Science. That’s how he later on showed us the path to become a governor, and he got to become one before he retired and passed away.

That was a good career for a young boy who did not have a good family name. I mean, he came up with a family name himself. My current family name. He invented it. And then I carry it.

But the childhood itself was in the lanes. I played in the lanes. I had fun in the lanes. I played with dogs in the lanes. My parents were really busy. I grew up with my nanny Boun Som. She was really strict, and I always remember her never smiling at me, but I think she loved me a lot.

We had to take her to the nursing home, and my dad had some connections to put her in a public nursing home and supported her once in a while until she could no longer speak or live by herself. We had to transport her to the hospital, and then she died alone.

Ever since I was born, I think from day one, all the way to when maybe I was in high school, I lived with this lady who actually taught me a lot of things. Primary school education. She taught me to be strict, kind of, and she’s really good at cooking. She was very conservative and traditional. When she died — 2002, I think — my dad and I were the only ones at her funeral. Her kids were not there at all. Her friends were not there. She died alone, but we love her. I love her. She was my second mom, really.

Every time we had a chance as a family, we would go eat together. I remember the small hot pot restaurant, which in Thailand we call sukiyaki, even though they were pretty much the Teochew style rather — flavored meatballs, flavored meats, fish. Really lovely. My mom was also teaching at the University of the Thai Chamber of Commerce. Every time she got extra money from teaching, she would take us to eat at a restaurant. Otherwise we ate at home. I love eating with my parents. I love sitting right there with them, sharing with them about my days, and I believe they love it too, because back then there was no TV, no phones, nothing — only one phone in the house, and nobody was allowed to use the TV during dinner time. We were just chatting, talking about food.

My maternal grandfather — agung — was the strongest guy in the family. Could drive and smoke cigarettes, very heavy lifting, could climb trees, could do all kinds of fabric and garment work using sewing machines, because he ran the shop in our rural hometown. Just one day, something happened. He could no longer swallow food by himself. He became weak, and then he lost his will to live and just didn’t want to live anymore.

A couple of years later, my maternal grandmother also passed away, and then my dad, and then my paternal grandmother, and then my paternal grandfather in 2020 during COVID.

I’m not done yet — I can write down her name for you. When I grew up, I actually call myself Non, and then between my first and my last name, I put a letter C. That’s a tribute to my mom’s family name, Jamsuan, which I also believe that I’m a part of their family as much as them — the Heng or the Wang or the dad’s family side of things.

Nobody in my family was special in any way. They were very traditional. It was pure luck that my dad got to where he was without sacrificing anything. When I was taking university exams, he was driving me around to all these schools, to all these prep schools for extra classes in physics, English, mathematics. He would drive me around and then he would feed me. He was back in Bangkok to run a center that was focused on IT. At the time, in the 90s, IT was something really wacky. Because he was in a high position enough to run the center, even though he didn’t have any IT background — but nobody had an IT background anyway — I got access to a computer for the first time.

My mom, from her side, was given a really classic IBM laptop with a rollerball in the middle. I got to play with it by plugging it into my telephone’s cords, dialing up with a 14.4 kilobytes per second US Robotics modem and then trying to get access to the Internet back in the 90s. I was really curious about this technology. I could have gone into coding. I could have gone into computer sciences, and I would have been a millionaire, right. But that didn’t happen.

I know I got all these amazing degrees from top universities without paying a penny. We did pay a little bit, which is very little — around 3 million baht — when my dad had to pay to cut me off from the university ties because I took some money from them to do research in Shanghai, which became the foundation of my PhD studies at Harvard later on. It was an investment worth spending, but the sad part was that I didn’t know that my dad would leave us so early. Otherwise I would have spent all those years not paying the 3 million baht and just paid by work for the university for four more years. By that time I would be ready to go back to school again, and I would get to spend those four years of my life with my dad before he passed away.

What happened was that I was traveling, going here and there teaching, visiting lecturer at MIT, traveling to China to take part in a Beijing studio, living a life like a Casanova, a kind of Don Juan academic prince who just didn’t care, even though he’s very stingy as well. He realized later on, as of this year, that he’s actually quite wealthy, he’s healthy, and he’s happy.

I think what my mom told other people the most about me was the fact that she never saw me studying, which, back then, when I was young, was concerning. It’s like this guy would never be good at studying, but little did she know that I did not study because I already understood everything.

So yeah, you could say I made it. I made it to MIT, Oxford, Harvard. I had a great job. I had a great life. I’m healthy (knock on wood). I’m happy in general. I’m inventing. I’m being transported all around the world to big avenues to give grand lectures. I have a dignified life alongside the freedom to do whatever I want with my jobs and beyond.

I kind of made it to the point where I think if I died today, I would have said I made it already, because I don’t think I can be happier than this, really. If I had better food, I don’t think I like food that much anyway. I just don’t want anything anymore, and I think that is the piece of my life that sometimes, even if you have a trillion dollars, you still don’t have it. That’s why I believe that we have to look at happiness from a personal perspective, because everybody would have a different way of looking at that.

It could have been better, as in it could have been smoother, it could have been less suffering, less painful, less traumatic. It would not have built me to be this guy, but it would have been less painful.

Narration — Biographer

Day two was supposed to be about his mother. It turned into a double portrait — both parents, the nanny, both grandfathers, the maternal family name, the price of leaving the Thai university system, and the closing line: I made it.

His mother was the breadwinner. Honours degree from Thammasat Economics in the era when that faculty was a pipeline into Thailand's central banking class. Krung Thai for a few years, then Bank of Asia in the 1990s — the bank whose Robot Building by Sumet Jumsai is one of postmodern Asian architecture's most photographed objects — then Thai Military Bank, which renamed itself TMB to shed its military origin and is now part of ttb. Adjunct teaching at the University of the Thai Chamber of Commerce on the side. The extra teaching money is what bought the family their restaurant meals; the rest of the week was at the dinner table at home.

His father was the harder name to carry. Born Kimpo Saehaeng — a Sino-Thai registration name in which the registrar misread 姓 (the Chinese marker for “the surname”) as part of the surname itself. To enter the Thai school system the family invented a fully Thai name: Kongkiet Arkaraprasertkul. That invented name is the one Non himself carries. The whole family identity is a one-generation construction by a Sino-Thai boy who needed a Thai-sounding signature to sit for school exams. From there Kongkiet went to Thammasat — first Economics, then switching to Political Science — and out into the Ministry of Interior’s provincial-governor pipeline. Buriram first. Then Nakhon Ratchasima. Then a final year back at the ministry as Inspector General. Then dead. Along the way, in the 1990s, a Bangkok IT center he ran without an IT background — nobody had an IT background anyway — which gave Non his first computer.

Non said the death of his father dissolved the role-model layer that everyone else’s life maintains as scaffolding. I didn’t think I had a role model left to look up to anymore. That might explain how I became so much of myself as I am today.

The childhood was in the lanes. Dogs, friends, motorbikes without helmets. The nanny Boun Som lived in the house from his birth through his high-school years. Strict, never smiled, good at cooking. When she could no longer care for herself, his father’s government connections placed her in a public nursing home. She died alone in the hospital. At her 2002 funeral, only two mourners came: his father, and him.

The maternal grandfather — agung — was the strong one. Played badminton, drove, smoked, did heavy lifting, climbed trees. He ran ร้านสุวรรณพานิช, the Suwan Panit shop, in Sikhio — the man whose hands worked the sewing machines that produced the ready-made clothing on the 1969 registration certificate in the Day 1 archive. The artifact has a human now. Non says he went from the strongest in the family to unable to swallow food, lost his will to live, and went.

Then Message 3 gave the maternal family name: Jamsuan. And the explanation of why Non writes his own name as Non C. Arkaraprasertkul — the C is a tribute to Jamsuan, the maternal line. A single letter in his self-styled name is the maternal coat-of-arms. The whole surname Arkaraprasertkul is the paternal coat-of-arms. The name he signs at the bottom of letters is bilineal.

Then the cost line. Three million baht — what his father paid to release him from a Thai university funding bond after the Thai university had backed his early Shanghai research that would later become the foundation of his Harvard PhD. It was an investment worth spending, he says — and then adds the regret: the sad part was that I didn’t know my dad would leave us so early. Otherwise I would have spent all those years not paying the 3 million baht and just paid by work for the university for four more years. By that time I would be ready to go back to school again, and I would get to spend those four years of my life with my dad before he passed away. Money and time, in the same paragraph, with the trade revealed in retrospect.

Message 4 closed: I made it. MIT, Oxford, Harvard. Invited grand lectures around the world. A dignified life. Freedom in the work. The arrival framed in the past tense — if I died today, I would have said I made it already. The wanting has stopped. And then the third position on the parents/upbringing question that Day 1 already had two positions on: it could have been less painful. It would not have built me to be this guy, but it would have been less painful.

Three positions across two days: nothing to forgive (Day 1), cannot forgive them for not recognizing my genius (Day 1), less painful would have been better but would not have made me me (Day 2). The Day 76 question on Family & responsibility now has three positions to triangulate against, not two.
