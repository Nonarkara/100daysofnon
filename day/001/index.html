NEAR SIAM PARAGON
Phase 1  /  Origin & Place
Question

Born 1981. That's the claim. We start there. Give me: day, month, year. Hospital. City. Who was in the room. The version your family tells of that day — verbatim if you remember the exact words, paraphrased if not. And one detail from that version that you've never quite believed.

Answer — Non Arkara

So it was November 1st, 1981, a Sunday. The hospital was Payatai in the city, close to where we lived at the time, which was near Siam Square. We moved out when I was probably a couple of years old to this current house I've been living in for the past 42 years.

It was on a Sunday that I was born. The pictures of the baby look kind of strange and didn't look like me at all. I feel like I'm definitely much more handsome than that baby. Strange head shape. I didn't remember much about anything when I was young. The only time that I remember was probably when I got lost in a mall and no one actually found me for a while. That got me worried and may have become part of my psychological scar until now.

I was born six years after my brother. That's all I got from my mom and from people around me.

Well, first of all, that wouldn't be Siam Paragon back then. It would have been this hotel called Siam Intercontinental. The hotel itself was designed to look like a hat, because the architect himself was actually a direct student of Frank Lloyd Wright. Surrounded by a park. A small portion of it is hotel rooms, and that's actually where my mom usually goes to get her dry cleaning. They hosted a number of events I took part in, especially weddings. I think the last event that I went there before they tore down the buildings and built this gigantic structure called Siam Paragon was probably an international student affair expo for Australia. Around 2002 or so. Only a few months after that they decided to tear down the buildings.

Back then, Siam Square was calm. Not as hipsterish as the 90s, not as Gen Z as today. Lots of interesting stores, comic book stores, three big theatres. My mom mentioned we would cross this elephant-headed bridge over a small canal, over to the area of the Princess Palace — a beer garden, then the science center, then Siam Square. My mother also worked down the road at the headquarters of Krung Thai Bank on Sukhumvit. She could commute by bus from there to the row house.

My maternal grandmother is the one who was more visionary. She saved every penny in order to buy property in the city center, which later on was taken by eminent domain for an expressway landing. We had to move out. That's how we got the money to build this house — the land was already bought by the visionary grandmother, who understood what it takes to have a piece of land in the capital.

The mall was called Pantip Plaza. My mom would go there with me in the baby carrier, my dad and brother too sometimes. I got lost for a while and then was found by my mom and brother. I still remember that moment. I still remember the interior — very Roman-esque classical, with lots of columns. I later on, as an architecture student, learned these were called Doric and Ionic columns. Everything was moving around me. Everything was so big, so frightening. The acoustics were not fun. I remember that as well.
